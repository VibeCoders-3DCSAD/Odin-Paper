## TL;DR

Freelancers in Metro Manila face persistent income volatility and lack of traditional benefits; budgeting and saving strategies are common but insufficient for long-term financial security.

## Problem and Motivation

Research on the gig economy has focused on platform growth and labor conditions, leaving a gap in understanding freelancers’ specific financial behaviors and coping strategies. Financial instability among gig workers threatens their well-being and the sustainability of freelance work as a primary income source. No prior study had systematically explored how freelancers manage income volatility, access financial services, or plan for long-term security in the Philippine context.

## Approach

- Qualitative study using semi-structured interviews with 50 freelancers in Metro Manila.
- Participants had at least six months of experience in fields such as writing, graphic design, virtual assistance, and software development.
- Purposive and snowball sampling to capture diverse perspectives.
- Thematic analysis of interview transcripts using NVivo software.
- Interview guide covered income volatility, access to financial services, budgeting practices, saving habits, and perceived opportunities.

## Findings

- **Income instability—driven by seasonal fluctuations, client cancellations, delayed payments, and short-term projects—is the most pervasive financial challenge.**
- Freelancers lack traditional benefits (health insurance, retirement plans, paid leave); those who obtain coverage pay higher premiums or rely on spouses.
- Banks view freelancers as high-risk borrowers, making loans and credit difficult to access; many rely on cash, budgeting, and alternative tools instead.
- Financial uncertainty causes anxiety, stress, delayed life decisions (e.g., moving, travel), and strained personal relationships.
- Common budgeting practices include expense consciousness, structured allocation (e.g., 30% savings, 70% expenses), manual tracking via spreadsheets, and weekly budgeting to accommodate irregular income.
- Digital tools (budgeting apps, digital wallets, tax software) help with day-to-day management but cannot solve income irregularity or delayed payments.
- Freelancers express need for income-smoothing tools, affordable health insurance, government safety nets, and financial education tailored to gig work.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Gig economy | A labor market characterized by short-term, task-based, or freelance work arranged through digital platforms. |
| Income volatility | Fluctuations in earnings from month to month or season to season, making financial planning difficult. |
| Freelancer | A self-employed individual who takes on projects or tasks without a long-term employment contract. |
| Portable benefits | Social protections (health insurance, retirement, paid leave) that workers can carry across multiple jobs or platforms. |
| Micro-savings | A strategy of saving small amounts of money regularly using digital tools or apps. |
| Income-smoothing | Financial products or strategies that help workers balance high-income and low-income periods. |

**Limits of relevance:**

- Qualitative study with N=50; findings are exploratory and not statistically generalizable to all Filipino young professionals.
- Focuses on freelancers broadly, not specifically on the 20–40 age bracket or on users of personal budget management systems.
- Does not evaluate any algorithmic or ML-based financial tool; relevance is behavioral and contextual, not technical.
- No longitudinal data; coping strategies and outcomes were self-reported at a single point in time.

## Limitations

- Small sample size (N=50) and qualitative design limit statistical generalizability.
- Participants were recruited via purposive and snowball sampling, which may introduce selection bias (e.g., overrepresentation of digitally savvy freelancers). [unacknowledged]
- No comparison group (e.g., traditional employees) to isolate gig-specific vs. general financial challenges.
- Self-reported data may suffer from recall bias and social desirability bias (e.g., overreporting budgeting discipline).
- The study does not differentiate between freelancers who rely on gig work as primary income vs. supplemental income, which may affect financial behavior patterns.

## Remember This

- 🔑 Income volatility is universal—seasonal dips and client cancellations make planning impossible for all freelancers interviewed.
- 📌 30% savings / 70% expenses is a common rule, but it fails when income drops unpredictably.
- 💡 Manual tracking (Google Sheets) and digital wallets are widely used, yet they don’t solve late payments or irregular cash flow.
- 😰 Financial stress delays major life decisions—moving, travel, even professional training—creating a cycle of instability.
