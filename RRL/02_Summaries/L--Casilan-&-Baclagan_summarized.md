tldr: Parents from lower middle‑income Filipino families view children helping out as a non‑obligatory, autonomous act that should arise from the child’s own volition, not from parental pressure.
problem_and_motivation: The practice of children helping parents is common in Filipino families, but most research focuses on adult children’s obligations or youth perspectives. Parents’ own expectations, especially among lower middle‑income families, remain underexplored despite their potential to reshape understanding of filial responsibility and tagasalo personality.
approach:
  - Exploratory qualitative study using semi‑structured interviews with 10 parents from lower middle‑income families (monthly income ₱21,194–₱43,828) in NCR, Region III, and Region IV‑A.
  - Participants had two or more children living at home who were not yet working; purposive sampling ensured all inclusion criteria.
  - Interviews were conducted online via Google Meet and Facebook Messenger, recorded, transcribed, and translated from Filipino to English.
  - Data were analyzed using thematic analysis with open, axial, and selective coding in Google Spreadsheets.
  - The study did not include other socioeconomic classes, single‑parent households, or extended‑family heads, and did not compare gender differences.
findings:
  - Helping out is viewed by parents as a non‑obligatory practice; children have no duty to help, and any assistance must come from autonomous decision (kusang loob).
  - Parents value helping out as an admirable, cultural, and generational practice but reject toxic forms that involve pressure or manipulation.
  - Key reasons children help: poverty, sensitivity to family struggles, utang na loob (debt of gratitude), and aging parents.
  - Parental support provided includes educational, caregiving, financial, guidance, spiritual, and emotional availability.
  - Children are expected to help in areas such as household chores, financial contributions, home upgrades, emotional support, sibling care, and eventually parents’ health needs.
  - A capable child is described as studious, financially capable (degree, job, no own family), determined, generous, kind (kabutihang loob), mature, and honest; birth order is not decisive though eldest may help first.
