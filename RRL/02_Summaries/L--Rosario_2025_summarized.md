## TL;DR

Indigenous households in Bontoc blend strict budgeting and kinship-based reciprocity (og-ogfo, bayanihan, paluwagan) to manage income insufficiency, prioritizing cultural obligations over individual wealth accumulation.

## Problem and Motivation

Most financial studies on Filipino households focus on budgeting and credit but ignore how indigenous culture directly governs financial decisions through practices like supon, og-ogfo, and ritual obligations. Without understanding these culturally embedded systems, poverty reduction policies risk eroding community-based safety nets that sustain survival. Prior work failed to integrate indigenous reciprocity mechanisms into financial management frameworks, leaving a gap in culturally grounded financial literacy models.

## Approach

- Qualitative descriptive phenomenological design (Creswell, 2013) to capture lived financial experiences.
- 12 participants purposively sampled from average-earning Indigenous households in Bontoc, Mountain Province.
- Criteria: Indigenous residents for ≥5 years, aged ≥25, active financial decision-makers, lower to lower-middle income (PSA definition).
- Data collection: semi-structured interviews (20–30 min each) and one focus group discussion (FGD).
- Interviews conducted in Ilokano and Bontok languages, audio-recorded, transcribed verbatim.
- Thematic analysis following Colaizzi’s phenomenological method with member checking.
- Theoretical framework: Social Capital Theory (Putnam), Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner), Behavioral Economics (Kahneman & Tversky).

## Findings

- Households practice **strict budgeting and expense prioritization** (food, electricity, education), but cultural obligations (rituals, kinship support) often override personal savings goals.
- **Og-ogfo (mutual labor/aid) and bayanihan** function as informal insurance systems, reducing reliance on formal credit during emergencies.
- Savings are thin and irregular (micro-savings of **₱50–₱100** daily via coin jars or paluwagan), not separate emergency funds.
- Income insufficiency and rising costs (LPG, transport, electricity) force adaptive coping: sideline work, bill deferrals, in-kind contributions (livestock, rice, preserved meat).
- Health and education expenses repeatedly disrupt budgets despite PhilHealth coverage; families turn first to kinship networks, not loans.
- Formal financial mechanisms (banks, cooperatives) face access barriers (distance, paperwork); paluwagan remains preferred for trust and simplicity.
- Cultural values of thrift, charity, and collective responsibility guide financial decisions; financial resilience is measured by ability to fulfill communal duties, not by wealth.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Og-ogfo / Og-ogbu | Communal labor and collective aid system; neighbors provide work or resources (rice, livestock) without cash exchange. |
| Bayanihan | Traditional Filipino community spirit of mutual help, often in-kind or labor-based. |
| Paluwagan | Rotating savings group where members contribute fixed amounts periodically and take turns receiving the lump sum. |
| Supon | Monetary support given during rituals, weddings, wakes, or gatherings as symbolic investment in future solidarity. |
| Mangmang / Pasup-ok | Ritual practices involving butchering or offerings to affirm transactions or guard against misfortune. |
| FGD | Focus group discussion — a qualitative method where a small group discusses a topic guided by a facilitator. |
| PSA | Philippine Statistics Authority — government agency that defines income classifications and poverty thresholds. |
| ROSCA | Rotating Savings and Credit Association — formal term for systems like paluwagan. |

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper directly informs Odin's user profiling (F.1) and expense categorization (G.1) by documenting how cultural obligations (og-ogfo, ritual contributions, kinship aid) override individual financial goals in Filipino indigenous contexts — a pattern that may generalize to Metro Manila young professionals with strong family ties. The finding that households prioritize "protected categories" (education, health, ritual expenses) even under scarcity provides empirical grounding for Odin's budget recommendation module to treat certain expenses as non-negotiable. Additionally, the documented reliance on informal reciprocity (paluwagan, og-ogfo) rather than formal credit suggests that Odin's anomaly detection should flag sudden drops in discretionary spending as potential cultural obligations (e.g., a wedding contribution) instead of overspending.

**Directly justifies:**

- "Health and education expenses are treated as non-discretionary priorities, but out-of-pocket costs still destabilize budgets even with PhilHealth coverage."

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is Indigenous rural households in Mountain Province, not young professionals in Metro Manila; urban financial pressures (rent, transport, digital services) differ significantly.
- No digital finance or mobile app usage documented; findings reflect cash-based, informal systems that may not translate to Odin's manual input interface.
- Qualitative design with N=12; findings are not statistically generalizable but provide behavioral patterns usable for hypothesis generation.
- Cultural practices (og-ogfo, supon) are specific to Cordillera Indigenous groups; Metro Manila young professionals may have weaker or different kinship obligations.

## Limitations

- Small qualitative sample (N=12) limits transferability to other Indigenous groups or urban populations.
- No longitudinal data; findings capture cross-sectional practices, not how financial strategies evolve over time. [unacknowledged]
