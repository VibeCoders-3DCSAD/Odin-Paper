## TL;DR

Filipino migrant domestic workers enrol in financial literacy courses to shift from a family-first breadwinner mindset towards self-responsibility and personal savings for retirement.

## Problem and Motivation

Ageing Filipino migrant domestic workers face precarious futures with limited state pensions and uncertain kinship care after decades of sending remittances home. Most have little personal savings because the breadwinner mentality prioritises family demands over self-preservation. No prior research had examined how financial education programmes reshape migrant women’s aspirations for later-life security in Asian migration hubs.

## Approach

- Ethnographic research in Singapore and Hong Kong (2018–2022) with Filipino domestic workers aged 45–65 who have worked abroad for 15–35 years.
- Over 50 in-depth interviews plus observations in financial literacy classes run by NGOs, corporations (KPMG), co-operatives, and domestic worker-led collectives.
- Courses cover saving, budgeting, avoiding debt, investing in stocks/insurance/real estate, and saying “no” to family money requests.
- Comparison with non-participants including activists (state accountability focus) and those relying on land ownership, faith, or informal lending groups.

## Findings

1. Only **5%** of overseas Filipinos in Singapore pay into the Philippine Social Security System (SSS), and payouts are insufficient for retirement.

- Financial courses transform self-narratives from “breadwinner” to “financially-smart investor”, emphasising self-responsibility for future security.
- Participants value courses for building confidence, purpose, and peer support networks, not just financial skills.
- Employers sometimes fund course fees, creating new dependencies alongside aspirations for independence.
- Alternative strategies include land purchase, village savings groups (*paluwagan*), faith-based resignation, or political advocacy for state pensions.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper informs Odin’s understanding of Filipino financial behavior by documenting how deeply ingrained family obligations override personal savings goals — a key constraint that budget recommendation UI must address. The finding that financial literacy courses teach migrants to set boundaries on family money requests directly supports Odin’s design principle of helping users prioritise personal savings without guilt. Although the study focuses on older migrant workers, the “breadwinner” mindset and cultural obligations to kin are equally relevant to Filipino young professionals who support parents or siblings.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Only 5% of overseas Filipinos in Singapore contribute to the state pension system, leaving most without formal retirement savings.”
- “Filipino migrant workers describe a ‘breadwinner mentality’ — the belief that all earnings must be sent to family — which leads to decades of work without accumulating personal savings.”
- “Financial literacy courses explicitly teach migrants how to say ‘no’ to family money requests, reframing self-savings as legitimate rather than selfish.”
- “Migrant women who shift to a ‘financially-smart’ identity report greater confidence in refusing family demands and investing in their own future.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is ageing migrant domestic workers (45–65), not young professionals (20–40) — but the cultural tension between family obligations and self-savings likely generalises.
- Qualitative ethnography provides behavioral insights, not algorithmic benchmarks or quantitative performance metrics for budgeting systems.
- Context is Singapore/Hong Kong labour migration with no permanent residency; Odin users are local Filipinos with different employment structures.
- Focus is on retirement social protection, not day-to-day personal budget management within a mobile app.

## Limitations

- Small qualitative sample (50+ interviews) limits generalisability to all Filipino young professionals.
- No quantitative comparison of financial literacy intervention effectiveness; relies on self-reported narratives. [unacknowledged]
- Single-country focus on Philippines, but findings may not apply to other Southeast Asian nationalities mentioned (Indonesian, Sri Lankan).
- Does not examine whether financialised aspirations actually lead to improved retirement outcomes — only captures intentions.
- Potential selection bias: course participants are already motivated to change; non-participants may hold different but equally valid strategies.
