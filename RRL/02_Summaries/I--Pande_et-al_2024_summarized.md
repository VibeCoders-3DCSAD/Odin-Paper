## TL;DR

A 20-year systematic literature review shows growing scholarly interest in personal financial planning, the rise of digital tools like mobile apps, and persistent gaps in understanding financial behaviors across socioeconomic groups.

## Problem and Motivation

No comprehensive systematic review exists that synthesizes two decades of personal financial planning literature, leaving researchers without a clear map of trends and gaps. Understanding these patterns is critical for policymakers and practitioners aiming to improve individual financial well-being amid economic volatility. Prior work lacked a structured, longitudinal synthesis of the evolution of financial planning practices, technology’s role, and behavioral factors.

## Approach

- PRISMA framework guided the systematic literature review of the Scopus database.
- Search terms: “Personal Financial Planning”, “Financial Wellness”, “Financial Management”, combined with “Behavioral Finance” and “Financial Literacy”.
- Inclusion criteria: 2004–2024, English, final-stage documents in economics, finance, business, social sciences.
- Exclusion: non-English, duplicates, documents before 2004.
- Final corpus: 158 documents.
- Analysis: bibliometric (year, source, affiliation, country) plus content analysis.

## Findings

1. Publication output grew from fewer than 5 papers per year (2004–2009) to **peak years of 2017, 2019, 2020, and 2023**, exceeding 15 papers in each.
2. The *Journal of Financial Counselling and Planning* was the most productive source, publishing 17 of the 47 identified documents.
3. United States led geographical output with 62 publications, followed by India (19) and Malaysia (18).
4. Kansas State University produced the most papers (9), followed by Ohio State University (8).

- Digital tools (mobile apps, robo-advisors, FinTech) emerged as central themes in recent years.
- Financial literacy consistently linked to better financial planning and management practices.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This review confirms that mobile applications and digital planning tools have become central to personal financial management, directly supporting Odin’s mobile-first design decision. The identification of persistent gaps in understanding financial behaviors across socioeconomic groups justifies Odin’s focus on a specific demographic (Filipino young professionals) rather than assuming universal patterns. The paper’s finding that financial literacy influences planning practices can be cited to argue that intelligent systems like Odin must supplement user literacy through automated recommendations and behavioral nudges. Although the review is bibliometric rather than algorithmic, it provides RRL-level evidence that the domain has matured and that technology-mediated planning is now the norm.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Mobile applications and robo-advisors have made financial information and services easily accessible, enabling individuals to manage their finances.” (Haridan et al., 2020, cited in review)
- “There is a lack of clear identification of the cause-related constructs critical to individuals' financial processes” – a gap that Odin’s behavioral profiling module can address.
- “People with better financial literacy have better practices (budgeting, monitoring expenditures, managing debt)” – justifying Odin’s role as a literacy-supporting tool.
- “FinTech has democratized investment opportunities and connected unbanked individuals to banking services” – supporting Odin’s inclusion of budgeting for underserved young professionals.

**Limits of relevance:**

- The review is bibliometric, not empirical; it reports on publication trends, not causal effects of digital tools on outcomes.
- No specific algorithm (LSTM, Isolation Forest, etc.) is evaluated, so it cannot justify algorithm selection directly.
- Findings are global; Filipino cultural and economic specificities are not addressed.
- The review does not distinguish between manual vs. automated transaction entry, a key Odin design constraint.

## Limitations

- Only the Scopus database was used; other databases (Web of Science, Google Scholar) were excluded. [unacknowledged]
- Potential selection and interpretation bias, as acknowledged by the authors.
- Cross-sectional bibliometric data cannot establish causality between FinTech adoption and financial well-being. [unacknowledged]
- No analysis of user retention, usability, or system evaluation metrics (e.g., SUS, ISO 25010).
- Geographic output heavily skewed to the US (62 of 158 papers), limiting generalizability to the Philippines.
