## TL;DR

Financial well-being fully mediates the relationship between financial behavior and stress in irregular income workers aged 20–40, meaning behavior reduces stress only indirectly through improved well-being.

## Problem and Motivation

Irregular income workers face high stress due to financial instability, but the mechanism linking their financial behavior to stress remains unclear. Understanding this mechanism is critical for designing interventions to reduce stress in this vulnerable population. Prior studies examined financial behavior and well-being separately, but none tested financial well-being as a mediator specifically for irregular income workers.

## Approach

- Sample: 266 irregular income workers aged 20–40 in Indonesia (plus 200 for instrument validation).
- Instruments: DASS-21 stress scale (7 items), Financial Management Behavior Scale (4 factors: cash, credit, savings, insurance), Financial Well-Being Scale (8 items).
- Procedure: Online survey via Google Forms after informed consent.
- Analysis: Simple mediation using Hayes Process macro (Model 4) with bootstrapping (95% CI).
- Validation: Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) for each instrument; all fit indices met criteria after modifications.

## Findings

1. Financial behavior positively predicts financial well-being: β = **0.6566** (p < .001).
2. Financial well-being negatively predicts stress: β = -0.6761 (p < .001).
3. The indirect effect of financial behavior on stress via financial well-being is **-0.5045** (95% CI [-0.6070, -0.4119]) — full mediation, as the direct effect (c') is non-significant (β = -0.1039, p = .0564).

- Financial behavior alone explains 30% of stress variance; adding well-being raises explained variance to 56%.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Irregular income workers | Individuals whose earnings fluctuate unpredictably (e.g., freelancers, gig workers). |
| Financial behavior | Actions like budgeting, saving, using credit, and buying insurance. |
| Financial well-being | Perceived adequacy and stability of one’s financial resources. |
| Stress (DASS-21) | Difficulty relaxing, irritability, overreaction, and nervous arousal. |
| Mediation | A variable (here, financial well-being) explains the relationship between two others. |
| COR theory | Conservation of Resources theory: stress occurs when resources are threatened or lost. [think: like a resource reservoir] |
| CFA | Confirmatory Factor Analysis – tests whether measured items reflect intended concepts. |

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper directly informs Odin’s user profiling and budget recommendation modules by demonstrating that financial well-being is the mechanism through which financial behavior (including budgeting) reduces stress—particularly for irregular income workers, which matches Odin’s target demographic. The finding that behavior alone does not directly lower stress justifies Odin’s focus on improving financial well-being (e.g., through smart budget recommendations and stability signals) rather than simply tracking transactions. While the study is from Indonesia, the psychological mechanism likely generalizes to Filipino young professionals with irregular income, supporting Odin’s design to prioritize well-being as an intermediate outcome.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Financial behavior does not directly reduce stress among irregular income workers aged 20–40; its effect is fully mediated by financial well-being (indirect effect = -0.5045, 95% CI excludes zero).”
- “Financial behavior positively predicts financial well-being in irregular income workers (β = 0.6566, p < .001), indicating that better budgeting and saving practices improve perceived financial stability.”
- “Higher financial well-being is associated with significantly lower stress levels (β = -0.6761, p < .001), a relationship that holds even when controlling for financial behavior.”
- “Irregular income workers—who face income volatility and uncertainty—require financial behavior improvements to first raise financial well-being before stress reduction occurs.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is Indonesian, not Filipino; cultural and economic differences (e.g., social security systems, cost of living) may affect generalizability.
- No algorithmic or ML component; relevance is behavioral and psychological, not computational.
- Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality, only mediation pathways.
- Self-reported data may be subject to social desirability and recall bias.

## Limitations

- Online survey may introduce sampling bias (only participants with internet access).
- Cross-sectional data; mediation claims are statistical, not causal. [unacknowledged]
- Only internal psychological variables considered; external factors (social support, economic shocks) not included.
- Instruments adapted but not validated specifically for Filipino context. [unacknowledged]
- No longitudinal follow-up to assess whether improved financial behavior sustains well-being over time.
