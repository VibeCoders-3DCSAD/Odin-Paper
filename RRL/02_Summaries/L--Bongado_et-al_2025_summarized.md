## TL;DR

Digital wallet use explains 45.4% of variance in financial behavior among Filipino HEI students, with positive effects on cash management and budgeting.

## Problem and Motivation

The influence of digital wallets on students' financial behavior remains underexplored despite widespread adoption. Understanding this relationship matters because digital wallets could either promote financial discipline or encourage impulsive spending. Prior studies reported mixed findings—some linked e-wallets to overspending, others to improved transaction control—without a clear empirical resolution.

## Approach

- Sample: 219 randomly selected HEI students at Bukidnon State University – Talisayan Campus.
- Design: Quantitative descriptive-correlational, using adapted 4-point Likert-scale survey.
- Independent variables: Perceived ease of use, usefulness, value, social influence, attractiveness of alternatives, trust, security, and intention to use (based on TAM).
- Dependent variables: Financial behavior measured as cash management and financial planning/budgeting.
- Analysis: Pearson correlation and linear regression (α = 0.05).

## Findings

1. All digital wallet determinants scored means of 2.91–2.99 ("Agree"), with Perceived Trust showing highest variability (SD = 1.24).
2. Financial behavior overall mean = 2.99 (SD = 0.68), indicating general agreement with responsible practices.
3. **Digital wallet usage accounted for 45.4% of variance in financial behavior (R² = 0.454, p < .001).**
4. Standardized beta coefficient β = 0.673 (p < .001), showing a positive, significant influence.

- Students with stronger intention to use digital wallets exhibited better budgeting and cash management.

## Key Figures and Tables

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is HEI students, not young professionals (Odin's target), though both are digital-native Filipinos with similar financial behaviors.
- Focuses on general financial behavior (cash management, budgeting) rather than specific ML-based forecasting or anomaly detection.

- No direct evaluation of budgeting algorithms or recommendation systems — only user-reported behavior.

## Limitations

- Sample limited to one state university in Talisayan (rural Northern Mindanao), not generalizable to all Filipino young professionals or urban Metro Manila users.
- Self-reported survey data subject to social desirability bias; actual financial behavior may differ from reported practices. [unacknowledged]
- Cross-sectional design precludes conclusions about long-term effects of digital wallet use on financial discipline.
- Did not control for income level, digital literacy, or parental financial socialization, which could confound the observed relationship. [unacknowledged]
- R² of 0.454 indicates that 54.6% of variance in financial behavior is explained by other unmeasured factors.
