## TL;DR

Filipino nurses face persistent income-expense deficits due to low government salary grades and high family obligations, leaving most unable to save for emergencies or retirement.

## Problem and Motivation

Nurses in the Philippines experience a structural mismatch between standardized salaries and rising living costs, especially in Metro Manila, leading to financial insecurity. This insecurity reduces job satisfaction, increases turnover, and drives migration, threatening healthcare workforce stability. Prior research focused on burnout and migration but did not examine financial independence as a central, policy-relevant issue.

## Approach

- Qualitative phenomenological design with 28 registered nurses from three government Level III hospitals in Metro Manila.
- Purposive sampling; participants were licensed, clinically active nurses; excluded unlicensed, on leave, or administrative roles.
- Semi-structured, validated interview guide (Aiken’s V 0.8–1.0) with 40–60 minute one-on-one interviews.
- Thematic analysis using Colaizzi’s method, with participant validation for credibility.
- Participants categorized into three types: single living alone (3), single adult breadwinner (12), married breadwinner (13).

## Findings

1. **64.29%** of participants were female; **53.57%** aged 33–39; **50.0%** in government salary grades 2–6.
2. **53.57%** held master’s degrees; **89.29%** were family breadwinners; **53.57%** supported four or more dependents.
3. **Only a few nurses reported modest savings**; the majority experienced monthly deficits, with zero capacity for long-term planning.

- Single nurses living alone can cover rent, food, and utilities but **cannot save**; they feel functional rather than empowered financial independence.
- Single adult breadwinners face severe strain: “I barely have anything left after I send money to my family” and experience chronic anxiety.
- Financial independence is perceived as essential for personal security, professional motivation, family support, stress reduction, autonomy, and retirement planning.
- Proposed Nurse Financial Empowerment and Resilience Program (N-FERP) includes financial literacy workshops, supplementary income channels, salary adjustments, and affordable cooperative loans.

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is nurses, not the general young professional population; income and obligation structures may differ (e.g., nursing has standardized government salary grades).
- No quantitative income/expense amounts are reported; only qualitative deficits and percentages, limiting direct calibration of forecasting models.
- Single nurses living alone sample is very small (n=3), so findings for that subgroup are tentative.
- The study does not evaluate any digital financial tool; relevance is behavioral and categorical, not algorithmic.

## Limitations

- Purposive sampling from only three Metro Manila government hospitals; findings may not generalize to private sector or provincial nurses.
- No quantitative income or expense data collected; relies on self-reported qualitative descriptions of deficits. [unacknowledged]
