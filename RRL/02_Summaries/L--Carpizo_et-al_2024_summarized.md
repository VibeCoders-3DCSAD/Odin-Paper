## TL;DR

AUP-CES livelihood initiatives and management training enabled Buklod Bahayan residents to build a resilient cooperative that provides income, loans, camaraderie, and crisis support.

## Problem and Motivation

Community cooperatives need effective leadership and management to survive, but the specific impact of training seminars on long-term sustainability remains underexplored. Without structured capacity-building, many community‑based initiatives fail after initial success. This study evaluates how AUP’s Community Extension Services (CES) training directly affects cooperative resilience in a low‑income Philippine subdivision.

## Approach

- **Participants:** Members of the Paluwagan Family Cooperative in Buklod Bahayan, Tartaria, Cavite.
- **Data collection:** Focus Group Discussions (FGDs) and individual interviews recorded and transcribed.
- **Instrument:** Three open‑ended questions translated to Filipino, validated by three consultants.
- **Analysis:** Thematic analysis to extract economic, social, and organizational themes.
- **Context:** Follow‑up on a previous failed cooperative (Buklod Producers Cooperative) that received initial AUP‑CES training.

## Findings

1. **Economic return:** A Php 3,300 share investment yielded a **Php 5,580 dividend** (169% return); members can borrow up to 10K based on share capital.
2. **Loan access:** Cooperative offers payable loans within a year or less, depending on repayment capacity.
3. **Social cohesion:** Members report strong camaraderie, mutual help during crises, and a sense of security not available from relatives.
4. **Organizational learning:** Accounting and leadership seminars from AUP‑CES directly enabled members to reorganize after a prior cooperative failure.
5. **Resilience:** The cooperative has operated successfully for **10+ years** after restarting with only five members.
6. **Skill transfer:** Members explicitly credit AUP‑CES for teaching accounting, organizing the group, and providing free management seminars.

## Limitations

- Small sample from one subdivision; findings may not generalize to other Philippine communities.
- No quantitative measurement of economic impact beyond self‑reported dividends and loans.
- Retrospective accounts may suffer from recall bias.
- The previous cooperative’s failure is not analyzed in detail, limiting lessons on what not to do.
