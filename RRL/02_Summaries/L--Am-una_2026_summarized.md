## TL;DR

Public school teachers in Baguio City show moderately positive financial behaviors, but these reflect "conscious constraint"—disciplined management of scarce resources—not true financial security.

## Problem and Motivation

Prior financial literacy research has focused on what people know, not how they actually behave under economic constraint, creating a knowledge–action gap. This gap matters because teachers model financial behavior for students and face rising costs, stagnant salaries, and persistent debt. No existing study had jointly examined behavioral frequency and perceived difficulty to distinguish necessity-driven practices from mastery.

## Approach

- Sample: 335 regular public school teachers (Kindergarten to Senior High) in Baguio City, Philippines.
- Design: Explanatory sequential mixed-methods (survey + 9 follow-up interviews).
- Instrument: Modified OECD/INFE 2020 survey measuring financial behavior frequency (4-point scale) and perceived difficulty.
- Moderators tested: Marital status (ANOVA), employment rank (t-test), financial literacy seminar attendance (t-test).
- Perceived difficulty across five behavioral domains analyzed with Friedman test and Nemenyi post hoc.

## Findings

1. General financial behavior mean = 2.69 (moderately positive), but qualitative data show this reflects endurance, not security.
2. **No significant difference in financial behaviors between seminar attendees and non-attendees (p = .991)**—identical means of 2.69.
3. Married teachers scored significantly lower than single teachers (p = .034), while Master Teachers scored higher than non-Master Teachers (M=3.00 vs 2.65, p=.002).
4. Budgeting was most difficult (M=2.17) yet performed often—paradox explained by "conscious constraint."

- Choosing financial products was easiest (M=4.15) despite low ownership, suggesting overconfidence from institutional endorsement bias.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Conscious constraint | Disciplined financial management under limited income, where positive behaviors reflect necessity, not mastery. |
| Knowledge–action gap | The disconnect between knowing what to do financially and actually doing it under structural constraints. |
| Financial behavior | Concrete actions individuals take to manage finances: budgeting, saving, debt management, and product selection. |
| Perceived difficulty | The psychological effort, stress, and friction a person experiences when performing a financial behavior. |

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is public school teachers, not the target Filipino young professional demographic (ages 20–40) in Metro Manila; income and obligation structures may differ.
- Data collected in Baguio City only; spending patterns and cost-of-living pressures may not generalize to Metro Manila.
- No evaluation of any ML or algorithmic approach—behavioral findings only, not method-specific.
- Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality between demographic factors and behavior changes.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design limits causal inference about how financial behaviors evolve over time.
- Self-reported financial behavior data may be subject to social desirability bias. [unacknowledged]
- Sample excludes non-permanent teachers (contractual, substitute), who may face even greater financial precarity.
- Qualitative phase used only nine interviews; thematic saturation claimed but limited generalizability.
- No longitudinal follow-up to assess whether "conscious constraint" persists or leads to financial deterioration.
