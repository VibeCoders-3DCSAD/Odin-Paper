## TL;DR

High financial knowledge and positive attitudes among Filipino maritime students do not translate into better financial behavior, revealing a gap between knowing and doing.

## Problem and Motivation

Financial literacy research often assumes knowledge and positive attitudes lead directly to responsible financial behavior, but evidence among young adults is mixed. Maritime students in the Philippines face unique income patterns (high but irregular earnings as future seafarers), making their financial preparedness critical. Prior studies focused on general students or working adults, leaving a gap in understanding whether knowledge and attitude actually predict behavior for this vocational group.

## Approach

- Descriptive-correlational design with 100 randomly selected maritime students from a private Philippine higher education institution.
- Data collected using adapted standardized Likert-scale questionnaires for financial knowledge (10 items), attitude (10 items), and behavior (7 items).
- Respondents had weekly allowance ≥ ₱1,000 to ensure consistent financial exposure.
- Analysis used mean, standard deviation, and Pearson correlation (r) with significance at p < 0.01.
- Scales: 1.0–1.74 Very Low, 1.75–2.49 Low, 2.50–3.24 High, 3.25–4.0 Very High.

## Findings

1. Mean financial knowledge = **3.74** (Very High; SD=0.14).
2. Mean financial attitude = 3.71 (Very High; SD=0.10).
3. Mean financial behavior = 3.85 (Very High; SD=0.17).
4. Financial knowledge vs. behavior: r = -0.46, p = 0.652 → **not significant**.
5. Financial attitude vs. behavior: r = 0.59, p = 0.558 → not significant.

## Key Figures and Tables

- Table 4: Knowledge–behavior correlation → r = -0.46, p = 0.652 → no meaningful link.
- Table 5: Attitude–behavior correlation → r = 0.59, p = 0.558 → positive but not statistically significant.

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is maritime students, not the full spectrum of Filipino young professionals (ages 20–40); income patterns (lump‑sum seafarer pay) may not generalize.
- All measures are self‑reported Likert scales; no objective transaction data was collected, so actual behavior may differ from claimed behavior.
- Sample size (n=100) from a single private institution limits generalizability to lower‑income or non‑maritime young Filipinos.
- No longitudinal data — the study cannot determine whether the knowledge‑behavior gap persists after students enter the workforce.

## Limitations

- Self‑reported financial behavior may suffer from social desirability bias; students may overstate good habits. [unacknowledged]
- Correlation design prevents causal claims — the study cannot determine if knowledge/attitude cause behavior or vice versa.
- Single institution in Ozamiz City; findings may not represent maritime students from other regions or public institutions.
- Weekly allowance threshold (≥₱1,000) excludes lower‑income students, potentially narrowing the observed behavior range. [unacknowledged]
- No qualitative component to explain why the gap exists — only quantitative correlations are reported.
