## TL;DR

Myopic decision-making among low-income micro-entrepreneurs in Manila significantly reduces business performance (p < 0.001), but better access to finance moderates this negative effect (p = 0.005).

## Problem and Motivation

Micro-enterprises operated by low-income households in Manila often suffer from myopic decision-making (prioritizing short-term gains) and poor access to formal financing, yet no prior study has examined how these two factors jointly affect business performance. These enterprises are vital for Philippine employment and poverty alleviation, so understanding this relationship is critical for targeted interventions. The literature lacks an integrated analysis of myopic decision-making, access to finance, and business performance specifically for urban micro-enterprises in low-income Filipino contexts.

## Approach

- Sample: 100 sari-sari store owners from low-income households across Manila districts (purposive non‑probability sampling).
- Data collection: In‑person pen‑and‑paper survey over five weeks.
- Myopic decision-making measured via four dimensions (competitive, cooperative, temporal, learning) using a 5‑point Likert scale (Czakon et al., 2023).
- Access to finance measured via barriers to access, access to formal lending institutions, and access to informal credit (Amadasun & Mutezo, 2022).
- Business performance measured via financial performance, customer satisfaction, market competitiveness, growth mindset, and operational efficiency.
- Analysis: Descriptive statistics (means, standard deviations) and hypothesis testing using regression/moderated regression (p‑values reported).

## Findings

1. Myopic decision-making has a **significant negative effect** on business performance (p < 0.001).
2. Access to finance has a **significant positive effect** on business performance (p < 0.001).
3. Access to finance **significantly moderates** the relationship between myopic decision-making and business performance (p = 0.005).

- Temporal myopia (short‑term financial focus) scored highest among the four myopia dimensions (mean = 2.96/5).
- Access to formal lending institutions is very low (mean = 1.49/5), while barriers to access are moderate (mean = 2.81/5).
- Customer satisfaction scored highest among business performance components (mean = 3.53/5).

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Myopic decision-making | A cognitive bias that prioritizes immediate rewards over long-term benefits (short‑sighted choices). |
| Access to finance | The ability of a household or business to obtain formal financial services (loans, savings, payments). |
| Micro-enterprise | A business with assets under ₱3 million and fewer than 10 employees (e.g., sari‑sari store). |
| Low-income households | Filipino families earning ≤ ₱24,000 monthly, enough for basic food but not for non‑food essentials. |
| Temporal myopia | Prioritizing short‑term financial outcomes at the expense of long‑term investments and strategic growth. |
| Competitive myopia | Narrow focus on immediate nearby competitors, overlooking less obvious threats or opportunities. |
| Cooperative myopia | Ignoring potential collaborations with competitors, suppliers, or customers that could create joint value. |
| Learning myopia | Reluctance to learn from past experiences or adapt to new information, leading to strategic inertia. |
| Formal lending institutions | Government‑regulated entities (banks, cooperatives) that require collateral and documentation for credit. |
| Informal credit | Funding from unregulated sources (family, friends, pawnshops, payday lenders) often with higher costs. |

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is micro‑enterprise owners (sari‑sari stores), not personal finance users; findings may not fully generalize to young professionals with different income sources and spending patterns.
- Myopia measured via self‑reported Likert scales, not objective transactional data; potential social desirability bias.
- Correlational design (no causal inference); the moderation effect is statistical, not experimentally identified.
- Business performance metrics (profitability, customer satisfaction) differ from personal budget adherence outcomes.

## Limitations

- Sample size of 100 and non‑probability purposive sampling limit generalizability to all low‑income micro‑entrepreneurs in Manila.
- Self‑report bias may inflate or deflate reported myopia levels and business performance.
- No longitudinal data — cannot observe whether myopia changes with improved access to finance over time. [unacknowledged]
- Focus exclusively on sari‑sari stores; results may not apply to other micro‑enterprise types (e.g., street vendors, home‑based services). [unacknowledged]
- Did not control for education level, household size, or number of dependents — potential confounders of both myopia and performance. [unacknowledged]
