problem_and_motivation: Micro-enterprises face high failure rates due to poor cash management, financial illiteracy, and lack of disciplined budgeting. Existing studies show gaps in linking cash management practices directly to financial performance indicators such as sales growth, profitability, and liquidity. This study addresses the need for empirical evidence on these relationships in the Philippine context.
approach:
  - Data were collected from 163 micro-enterprise owners in Baco, Oriental Mindoro using random sampling.
  - A researcher-made survey questionnaire with a 4-point Likert scale measured budgeting and cash flow management (operating, financing, investing activities).
  - Financial performance was measured via sales growth, profitability, and liquidity indicators.
  - Cronbach's alpha (0.790 to 0.925) confirmed acceptable to excellent reliability for all constructs.
  - Spearman's correlation was used due to non-normal data distributions.
findings:
  - "num: Budgeting shows strong positive correlations with sales growth (rho=0.506), profitability (0.536), and liquidity (0.526)."
  - "num: Cash flow management has even stronger correlations: sales growth (0.648), profitability (0.558), and liquidity (0.589)."
  - Micro-enterprises practice cash management at a moderate extent overall, with budgeting for monitoring performance at a high extent.
  - Gaps exist in sticking to prepared budgets, regularly updating cash flow plans, and quickly converting assets to cash.
  - Profitability and liquidity scales showed excellent reliability (Cronbach's alpha 0.913 and 0.925).
