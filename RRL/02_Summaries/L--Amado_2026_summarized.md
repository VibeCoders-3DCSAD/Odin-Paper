## TL;DR

Twice-a-month salary release improves short-term financial planning for teachers, but financial stress persists; higher financial literacy directly enables better budgeting and survival strategies.

## Problem and Motivation

Prior studies treat financial literacy as a static skill without analyzing how bi-monthly salary cycles constrain access to conventional budgeting strategies. Teacher debt in the Philippines exceeds ₱319 billion, and structural pay timing may inadvertently drive debt cycles rather than reduce them. No explicit analysis exists of the inter-payday period as a psychological and economic stressor that dictates resource management.

## Approach

- Sample: 115 full-time permanent teachers from Claveria North District, SY 2023–2024, with at least one year in the twice-a-month system.
- Design: Descriptive survey using a structured questionnaire with a 5-point Likert scale.
- Reliability: Cronbach’s α = 0.88 after pilot testing with teachers outside the main sample.
- Variables measured: financial well-being, management practices, literacy, coping strategies, perceived advantages/disadvantages.
- Analysis: Weighted mean and percentage distribution.

## Findings

1. Overall perceived impact of twice-a-month salary release on financial well-being had a weighted mean of **4.18 (Agree)**.
2. Financial management practices overall mean = 4.13; highest item: prioritizing essential expenses (mean 4.46).
3. Perceived advantages (overall mean 4.23) slightly outweigh disadvantages (overall mean 3.50); stress between pay periods persisted (mean 3.63).
4. **Teachers with higher financial literacy are better equipped to handle financial challenges under bi-monthly pay (mean 4.37)**.
5. Survival strategies overall mean = 4.07; strict budgeting (4.13) and seeking supplementary income (3.87) are common.

- Bi-monthly salary release does not fully eliminate financial anxiety; teachers still report difficulty covering expenses in the first half of the month (mean 3.65).

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Twice-a-month salary release | Salary paid on the 15th and 30th of each month (also called bi-monthly). |
| Financial literacy | Understanding of budgeting, saving, investing, and debt management to make informed financial decisions. |
| Survival strategies | Practical actions teachers take to stay financially afloat between paydays (e.g., side jobs, strict budgets). |
| Inter-payday period | The days between two salary releases; a time of liquidity constraint and potential stress. |
| Debt trap | A cycle where new loans are taken primarily to pay interest on existing debt, common among DepEd personnel. |
| DepEd | Department of Education (Philippines). |

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is public school teachers, not the broader young professional demographic; income levels and spending priorities may differ from Odin’s target users.
- Geography is Claveria North District (Masbate), which may not fully represent Metro Manila’s cost of living and financial behavior.
- Descriptive survey design only — no causal inference; self-reported perceptions may not reflect actual transaction-level behavior.
- No comparison to other salary frequencies (e.g., once‑monthly) within the same sample, limiting claims about relative advantage.
- Does not evaluate digital financial tools or app‑based interventions, only non‑digital coping strategies.

## Limitations

- Self‑report bias: Likert responses may overstate positive behaviors due to social desirability (acknowledged by author through anonymity measures).  
- Cross‑sectional design captures only a snapshot; cannot determine whether financial literacy causes better management or vice versa [unacknowledged].  
- No control for household size, number of dependents, or spousal income — these could confound the reported financial stress levels [unacknowledged].  
- Exclusion of teachers on long‑term leave or newly hired staff means findings may not apply to those in transition or with less than one year of experience.  
- Study does not quantify actual debt levels or track loan repayment cycles, limiting ability to assess true “debt trap” severity.
