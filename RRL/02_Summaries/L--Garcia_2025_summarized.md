## TL;DR

Public junior high school teachers in Quezon City show high financial literacy (mean 4.02/5) but are only “financially coping” (61.2/100), with critical gaps in emergency funds and retirement planning.

## Problem and Motivation

Limited research exists on the financial health of public-school teachers in urban Philippine districts, particularly in Quezon City. Teachers’ financial insecurity affects their personal well-being and professional effectiveness as educators. Prior studies did not comprehensively assess both financial literacy and health using validated instruments with detailed demographic comparisons.

## Approach

- Sample: 241 public junior high school teachers from five schools in Quezon City District VI, selected via proportionate random sampling.
- Instruments: Modified Financial Health Network (2024) questionnaire and Surendar & Subramanya Sarma (2018) financial literacy questionnaire.
- Validation: Cronbach’s alpha of 0.907 (literacy) and 0.815 (health), indicating good reliability.
- Design: Quantitative-descriptive, cross-sectional survey.
- Financial literacy dimensions (8): knowledge, behavior, attitude, budgeting/tax planning, liquidity management, financing large purchases, insurance, investment, retirement/estate planning.
- Financial health dimensions (4): spending, saving, borrowing, planning (scored 0–100).
- Analysis: Means for levels; Kruskal-Wallis H and Mann-Whitney U for group comparisons.

## Findings

1. Overall financial literacy mean = **4.02** (High), with retirement planning lowest (3.75) and financial knowledge highest (4.24).
2. Overall financial health mean = **61.2** (“Financially Coping”); spending bill payment history scored 86.41 (Healthy), but month-end surplus scored 39.00 (Vulnerable).
3. Emergency fund adequacy scored 38.07 (Vulnerable); concern about insufficient future savings scored 38.07 (Vulnerable).
4. Debt service consumed a large income share (mean 78.44 on 0–100, Financially Coping).

- Female teachers had significantly higher overall financial literacy than males (mean rank 126.87 vs 103.67, p=0.017).
- Significant differences in financial literacy and health appeared by age, number of children, income, education, teaching position, experience, and specialization.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Financially Coping | Can meet basic expenses but has little to no surplus for savings or long-term goals. |
| Financially Healthy | Consistently meets obligations, has savings buffer, and plans effectively for the future. |
| Financially Vulnerable | Struggles to cover expenses or has no safety net for unexpected shocks. |
| Financial literacy | Ability to understand and apply budgeting, saving, investing, and planning concepts. |

**Limits of relevance:**

- Sample consists of public school teachers (mean age 41+), not the target 20–40 young professional demographic.
- Findings are self-reported, not derived from actual transaction data, limiting direct comparability to Odin’s behavioral logs.
- No evaluation of digital financial tools or algorithm-based recommendations; purely descriptive survey.
- Geography limited to one district of Quezon City; may not generalize to other Philippine urban areas.

## Limitations

- Self-reported financial literacy and health scores may overstate actual abilities due to social desirability bias.
- Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality between demographic factors and financial outcomes.
- Sample drawn only from five schools in one district; results may not generalize to other regions or teacher populations.
- Financial health categories (coping, vulnerable, healthy) are not externally validated against objective financial defaults or insolvency events. [unacknowledged]
- No longitudinal follow-up to assess whether literacy translates into improved health over time. [unacknowledged]
