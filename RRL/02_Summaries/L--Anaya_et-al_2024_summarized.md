## TL;DR

Implicit peer pressure shows a weak positive correlation (r = 0.273) with impulsive buying among Filipino senior high school students, explaining only 7.45% of the variance.

## Problem and Motivation

Existing research on peer pressure and impulsive buying lacks focus on Filipino senior high school students, leaving a gap in understanding this relationship locally. Impulsive buying can undermine financial stability, especially among adolescents who are highly sensitive to social influences. Prior studies from other countries do not guarantee that findings transfer across cultural and age contexts.

## Approach

- Data from 108 senior high school students at a faith-based institution in Cavite, Philippines (simple random sampling from 313 eligible students).
- Descriptive-correlational design using printed and online survey questionnaires.
- Implicit peer pressure measured with Chang et al. (2019) scale; impulsive buying measured with modified Kim (2003) and Borromeo et al. (2022) scales.
- Analysis used Jamovi: means, standard deviations, Pearson correlation, and linear regression.
- Reliability: implicit pressure scale had acceptable consistency; impulsive buying scale had good reliability.

## Findings

1. Students reported **low implicit peer pressure** (overall M = 2.24, SD = 0.67 on a 1–5 scale).
2. Impulsive buying was **moderate** (overall M = 3.06, SD = 0.84).
3. **Weak positive correlation** (r = 0.273, p = 0.004) between implicit peer pressure and impulsive buying.
4. Implicit peer pressure explains only **7.45% of the variance** in impulsive buying (R² = 0.0745), with regression significant (p = 0.004, t = 2.94).

- The strongest impulsive buying triggers: seeing a good deal (M = 3.43) and post-purchase regret (M = 3.42).

**Limits of relevance:**

- Demographic mismatch: high school students vs. Odin’s target of young professionals (20–40); spending priorities and income sources differ substantially.
- Single faith‑based institution in Cavite, not representative of all Filipino young adults.
- Weak effect size (r = 0.273) limits the practical utility of peer pressure as a standalone predictor in Odin’s models.
- No financial literacy or budgeting behavior measured — the paper does not address budget adherence or forecasting.

## Limitations

- Small sample (n = 108) from one private, faith‑based school — limits generalizability to public school students or out‑of‑school youth. [unacknowledged]
- Self‑reported survey data may suffer from social desirability bias, especially on spending and peer influence.
- Cross‑sectional design cannot establish causality between peer pressure and impulsive buying.
- No control for allowance amount, household income, or access to digital payments — all relevant to Odin’s context. [unacknowledged]
- The study does not distinguish between online vs. offline spending, though digital spending is critical for young Filipinos.
