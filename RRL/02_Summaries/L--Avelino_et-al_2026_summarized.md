## TL;DR

Income level significantly affects solo parents' financial management and lifestyle modifications (p<.001), with Olongapo City respondents showing stronger adaptive behaviors than those in Balanga City.

## Problem and Motivation

Little empirical evidence exists on how solo parents adapt to economic pressures across different income levels in Philippine urban settings. Rising living costs disproportionately burden single-income solo-parent households, limiting their ability to save or handle emergencies. Prior work lacked comparative quantitative analysis of income-level effects on adaptive practices such as government support uptake, financial management, and lifestyle changes.

## Approach

- Causal-comparative design comparing two Philippine cities: Balanga City, Bataan and Olongapo City, Zambales.
- 363 solo parent respondents identified by local government social welfare offices (69.7% from Olongapo, 30.3% from Balanga).
- Adapted survey questionnaire measuring three adaptive practice domains: government support, work-child balance difficulties, and financial management/lifestyle modifications.
- Statistical analysis using weighted means, one-way ANOVA (Kruskal-Wallis H test), Spearman correlation, and reliability tests (Cronbach's α).
- Significance threshold set at p < 0.05; all three domains exceeded 0.86 Cronbach's α.

## Findings

1. Income level has a **highly significant effect on financial management and lifestyle modifications (χ²(5) = 47.4, p < .001)** — adaptive strategies differ across income brackets.
2. Income level also significantly affects perceived government support (χ²(5) = 23.6, p < .001) and work-child balance difficulties (χ²(5) = 83.8, p < .001).
3. Olongapo City solo parents reported higher mean scores than Balanga City in: government support (2.94 vs 2.33), work-child balance difficulties (3.50 vs 2.66), and financial management/lifestyle modifications (3.52 vs 2.45).

- A moderate positive correlation exists between financial management/lifestyle modifications and work-child balance difficulties (ρ = 0.515, p < .001).
- Majority of respondents (41.6%) earn less than ₱10,000/month; 39.7% earn ₱10,001–₱20,000; only 1.1% earn above ₱30,000.
- Most solo parents are never married (70.0%), aged 25–34 (59.2%), and work as vendors (14.3%).

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study focuses on solo parents, not exclusively on young professionals; age overlap exists (25–34) but parenthood status may change spending priorities.
- No evaluation of digital or mobile financial tools — all data from survey self-reports of general financial behavior.
- Sample includes many informal-sector workers (vendors, factory workers); Odin targets professionals with potentially more stable income patterns.
- Findings are descriptive correlations, not causal; no algorithm or ML method is tested or proposed.
- Geographic scope limited to two Luzon cities; may not generalize to other Philippine regions.

## Limitations

- Non-normal data distribution required non-parametric tests; parametric assumptions violated. [unacknowledged]
- Self-report survey bias — respondents may overstate adaptive behaviors or underreport difficulties.
- Cross-sectional design captures a single time point; cannot assess how adaptive practices change over time as income fluctuates.
- Income brackets have uneven sample sizes; very few respondents above ₱30,000 (1.1%) limit generalizability to higher-income solo parents.
- No qualitative follow-up to explain why Balanga respondents perceive less government support than Olongapo respondents.
