## TL;DR

Microfinance is a double‑edged sword: it provides short‑term relief but also creates chronic debt stress and psychological strain.

## Problem and Motivation

Existing microfinance research relies on quantitative metrics (repayment rates, income changes) and misses how borrowers *experience* debt emotionally. No prior study captured Filipino women’s subjective tension between financial access and debt‑related distress. This gap leaves policymakers blind to the hidden psychological costs of microfinance.

## Approach

- **Design:** Transcendental phenomenology (Moustakas, 1994) with Colaizzi’s 7‑step analysis.
- **Participants:** 15 purposively sampled female borrowers from Argao, Cebu (≥3 years borrowing, ≥3 active loans).
- **Data:** Semi‑structured interviews (30–45 min each) in Cebuano, adapted from CFPB Financial Well‑Being Scale.
- **Analysis:** Horizontalization → meaning units → themes → textural/structural descriptions.

## Findings

1. **Nine major themes** emerged: survival edge, family burden, relief, debt strain, adaptive resourcefulness, emotional resilience, faith anchoring, growth catalyst, and continuing burden.
2. **All participants** reported repayment pressure, with aggressive collectors causing shame and fear.
3. **Overlapping loans** (3–7 simultaneous accounts) trapped many in **cyclical debt** where new loans paid old debts.
4. **Psychological toll** included insomnia, panic attacks, suicidal thoughts, and brief disappearance from home.
5. **Coping strategies** divided into problem‑focused (budgeting, hustling) and emotion‑focused (positive thinking, prayer, family support).
6. **Positive outcomes** for some: house improvements, children’s education, livestock businesses, and a sense of money control.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Ka‑abag | Cebuano for “support” – microfinance as a helping tool. |
| Babag | Cebuano for “hindrance” – microfinance as an obstacle or trap. |
| Colaizzi’s method | A 7‑step phenomenological analysis procedure for qualitative interview data. |
| Turko / 5‑6 | Informal lending schemes with extremely high interest (e.g., borrow 5, pay 6). |

## Critical Citations

- [Lazarus & Folkman, 1984] — Provides the Transactional Model of Stress and Coping, used as the psychological framework to interpret debt as “challenge vs. threat.”
- [Sen, 1999] — Capability Approach, used to distinguish between mere loan access and real freedom/well‑being.
- [De Silva & Gunawardana, 2023] — Parallel Sri Lankan evidence that microfinance traps women in debt cycles.

## Limitations

- Single municipality (Argao, Cebu) – findings not generalizable to urban areas or other regions.
- All 15 participants are women – excludes male borrower perspectives.
- Self‑reported narratives subject to recall bias and social desirability.
- Cross‑sectional design – cannot track how coping strategies evolve over multiple loan cycles.

## Remember This

- 🔑 **3–7 simultaneous loans per participant** – overlapping debt is the norm, not an exception.  
- ⚠️ Microfinance acts as **both ka‑abag (support) and babag (hindrance)** – never a pure win or pure loss.  
- 💡 Coping strategies include **prayer, humor, and family support** – not just budgeting or hustling.
