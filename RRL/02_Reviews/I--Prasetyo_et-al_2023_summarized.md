## TL;DR

Gamification in personal finance apps can boost behavioral intention, but research is sparse and lacks robust theory — this review synthesizes 53 articles to map gaps.

## Problem and Motivation

Most gamification research focuses on education, leaving personal finance understudied despite rising PFM app adoption. No prior review connects financial behavior and gamification-related behavioral intention. A systematic mapping is needed to guide future design and theory.

## Approach

- Systematic literature review following Kitchenham (2004) guidelines.
- Searched Emerald, IEEE Xplore, Science Direct, Taylor & Francis (2018–2022).
- Search terms combined “financial behavior” and “gamification” with theory/model keywords.
- Initial 27,888 articles filtered to 53 empirical studies (27 financial behavior, 26 gamification).
- Used TCCM framework (Theory, Context, Characteristics, Methodology) for synthesis.

## Findings

1. Theory of Planned Behavior dominates financial behavior studies (50%), while Self-Determination Theory is most common in gamification research (14.3%).
2. **Only 5 of 29 gamification studies focus on finance** — most cover education, health, or general games.
3. Most cited gamification elements: badges (9.7%), points (9.7%), leaderboards (6.4%).
4. Gamification research overwhelmingly measures behavioral intention, not actual observed behavior.
5. Key antecedents of gamification adoption: hedonic motivation, perceived usefulness, and competence.

## Relevance to Odin

**Topics:**

A.2 Existing Personal Finance and Budget Management Systems — The paper reviews PFM app features, gamification elements in banking and budgeting apps, and identifies limitations of current systems.

I.1 User Retention and Engagement in Personal Finance Systems — Gamification is explicitly positioned as a mechanism to sustain user engagement, reduce discontinuance, and drive continued use of financial apps.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This SLR provides empirical justification that gamification elements (badges, points, leaderboards) significantly influence behavioral intention in PFM contexts. It supports Odin’s design decision to incorporate lightweight gamification (e.g., achievement tracking, progress feedback) to boost user retention without increasing cognitive load. The paper also highlights that gamification should be tied to meaningful financial behaviors — not just superficial rewards — which aligns with Odin’s goal of value-driven engagement.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Gamification in personal finance apps increases users’ behavioral intention to engage with budgeting and spending tracking.”
- “Badges, points, and leaderboards are the most frequently validated gamification elements for driving continuance intention.”
- “Lack of robust theory in gamification-for-finance research suggests opportunity for novel designs grounded in self-determination theory.”
- “Measuring actual financial behavior (not just intention) remains a gap, but gamification positively affects intention as a proximal outcome.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- Review includes only 5 finance-specific gamification studies; majority come from education and health domains.
- None of the 53 studies are set in the Philippines or focus on Filipino young professionals.
- Does not evaluate algorithmic or ML-based personalization of gamification.
- Findings are aggregated across app categories (banking, robo-advisors, PFM) — not exclusively budgeting apps.

## Limitations

- Only articles from 2018–2022; earlier foundational work may be missed.
- No meta-analysis or effect-size aggregation; qualitative synthesis only.
- Heavy reliance on questionnaire-based studies — common method bias not fully addressed.
- Gamification research often studies intention, not long-term retention or actual spending changes.
- Limited discussion of potential negative effects (e.g., addiction, superficial engagement).

## Remember This

- 🔑 **Only 5 of 29 gamification papers study finance** — big gap this review exposes.
- 💡 TPB and SDT are the dominant theories; Odin can ground engagement features in SDT.
- 📌 Badges and points appear in ~10% of studies — simple, mobile-friendly elements.
- ⚠️ Most gamification research measures *intention*, not actual financial behavior change.
- 🧠 Gamification for retention works best when tied to meaningful financial actions, not just points.
