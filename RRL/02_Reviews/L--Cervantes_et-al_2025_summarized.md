## TL;DR

Social media exposure and instant gratification drive unplanned online purchases, which strongly correlate (r=0.61) with poor budget management among Filipino business students.

## Problem and Motivation

Online buying decisions influenced by social media, instant gratification, and marketing tactics disrupt personal budgets, yet students with limited financial resources remain highly vulnerable. Understanding this relationship is essential because Filipino young adults (ages 18–24) are the most active digital consumers and face rising financial strain from impulsive e-commerce spending. Prior studies had not quantified the correlation specifically among Filipino business administration students using descriptive-correlational methods.

## Approach

- Descriptive-correlational design with 153 randomly selected business administration students at Bukidnon State University – Baungon Campus.
- Validated questionnaire adapted from Aiswarya and Mohan (2014) measured online buying behavior and budgeting practices.
- Four-point Likert scale (Strongly Agree to Strongly Disagree) with interpretation ranges (1.00–4.00).
- Statistical analysis: mean and standard deviation for perception levels; Pearson’s r for relationship; t‑test and ANOVA for group differences.
- SPSS software used for all calculations.

## Findings

1. Mean perception of online buying decisions was 3.02 (Agree / High), with social media likes and comments scoring highest at 3.10.
2. Mean perception of personal budget management was 3.15 (Agree / High); necessary expenses scored 3.32 (Strongly Agree / Very High).
3. **Pearson’s r = 0.612 (p < 0.05)** — a strong, significant positive relationship between online buying decisions and personal budgeting.

- Age and civil status significantly affect allowance spending and savings (p < 0.05); sex affects only savings.
- Monthly allowance does not significantly influence online buying’s effect on allowance or spending, but does affect savings.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Online buying decisions | Purchasing goods via internet, influenced by social media, promotions, and psychological triggers. |
| Personal budget | A plan for allocating monthly allowance to expenses and savings. |
| Impulsive purchases | Unplanned buying driven by immediate gratification rather than necessity. |
| Instant gratification | The tendency to choose immediate rewards (e.g., buying an item now) over long-term financial benefits. |
| Hyperbolic discounting | A psychological bias where near-term rewards are valued disproportionately more than future ones [think: “I want it now, tomorrow’s budget doesn’t matter”]. |

## Limitations

- Sample limited to 153 Business Administration students at a single campus; findings may not generalize to other disciplines or regions.
- Self‑reported data on budgeting and buying behavior may suffer from social desirability bias or recall error. [unacknowledged]
- Correlational design cannot establish causality — online buying and poor budgeting may influence each other bidirectionally. [unacknowledged]
- The study measures only perceptual Likert responses, not actual spending amounts or objective budget adherence rates. [unacknowledged]
- No longitudinal data — the effect over time or seasonal variations in online buying behavior are not captured. [unacknowledged]
