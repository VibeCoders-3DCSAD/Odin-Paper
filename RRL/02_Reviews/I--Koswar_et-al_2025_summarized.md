## TL;DR

Mobile internet and national digital ID systems in South Asia have expanded financial inclusion, but persistent rural-urban infrastructure gaps and low digital literacy limit equitable adoption of credit and insurance products.

## Problem and Motivation

Comprehensive, empirically grounded reviews on how digitization affects customer engagement and financial product adoption in South Asia are scarce despite rapid digital banking growth. Understanding these dynamics matters because digitization fundamentally reshapes user behavior, financial access, and banking strategies across diverse socio-economic contexts. Prior work lacked integration of infrastructure quality, behavioral drivers, and regulatory frameworks into a cohesive region-specific analysis.

## Approach

- PRISMA 2020 systematic review methodology for structured and replicable synthesis.
- 84 peer-reviewed studies and institutional reports published between 2010 and 2024.
- Databases: Scopus, Web of Science, JSTOR, EBSCOhost, ProQuest, Google Scholar, plus World Bank and regional banking authority repositories.
- Geographic scope: South Asia (India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Afghanistan).
- Thematic focus: digital banking infrastructure, customer engagement, financial product adoption, AI/big data applications, inclusion barriers.

## Findings

1. Mobile phone penetration exceeds **85%** and mobile broadband reaches over 95% of the population in India and Sri Lanka, enabling mass digital banking access.
2. Digitally engaged customers are **2.3 times more likely** to open secondary financial products (insurance, investment accounts) than non-engaged counterparts.
3. Over 480 million bank accounts opened via India’s Jan Dhan Yojana, many accessed digitally, yet up to 40% of new accounts show minimal transaction activity.

- Urban-rural disparities persist: power outages, weak signal, and high data costs in remote areas limit meaningful use.
- Low digital literacy and gender gaps correlate with mistrust of digital systems and reduced use of credit/investment tools.
- AI-driven prompts, dashboards, and personalized nudges significantly increase user satisfaction and platform loyalty.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This systematic review directly supports Odin’s mobile-first design priority by documenting that mobile penetration (>85%) and mobile broadband (>95%) are the primary access channels in developing economies, justifying Odin’s mobile-only scope. The review’s findings on AI-driven personalization and behavioral nudges (e.g., automated savings prompts, spending summaries) provide evidence for Odin’s budget recommendation and forecasting modules to incorporate real-time personalization rather than static templates. Additionally, the documented gap between account access and active usage (dormant accounts, low transaction diversity) reinforces Odin’s focus on engagement-centered retention mechanisms over simple onboarding, and the review’s identification of digital literacy as a barrier suggests Odin must prioritize simple UX and local language support for Filipino young professionals.

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study focuses on South Asia (India, Bangladesh, Pakistan), not the Philippines; cultural spending norms and regulatory environments differ.
- The review emphasizes retail banking (accounts, loans, insurance) rather than personal budget management systems like Odin.
- Algorithm-specific details (e.g., LSTM architecture, Isolation Forest configuration) are absent; the review discusses AI/ML only at a high level.
- Findings assume bank-integrated digital services (e.g., direct account linking), whereas Odin relies on manual transaction input.
- No evaluation of cold-start user profiles or algorithm performance for new users with no transaction history.

## Limitations

- Systematic review quality depends on included studies; regional heterogeneity may obscure country-specific nuances.
- Urban-rural infrastructure disparities (power, data costs, signal strength) are acknowledged but not quantified for each country.
- Most included studies use cross-sectional or short-term data; longitudinal user journey tracking is missing [unacknowledged].
- Gender and digital literacy gaps are identified but no meta-analysis of effect sizes is provided [unacknowledged].
- The review does not compare specific algorithmic approaches (e.g., LSTM vs. GRU, Isolation Forest vs. autoencoders) for financial tasks.
