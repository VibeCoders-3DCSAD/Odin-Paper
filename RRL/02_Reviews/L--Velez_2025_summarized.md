## TL;DR

GCash reduced cash dependency by 41% in urban and 29% in rural Philippines, but digital divides, gender gaps, and low financial literacy (34% understand security) persist.

## Problem and Motivation

No prior systematic review had synthesized mobile banking, fintech innovations, and regulatory frameworks collectively for marginalized communities in the Philippines. With 68% of adults unbanked as of 2021, digital financial inclusion is critical to bridging economic disparities. Existing studies were fragmented—covering only adoption, regulation, or literacy in isolation—leaving a gap in integrated evidence for policy and system design.

## Approach

- PRISMA-guided systematic review of 1,296 records from Google Scholar (n=994) and ProQuest (n=302).
- Final inclusion: 26 studies (2014–2024) — 16 peer-reviewed articles, 6 policy papers, 3 institutional reports, 1 thesis.
- Focus: mobile banking, fintech innovations, and regulatory frameworks for marginalized communities in the Philippines.
- Synthesis methods: narrative synthesis and thematic analysis.
- Excluded studies lacking digital components or Philippines-specific analysis, plus opinion pieces and conference abstracts without full papers.

## Findings

1. Mobile banking adoption surged **18–35% post-2019**, driven by GCash and pandemic digitization.
2. **Urban cash dependency dropped 41%**; rural dropped 29% — a 12-point gap persists.
3. Women-owned MSMEs represent **only 22% of fintech borrowers** despite 39% of entrepreneurs.
4. Only **34% of low-income users** understood digital payment security features.
5. Rural adoption rates are **1.8 times lower** than urban due to infrastructure and connectivity gaps.

- Fintech lenders increased loan approval rates for unbanked MSMEs by 27% using alternative data.
- The National Retail Payment System drove a 19% increase in transaction volumes but implementation remains fragmented in rural areas.
- Households receiving remittances are 20% more likely to maintain active bank accounts.

**Limits of relevance:**

- The review covers all Filipino adults, not specifically young professionals (20–40); spending patterns may differ for Odin’s target age bracket.
- No evaluation of algorithm performance or ML models; relevance is contextual (user behavior, trust, and infrastructure), not algorithmic.
- Data on GCash and Maya reflects commercial fintech; Odin is a budgeting tool, not a payment platform, so adoption figures inform only user readiness, not direct feature equivalence.
- The 34% security literacy figure is from low-income users generally, not specifically Odin’s college-educated young professional segment.

## Limitations

- Dominance of cross-sectional designs (80% of reviewed studies) limits causal claims about long-term financial inclusion effects.
- Only 4 of 11 studies advocating financial literacy programs provided empirical evidence of effectiveness — a policy-evidence gap. [unacknowledged]
- No included study specifically examines young professionals (20–40) as a distinct segment; age-disaggregated data is absent.
- Rural-urban comparisons are available, but intra-regional differences within Mindanao and Visayas are not synthesized. [unacknowledged]
- Regulatory analysis focuses on NRPS but does not assess enforcement or compliance variation across local government units.
