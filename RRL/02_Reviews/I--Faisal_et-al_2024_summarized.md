## TL;DR

Convenience (24/7 access, ease of use) is the top driver of digital banking adoption, followed by security and AI‑driven personalization, according to 112 studies.

## Problem and Motivation

Banks lack a synthesized understanding of which digital features most influence customer selection of online and mobile platforms. Without this knowledge, institutions cannot effectively compete in a rapidly digitizing market. Prior research examined isolated factors but did not integrate findings across convenience, security, personalization, and crisis resilience.

## Approach

- Systematic literature review following PRISMA guidelines.
- Database search: Scopus, Web of Science, ProQuest, Google Scholar.
- Initial yield: 3,284 articles; after screening, 112 peer‑reviewed studies (2012‑2023) included.
- Inclusion criteria: empirical studies on digital banking adoption, customer preferences, or technological advancements.
- Thematic synthesis of findings across convenience, security, personalization, competition, and pandemic impact.

## Findings

1. **47 studies (1,200+ citations)** identify user convenience — 24/7 availability, easy navigation, fast transactions — as the primary adoption driver.
2. 42 studies (970 citations) show robust security (encryption, MFA, fraud detection) and transparent data policies are critical for trust.
3. 36 studies (880 citations) find that AI‑driven personalization and tailored interfaces significantly boost satisfaction and retention.
4. 29 studies (760 citations) indicate competitive pressure pushes banks to adopt blockchain, AI, and biometrics.
5. 31 studies (800 citations) demonstrate the COVID‑19 pandemic accelerated digital adoption and forced rapid infrastructure upgrades.

- Younger, tech‑savvy users show strongest preference for personalized features (36 studies).
- Banks that communicate security measures effectively achieve higher retention.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Digital banking | Online and mobile platforms for banking services (transfers, loans, payments) without physical branches. |
| PRISMA | A structured guideline for conducting systematic literature reviews (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta‑Analyses). |
| TAM | Technology Acceptance Model [think: two beliefs — usefulness and ease of use — predict adoption]. |
| MFA | Multi‑factor authentication — requiring two or more verification methods (password + fingerprint) to log in. |
| AI | Artificial intelligence — machines simulating human decision‑making, used here for chatbots and personalization. |
| Blockchain | A decentralized digital ledger that records transactions securely, reducing fraud risk. |

## Critical Citations

- [Davis, 1989] — Foundational TAM (perceived usefulness/ease of use) underpins most digital adoption studies.
- [Venkatesh et al., 2003] — Unified Theory of Acceptance (UTAUT) extended TAM to include trust and security.
- [Zhou, 2012] — Demonstrated that initial trust in mobile banking is shaped by privacy and security perceptions.

**Limits of relevance:**

- The review covers banking, not personal budget management systems; feature priorities (e.g., loan applications) may differ for a pure budgeting app.
- All studies are from international contexts; Filipino cultural and income‑level preferences are not specifically addressed.

- Personalization findings rely on AI and bank‑level analytics; Odin’s minimal user input design may limit the depth of personalization possible.
