## TL;DR

Ethnic minority young adults in Hong Kong save roughly one-third of income, use digital budgeting tools, and struggle with self-control and high living costs.

## Problem and Motivation

Financial decision-making among racial/ethnic minority young adults is underexamined, particularly day-to-day behaviors like budgeting and spending rather than just banking and credit. Understanding these decisions matters because ethnic minorities face double vulnerability from low financial literacy and young adulthood, yet existing research lacks qualitative depth and focuses almost exclusively on Western contexts. No prior study had explored the full range of financial decisions (budgeting, spending, planning, products, debt, fraud detection) among ethnic minority young adults in a non-Western setting like Hong Kong.

## Approach

- 53 semi-structured interviews with Pakistani, Indian, Nepalese, and Filipino young adults aged 18–29 in Hong Kong
- Participants included 32 employed and 21 students; 30 male, 23 female; most had at least a bachelor's degree
- Explored six domains: personal budgeting, spending, financial planning, use of financial products, debt management, and fraud detection
- Qualitative thematic analysis identified practices, strategies, perceptions, and enabling factors
- No quantitative modeling or hypothesis testing — purely exploratory qualitative design

## Findings

1. Over half of participants saved **at least 30% (roughly one-third)** of monthly income, using tactics like separate bank accounts, stashing cash, or parental control.
2. One-third of participants owned credit cards; most paid on time, but some paid only minimum due due to ignorance of interest charges.

- Many used digital budgeting apps (Mobills, Spendee, Excel) and mental bucketing to track spending.
- Spending prioritized basic needs: 50% went to food, 20% to insurance, remainder to transport and tuition.
- Spending strategies included deferred purchases, bargain hunting, and lump-sum payments; expensive items (HK$5,000+) bought on installment.
- Informal borrowing from family/friends was common; some used government student loans or high-interest lending companies.
- Fraud victimization occurred via charity scams, fake medical tests, online gaming, and investment fraud.

- [Thaler, 1985] — Introduces mental accounting theory, which the paper uses to explain suboptimal financial decisions (e.g., credit card overspending).
- [Danes, 1994] — Financial socialization theory showing parents as key agents; this paper extends it to ethnic minority young adults.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper directly informs Odin's understanding of Filipino young adults' financial behavior, even though the study was conducted in Hong Kong. The finding that many participants saved one-third of income and prioritized food and transportation aligns with Philippine FIES data patterns. The identified barriers to budgeting — time consumption, stress, self-control struggles — justify Odin's design emphasis on reducing entry friction and providing automated tracking. The paper's documentation of digital tool usage (budgeting apps) and parental monitoring strategies supports Odin's assumption that Filipino young professionals may value both automation and social accountability features.

**Directly justifies:**

- "Ethnic minority young adults (including Filipinos) in Hong Kong save approximately 30% of monthly income, with food and transportation consuming the largest budget share."
- "Budgeting is perceived as tedious, time-consuming, and stress-inducing, leading many to abandon tracking — a friction point Odin's automated categorization must address."
- "Digital budgeting apps (Mobills, Spendee, Excel) are actively used and accepted by young adults, supporting Odin's mobile-first approach."
- "Informal borrowing from family is common and not always repaid, suggesting that debt management features in Odin should distinguish informal loans from formal credit."
- "Fraud victimization occurs despite protective tactics, highlighting the need for in-app scam alerts and financial literacy content within Odin."

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is ethnic minority young adults in Hong Kong, not specifically Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila — cultural context and financial systems differ.
- Findings are qualitative and descriptive, not quantitative or algorithm-focused; cannot justify algorithm selection directly, only user behavior assumptions.
- Participants were relatively well-educated (half with bachelor's degrees) and English-proficient, which may not represent lower-literacy Filipino users.
- Hong Kong's financial infrastructure (Octopus card, MPF, high living costs) differs from the Philippines; spending ratios may not transfer directly.

## Limitations

- Sample over-represents educated, English-speaking ethnic minorities; less educated or non-English speakers may have different financial behaviors. [unacknowledged]
- No ethnic Chinese comparison group, so findings cannot isolate ethnicity-specific vs. age-specific patterns.
- Small qualitative sample (n=53) limits generalizability to broader Filipino young professional population in Manila.
- Self-reported data may underreport undesirable behaviors (e.g., credit card minimum payments, fraud victimization).
- Does not evaluate any algorithmic or AI-based financial tool — purely behavioral and descriptive.
