## TL;DR

Digital wallets increase Generation Z’s spending frequency and impulsivity by reducing the psychological “pain of paying” through frictionless, gamified interfaces.

## Problem and Motivation

The convenience and immediacy of e-wallets may foster impulsive consumption and weaken financial restraint, yet few studies synthesize how digital payment features specifically drive spending behavior among Generation Z. Understanding this relationship matters because young adults are forming lifelong financial habits in an increasingly cashless environment. Prior work lacked a systematic synthesis of empirical evidence linking e‑wallet design elements to consumptive behavior in this demographic.

## Approach

- Systematic literature review following PRISMA guidelines, searching Scopus, Web of Science, ScienceDirect, ProQuest, and Google Scholar.
- Search period: January 2020 to April 2025. Keywords included “e‑wallet AND Generation Z,” “digital payments AND consumer behavior,” and “fintech AND youth spending.”
- Inclusion criteria: empirical, peer‑reviewed studies (2020 onward), participants aged 18–27, direct examination of e‑wallet use and spending behavior, English language.
- Initial yield: 186 articles. After duplicate removal and relevance screening: 42 articles. Final sample: 28 studies after full‑text content analysis.
- Thematic synthesis focusing on purchase frequency, impulse buying, promotional features, behavioral triggers, and financial awareness.

## Findings

1. The review of **28 empirical studies** found consistent positive correlation between e‑wallet usage and increased consumption among Generation Z.
2. Frequent e‑wallet users show higher daily/weekly transaction volumes, especially in food delivery, fashion, and entertainment (Yeo et al., 2021).
3. Real‑time processing, limited‑time offers, and reduced payment friction directly encourage impulsive buying (Wang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021).

- Promotional features (cashback, reward points, exclusive discounts) create a perceived “value‑saving” effect that drives unplanned spending.
- Most e‑wallet apps lack budgeting tools or spending categorization; users rely on balance visibility only, leading to post‑spending regret.
- Push notifications, gamification (streaks, loyalty tiers), and personalized offers condition repeat consumption through habit loops.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| E‑wallet | A digital application that stores payment information and enables cashless transactions via smartphone. |
| Generation Z | Individuals born between 1997 and 2012, the first generation raised entirely in a digital environment. |
| Consumptive behavior | Spending patterns characterized by frequent, often unplanned purchases driven by emotional or situational cues rather than necessity. |
| Impulse buying | An unplanned, immediate purchase made without deliberate consideration of need or budget. |
| Mental accounting | A behavioral economics concept where people treat money differently based on its source or intended use (e.g., digital money feels less “real” than cash). |
| Digital nudge | A subtle design element in an app that influences user decisions without restricting choice (e.g., push notification for a flash sale). |
| Pain of paying | The psychological discomfort experienced when parting with money; reduced in digital payments because money feels less tangible. |

## Critical Citations

- [Thaler, 1985] — Foundational mental accounting theory explaining why digital payments reduce the “pain of paying.”
- [Chatterjee & Kumar, 2020] — Demonstrated that convenience and seamless transactions increase adoption and spending among young users.
- [Liu et al., 2021] — Showed that mobile payment convenience and promotional features drive impulsive buying and shopping intensity.
- [Kim & Choi, 2023] — Introduced digital nudges in fintech and noted that current implementations favor spending over saving.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This systematic review directly informs Odin’s user profiling and budgeting modules by demonstrating that frictionless digital payments increase impulsive spending among young adults — a baseline behavioral pattern Odin must account for when classifying users and recommending budgets. The finding that most e‑wallet apps lack budgeting tools (Rahi et al., 2022) justifies Odin’s inclusion of spending summaries, category alerts, and goal‑setting features as core engagement mechanisms. Although the review focuses on e‑wallets (not budget management systems), its identification of psychological triggers (push notifications, gamification, cashback) provides a cautionary framework: Odin should avoid reinforcing spending and instead use similar engagement techniques to encourage savings and adherence.

**Directly justifies:**

- “E‑wallet usage among Generation Z shows a significant positive correlation with increased spending frequency and impulse buying, driven by reduced perceived payment friction (Yeo et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021).”
- “Most e‑wallet applications lack integrated budgeting tools or spending categorization, causing users to rely on balance visibility alone and report post‑spending regret (Rahi et al., 2022; Lestari et al., 2021).”
- “Promotional features such as cashback and limited‑time offers create a perceived ‘value‑saving’ effect that motivates unplanned purchases, even when the spending itself is unnecessary (Liu et al., 2021; Lestari et al., 2021).”
- “Push notifications and gamification elements (streaks, loyalty tiers) condition repeat spending habits through habit loops, making consumption less deliberate and more automatic over time.”
- “Digital payment systems reduce the psychological ‘pain of paying’ by decoupling consumption from the awareness of spending actual money (Thaler, 1985; Kim & Choi, 2023).”

**Limits of relevance:**

- The review synthesizes studies from multiple countries (including Indonesia and India), not specifically the Philippines; Filipino cultural spending priorities and digital payment adoption rates may differ.
- The focus is on e‑wallets as spending tools, not on personal budget management systems; findings on feature design (e.g., cashback) may not directly translate to Odin, which has no gamification or promotional mechanics.
- The reviewed studies are largely cross‑sectional and self‑reported; causality between e‑wallet use and consumptive behavior is not firmly established.
- The population is general Generation Z (ages 18–27), not specifically young professionals with stable income; Odin’s target may have different spending discipline.

## Limitations

- The review does not include a meta‑analysis; effect sizes and confidence intervals across studies are not reported. [unacknowledged]
- All included studies are from 2020–2025, which captures pandemic‑accelerated digital adoption but may overrepresent emergency‑induced behavioral changes.
- Geographic concentration is not disclosed; findings may be dominated by Asian or Western contexts, reducing applicability to the Philippines. [unacknowledged]
- The review does not assess the quality or risk of bias of the 28 included studies, limiting confidence in synthesized conclusions.
- No longitudinal studies are included; the long‑term persistence of e‑wallet‑induced spending habits remains unknown.
