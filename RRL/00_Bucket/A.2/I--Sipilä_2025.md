# The role of generative AI in software engineering education

## Abstract

This paper presents a viewpoint on the role of Generative AI (GenAI) in Software Engineering (SE) education. While GenAI can be seen as a threat to traditional teaching methods, it also provides an opportunity to modernize the curriculum and focus on higher-level SE skills. This paper argues that SE education should shift from teaching low-level coding skills to teaching system-level design, testing, and maintenance, as these are the skills that will be increasingly important in the era of GenAI.

## 1 Introduction

The field of Software Engineering (SE) is undergoing a significant transformation due to the rapid advancement of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI). Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-4, are now capable of generating high-quality code, documentation, and even test cases with minimal human intervention. This has led to concerns about the relevance of traditional SE education, which has historically focused heavily on teaching low-level coding skills.

However, rather than viewing GenAI as a threat, SE educators should see it as an opportunity to rethink the curriculum. In this viewpoint paper, I argue that the focus of SE education should shift from "how to code" to "how to build systems." This involves emphasizing skills such as requirement engineering, system design, software testing, and maintenance—areas where human expertise remains crucial.

## 3 Shifting the Educational Paradigm

To prepare students for a GenAI-augmented workforce, SE education must evolve. The following shifts are recommended:

### 3.1 From Syntax to Semantics

Students no longer need to spend months mastering the nuances of programming language syntax. Instead, they should focus on the underlying concepts of computation and how different components interact within a system.

### 3.2 Prompt Engineering as a Core Skill

Communicating effectively with AI models—often referred to as "prompt engineering"—is becoming a necessary skill for modern SE. This involves not just writing simple queries, but understanding how to provide context and constraints to the AI to produce the desired output.

### 3.3 Emphasis on Software Quality and Testing

As code generation becomes faster, the bottleneck shifts to verification. Students must become experts in software testing and quality assurance to ensure that AI-generated code is correct, secure, and maintainable.

## 4 Challenges and Ethical Considerations

The integration of GenAI in SE education is not without challenges. Academic integrity is a primary concern, as students may use GenAI to complete assignments without truly understanding the material. Furthermore, there is the risk of over-reliance on AI, which could lead to a decline in critical thinking and problem-solving skills.

## 5 Conclusion

GenAI is not a replacement for software engineers, but a powerful tool that changes how they work. SE education must adapt by moving up the abstraction level. By focusing on higher-level design, rigorous testing, and ethical AI usage, we can prepare the next generation of software engineers to thrive in an AI-driven world.
