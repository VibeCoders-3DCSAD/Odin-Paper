# Integrating Financial Management and Gamification: A Systematic Literature Review and Future Research Agenda

## TL;DR

Analysis of 53 articles reveals that gamification in personal finance enhances user motivation through elements like badges and points, though evidence connecting behavioral intention to actual financial outcomes remains sparse.

## Problem and Motivation

Poor financial behavior leads to long-term economic instability, yet existing personal financial management (PFM) tools struggle with low user engagement. While gamification is a proven motivator in education, its application and theoretical foundations in the financial domain are under-researched and fragmented.

## Approach

* Method: Systematic Literature Review (SLR) using the TCCM framework.
* Databases: Emerald Insights, IEEE Xplore, Science Direct, Taylor & Francis.
* Timeframe: 2018–2022.
* Sample: 53 articles (24 on financial behavior, 29 on gamification).
* Analysis: Synthesis of theories, contexts, characteristics, and methodologies.

## Findings

1. Research interest in financial gamification has **increased significantly** from 2018 to 2022.
2. Self-Determination Theory (14.3%) and Technology Acceptance Model (11.9%) are the dominant theoretical frameworks.
3. **Badges and points** (9.7% each) are the most frequently implemented gamification elements.
4. Only 17.2% of reviewed gamification studies focused strictly on the finance context.
5. Most studies utilize **quantitative methods** and structural equation modeling (SEM/PLS-SEM).
6. Key identified outcomes include financial well-being, sustained usage, and user behavioral change.

**Limits of relevance:**

## Limitations

* Search limited to four specific online databases.
* Exclusion of non-English and unpublished "grey" literature.
* **Predominance of cross-sectional data**, limiting insights into long-term behavioral changes.
* Potential publication bias toward studies reporting positive gamification effects.
