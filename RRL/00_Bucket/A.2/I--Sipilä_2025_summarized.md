# Executive Summary

This paper presents a strategic viewpoint on the evolution of Software Engineering (SE) education in response to the rise of Generative AI (GenAI). The author argues that while GenAI automates low-level coding, it necessitates a shift toward high-level system design, verification, and human-centric skills.

# Key Research Questions

* How does the proficiency of Large Language Models (LLMs) in generating code affect the relevance of traditional SE curricula?
* What specific SE phases are most impacted by GenAI, and how does the human role evolve within them?
* What core competencies must be integrated into modern SE education to maintain academic integrity and professional relevance?

# Key Findings and Results

* **Automation Hotspots:** GenAI has the highest impact on the **Implementation** (coding) and **Testing** (test case generation) phases.
* **Human-Centric Necessity:** Despite AI automation, human expertise remains critical for **validation**, **negotiation of requirements**, and **high-level architectural decisions**.
* **Pedagogical Shifts:** SE education must transition from teaching syntax-heavy programming to prioritizing:
* **Semantics and System Design:** Understanding how components interact rather than just how they are written.
* **Prompt Engineering:** Learning to provide context and constraints to AI models effectively.
* **Quality Assurance:** Shifting the human effort from writing code to verifying AI-generated output for security and maintainability.



# Conclusions and Implications

The author concludes that GenAI is a collaborative tool rather than a total replacement for engineers. The primary implication for educators is the urgent need to move "up the abstraction level". Failing to adapt risks producing graduates with obsolete skills or an over-reliance on AI that degrades critical thinking and problem-solving capabilities.
