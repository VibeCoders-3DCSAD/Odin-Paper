## TL;DR

Financial literacy and fintech synergistically improve Gen Z’s saving behavior, with literacy as foundation and fintech as facilitator.

## Problem and Motivation

Gen Z possesses strong digital skills but low financial literacy, creating a paradox that undermines healthy saving habits. Prior research treats financial literacy and fintech adoption separately, missing their complementary relationship. Understanding how both interact is critical to harnessing Indonesia’s demographic dividend through inclusive digital finance.

## Approach

- Systematic Literature Review (SLR) of 250 articles from Scopus, Emerald, Elsevier, MDPI, ProQuest, and Taylor & Francis (2020–2025).
- Keywords: “financial literacy,” “digital savings,” “saving behavior,” “Gen Z,” “fintech,” and related terms.
- Screening via title/abstract, full-text, eligibility, and duplicates removal → final 6 high‑quality peer‑reviewed studies.
- Analysis focused on patterns, research gaps, and theoretical synergy between literacy and fintech.

## Findings

- **Synergy between literacy and fintech strengthens sustainable saving behavior.**
- Fintech features (auto‑reminders, balance transparency, gamification) directly increase saving motivation and discipline.
- Approximately **72%** of Gen Z’s financial literacy variation is explained by fintech usage (Hastuty Hs et al., 2024).
- Digital financial literacy reduces reliance on high‑cost loans and improves long‑term financial resilience.
- Fintech acts as an educational tool, not just a transaction channel.

## Relevance to Odin

**Topics:**

A.1 – Spending and Budgeting Behavior of Filipino Young Professionals

**Contribution to Odin:**

This SLR provides rigorous evidence that financial literacy is the cognitive foundation of healthy saving behavior among Gen Z in a Southeast Asian digital economy. It demonstrates that fintech features (gamification, transparency, reminders) directly foster saving discipline – a finding that justifies embedding educational and motivational elements into Odin’s budgeting interface. The paper also quantifies how much of financial literacy (72%) can be attributed to fintech usage, supporting Odin’s design decision to use the app itself as a literacy tool.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Financial literacy is a stronger predictor of healthy saving behavior than digital proficiency alone.”
- “Gamification and automatic reminders in fintech apps increase saving motivation and reduce impulse spending.”
- “The synergy between financial education and fintech features produces more sustainable financial habits than either factor alone.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- Geographic focus is Indonesia, not the Philippines, though both are emerging Southeast Asian economies with similar fintech adoption trends.
- The paper does not examine specific budgeting strategies (e.g., 50/30/20) or algorithmic budget allocation.
- All findings are qualitative syntheses; no comparative performance metrics for specific algorithms.

## Limitations

- Only 6 studies met strict inclusion criteria, limiting generalizability.
- Heavy reliance on Indonesian and international literature; no Philippines‑specific data.
- All reviewed studies are observational or survey‑based – no controlled experiments.
- Does not quantify the magnitude of savings increase attributable to fintech alone.
