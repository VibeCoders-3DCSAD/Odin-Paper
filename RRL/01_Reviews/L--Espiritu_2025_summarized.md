## TL;DR

Financial knowledge, attitudes, and practices are strongly correlated (R=0.697) and vary significantly by age, sex, and year level among Filipino business students.

## Problem and Motivation

Financial literacy among Filipinos is critically low (only 25% of adults are literate), yet business administration students – future financial decision‑makers – have not been systematically assessed. Without understanding their specific knowledge gaps, attitudes, and behaviors, educators cannot design effective financial literacy interventions. Existing studies focus on general college populations or other countries, leaving a gap for urban Philippine business students.

## Approach

- Survey of 2,313 business administration students at a college in Novaliches, Quezon City.
- Structured 4‑part questionnaire: Demographic profile, financial knowledge, financial attitudes, financial practices.
- Covered five domains: income, expenses, debt, credit, and savings.
- Reliability: overall Cronbach’s α = 0.916 (knowledge α = 0.459, attitude α = 0.172, practices α = 0.158 – note low sub‑scale reliability).
- Descriptive statistics and inferential tests (p‑values for demographic differences).

## Findings

1. Students generally **agree** on financial knowledge (means 2.98–3.13 out of 4) and attitudes (means 3.03–3.12).
2. **Significant differences** in knowledge by age (p=0.013), sex (p=0.020), and year level (p=0.000), but not by monthly income (p=0.092).
3. Attitudes differ significantly by age (p=0.049), monthly income (p=0.038), and year level (p=0.000), but not by sex.
4. Practices differ significantly by **all** demographic variables (age, sex, monthly income, year level; all p=0.000).
5. A **strong positive relationship** exists among knowledge, attitudes, and practices (R=0.697, p=0.000).

This paper provides empirical evidence that financial literacy – specifically knowledge, attitudes, and practices related to income, expenses, debt, credit, and savings – varies systematically by age, sex, and year level among Filipino business students (proxy for young professionals). It confirms that knowledge, attitudes, and practices are strongly interrelated (R=0.697), justifying Odin’s integrated approach of combining user profiling, adaptive budgeting recommendations, and behavioral nudges. The findings also highlight the need for targeted financial education, which Odin can partially address through personalized, real‑time feedback and spending forecasts.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Financial literacy levels among Filipino young professionals differ significantly by demographic factors such as age, sex, and year level, supporting the need for personalized budgeting systems.”
- “A strong positive relationship exists between financial knowledge, attitudes, and practices, implying that interventions improving one dimension may enhance the others.”
- “Business administration students – a key segment of future Filipino professionals – demonstrate only moderate financial practices (mean = 3.00–3.13), indicating room for automated budgeting tools.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- Sample is university students, not actively working young professionals with independent income streams.
- Study is descriptive and correlational; it does not test any budgeting algorithm, forecasting model, or anomaly detection system.
- Self‑reported attitudes and practices may not reflect actual financial transaction behaviors.
- Conducted at a single urban college; may not generalize to rural or lower‑income Filipino populations.

## Limitations

- Low internal consistency for sub‑scales (knowledge α = 0.459, attitude α = 0.172) suggests measurement issues for those constructs.
- Cross‑sectional design cannot establish causality between knowledge, attitudes, and practices.
- Purposive sampling (only business administration majors) limits generalizability to other disciplines.
- Relies entirely on self‑reported data, which may be subject to social desirability bias.
