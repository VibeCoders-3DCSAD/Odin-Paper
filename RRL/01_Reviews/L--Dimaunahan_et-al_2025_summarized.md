## TL;DR

Low financial literacy among Filipino millennials does not directly affect financial planning; instead, monthly expenses, income, occupation, and goal-setting factors drive planning behavior.

## Problem and Motivation

Filipinos have very low financial literacy (only 25% of adults), yet no recent studies examine how financial literacy affects financial planning specifically for millennials — the largest working-age demographic. Understanding these relationships is critical for designing effective financial inclusion and education policies.

## Approach

- Survey of 400 Filipino millennials (aged 28–43) in Makati City using non-probability purposive sampling.
- Measured financial literacy via Lusardi & Mitchell's "Big Three" (interest compounding, inflation, risk diversification).
- Used Goal Setting Theory (Locke & Latham, 1990) with four moderators: goal commitment, specificity, acceptance, difficulty.
- Applied Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM) to test direct effects.

## Findings

1. Financial literacy had **no significant direct effect** on financial planning (β = 0.077, p = 0.153).
2. Monthly expenses showed the strongest effect (β = 0.205, p = 0.001), followed by monthly income (β = 0.180, p = 0.003) and occupation (β = 0.164, p = 0.004).
3. Among goal-setting moderators, goal acceptance had the largest effect (β = 0.924, p < 0.001), then goal commitment (β = 0.809), goal specificity (β = 0.597), and goal difficulty (β = 0.212).
4. Only 54.5% answered interest compounding correctly; inflation 38.8%; risk diversification 33.8%.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper provides empirical evidence that Filipino millennials' financial planning is driven not by financial literacy but by demographic factors (income, expenses, occupation) and goal-setting dimensions (commitment, specificity, acceptance, difficulty). For Odin's user profiling module, these findings justify incorporating multi-dimensional behavioral indicators — goal orientation and demographic constraints — rather than relying solely on financial literacy scores. The low literacy (mean 0–1 correct out of 3) suggests Odin's budgeting and forecasting should assume limited user financial knowledge.

**Directly justifies:**

- "Monthly expenses have a stronger influence on financial planning than income among Filipino young professionals (β = 0.205 vs. 0.180)."
- "Goal acceptance (commitment to financial goals) is the strongest predictor of financial planning behavior (β = 0.924)."
- "Financial literacy alone does not significantly affect financial planning for Filipino millennials (p = 0.153) — demographic and motivational factors dominate."
- "The 'Big Three' literacy scores for Filipino millennials are very low (interest 54.5%, inflation 38.8%, risk 33.8%), implying that financial systems must minimize literacy requirements."

**Limits of relevance:**

- Sample limited to Makati City (urban, higher-income area) — not representative of rural or lower-income Filipinos.
- Focus on millennials only (born 1981–1995) — results may not generalize to Gen Z or Gen X.
- Does not test any algorithm or system — provides behavioral inputs for profiling, not implementation.
- Goal-setting moderators were measured via self-report Likert scales, not observed behavior.

## Limitations

- Geographic scope only Makati City — cannot generalize to all Philippine regions.
- Purposive sampling may introduce selection bias (volunteers may be more financially engaged).
- Cross-sectional design — cannot establish causality, only associations.
- Low R²? Not reported but model fit acceptable (SRMR=0.075).
