# Martime Students' Financial Knowledge, Attitude, and Behavior

## TL;DR
High financial knowledge and positive attitudes do not guarantee good financial behavior among maritime students — no significant correlations found.

## Problem and Motivation
Most financial literacy studies focus on knowledge alone, ignoring how attitudes translate into actual behavior. Maritime students are overlooked despite facing unique financial challenges (large but irregular income from seafaring). Existing research cannot explain why knowledgeable students still make poor financial decisions.

## Approach
- Descriptive-correlational survey of 100 maritime students at a private Philippine university (Ozamiz City).
- Standardized 4-point Likert questionnaires measuring financial knowledge (10 items), attitude (10 items), and behavior (7 items).
- Inclusion criteria: enrolled SY 2023–2024, weekly allowance ≥ PHP 1,000, informed consent.
- Analysis: mean, standard deviation, Pearson correlation.

## Findings
1. Financial knowledge: **Very high (M = 3.74/4.00, SD = 0.14)**.
2. Financial attitude: **Very high (M = 3.71/4.00, SD = 0.10)**.
3. Financial behavior: **Very high (M = 3.85/4.00, SD = 0.17)** — the highest of the three.
4. Knowledge vs. behavior: Not significant (r = -0.46, p = 0.652).
5. Attitude vs. behavior: Not significant (r = 0.59, p = 0.558).
6. **Despite very high scores on all three dimensions, no meaningful statistical relationship exists between knowledge/attitude and actual behavior.**

## Key Figures and Tables
- Table 1: Financial knowledge (3.74) → *very high, consistent across students*.
- Table 2: Financial attitude (3.71) → *positive money mindset is strong*.
- Table 3: Financial behavior (3.85) → *self-reported habits are excellent*.
- Table 4: Knowledge-behavior correlation (r = -0.46, p = 0.652) → *not significant, negative direction is noise*.

**Contribution to Odin:**
This paper provides critical evidence that financial knowledge and positive attitudes do not reliably predict financial behavior — a finding that directly informs Odin's design philosophy. It justifies moving beyond "financial literacy education" as a primary intervention and toward **system-enforced behavior support** (budgeting tools, spending limits, savings automation). The non-significant correlations (r = -0.46 and 0.59, both p > 0.05) demonstrate that self-reported capability is a weak proxy for actual financial discipline, supporting Odin's need for **behavioral profiling based on transaction data, not user surveys**. For Filipino users with irregular income patterns (like seafarers and maritime graduates), this paper validates that user profiling cannot rely on knowledge/attitude questionnaires — classification must derive from observed spending patterns.

**Directly justifies:**
- "Self-reported financial knowledge does not predict actual spending behavior — systems must observe transactions, not ask users."
- "Positive financial attitudes toward saving and budgeting fail to translate into consistent financial actions without structural enforcement."
- "User behavioral profiles in Filipino personal finance contexts must be constructed from historical transaction data, not literacy assessments."
- "Financial literacy alone is insufficient for behavioral change — Odin's budget recommendation and anomaly detection must compensate for the knowledge-action gap."

**Limits of relevance:**
- Population is maritime students (future OFWs), not general young professionals — income irregularity is extreme (months at sea with high pay, followed by months with zero income).
- All measures are self-reported — social desirability bias may inflate behavior scores (actual behavior could be lower).
- No actual transaction data was collected — the study purely uses survey instruments, not behavioral observation.

## Limitations
- Single-institution sample (Ozamiz City) — not generalizable to all Filipino maritime students.
- Self-reporting bias — students may overstate good financial habits.
- Weekly allowance minimum (PHP 1,000) excludes lower-income students, potentially inflating results.
- Correlational design — cannot determine causation.

## Remember This
- 🔑 **No significant relationship** — knowledge (r = -0.46) and attitude (r = 0.59) both fail to predict behavior (p > 0.05).
- 💡 **Behavior scores highest (3.85)** but still disconnected from knowledge/attitude — self-reporting is unreliable.
- 📌 **Theory of Planned Behavior** explains the gap: perceived behavioral control and social norms matter more than knowledge or attitude alone.
