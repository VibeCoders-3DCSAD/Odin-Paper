## TL;DR

Below minimum wage earners in Pagadian City supplement income via side hustles, prioritize basic needs, and use tight budgeting to manage financial stress.

## Problem and Motivation

Little is known about how below‑minimum‑wage sales personnel in the Philippines budget their limited income. Understanding these practices matters because underpaid employment limits economic activity and traps workers in financial instability. Prior work lacks qualitative, in‑depth case studies of specific budgeting behaviors in local Filipino enterprises.

## Approach

- Qualitative case study of 10 below‑minimum‑wage sales personnel in Pagadian City.
- Semi‑structured interviews with five open‑ended questions (income sources, allocations, challenges, coping, insights).
- Thematic analysis to identify recurring patterns across responses.
- Participants recruited voluntarily from local enterprises.
- Interviews recorded, transcribed, and coded.
- No quantitative metrics or statistical tests.

## Findings

1. **5 of 10** receive government financial assistance; 3 sell online; 1 sells thrift items; 1 sells side dishes — side hustles are universal.
2. Budget allocations: utilities (6), rent (3), **food (9)**, **childcare (10)** , daily allowance (2) — food and childcare are nearly mandatory.
3. Challenges: 5 struggle with multiple expenses; 3 have limited healthcare access; 2 have insufficient savings.
4. Coping mechanisms: emergency funds (3), resource utilization (4), expense tracking (4) — all report a positive outlook despite low income. Qualitative: Participants proactively prioritize essential needs and develop creative side hustles to stretch their income.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper provides qualitative evidence of real‑world budgeting behaviors among low‑income Filipino workers, directly informing the design of personalized budget recommendation systems. It shows that individuals with variable income sources (salary plus side hustles) prioritize childcare, food, and utilities, and employ tight budgeting and expense tracking as coping mechanisms. These findings justify Odin’s need to accommodate lump‑sum and irregular income patterns and to treat essential expense categories as protected in its recommendation algorithm.

**Directly justifies:**

- Filipino low‑income earners supplement wages with informal side hustles (online selling, thrift items, side dishes) — budget systems must accept multiple income streams.
- Childcare is a universal mandatory expense (10 of 10 participants), suggesting it should be a protected category in budget allocation.
- Expense tracking and emergency fund creation are common adaptive strategies — supports goal‑setting and savings recommendation features.
- Below‑minimum‑wage workers face healthcare access limitations — budget systems should flag underfunded health categories.

**Limits of relevance:**

- Small sample (n=10) from a single city (Pagadian); may not generalize to all Filipino young professionals.
- Qualitative only — no quantitative validation of budgeting effectiveness or causal claims.
- Focus on below‑minimum‑wage earners, not the higher‑income graduate demographic Odin primarily targets.

## Limitations

- Small convenience sample (n=10) limits generalizability beyond Pagadian City.
- No longitudinal data — captures budgeting practices only at a single point in time.
- Relies on self‑reported interview data, subject to recall and social desirability bias.
- Does not evaluate the effectiveness of budgeting practices on long‑term financial outcomes.

## Remember This

- 🔑 **9 of 10** allocate budget to food; all 10 to childcare — essentials dominate every decision.
- 💡 Side hustles (govt aid, online selling, thrift, side dishes) are universal — never rely on salary alone.
- 📌 Tight budgeting + expense tracking = the main coping mechanism despite almost no savings.
