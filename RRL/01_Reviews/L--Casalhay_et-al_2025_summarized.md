## TL;DR

Freelancers face severe income volatility and lack of benefits, relying on manual budgeting and emergency savings to cope.

## Problem and Motivation

Despite extensive research on gig economy growth, little is known about how freelancers actually manage their finances day‑to‑day. Understanding these behaviors is critical because freelancers lack employer‑sponsored safety nets (health insurance, retirement, paid leave) and face exclusion from traditional credit. Prior studies focused on platform dynamics or labor rights, not individual financial coping strategies.

## Approach

- Qualitative study using semi‑structured interviews with **50 freelancers** in Metro Manila.
- Purposive and snowball sampling; participants had at least six months of gig experience (writing, graphic design, virtual assistance, software development).
- Thematic analysis of transcribed interviews using NVivo.
- Coded for financial challenges, coping practices, and perceived opportunities.

## Findings

1. **Income instability** is the dominant challenge — seasonal dips, client cancellations, and short‑term projects create unpredictable cash flow.
2. Freelancers use **structured budgeting** (e.g., 50/20/30 allocation) and **manual tracking** (Google Sheets) to manage volatility.
3. **10% of each payment** is typically set aside into an emergency fund — surplus‑based saving during high‑income months is common.
4. **Lack of access to credit and insurance** forces reliance on cash and alternative tools; banks view freelancers as high‑risk.
5. Financial stress leads to **anxiety, lifestyle cuts, and delayed life plans** (e.g., moving, professional development).
6. Freelancers demand **affordable health insurance, income‑smoothing products, and government safety nets**.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Gig economy | Short‑term, task‑based work mediated by digital platforms (e.g., Upwork, Uber). |
| Freelancer | Self‑employed individual who performs predefined tasks without long‑term employment contracts. |
| Income volatility | Fluctuating earnings from month to month due to irregular client demand and project‑based work. |
| Micro‑savings | Small, frequent automatic deposits into a savings account, designed for irregular earners. |
| Income‑smoothing tools | Financial products that help even out high‑income and low‑income periods (e.g., flexible savings, advance pay). |

**Contribution to Odin:**

Provides empirical, local‑context evidence on how Filipino freelancers (a proxy for young professionals with irregular income) manage budgeting, saving, and financial volatility. The study's qualitative themes — structured allocation rules, manual tracking, surplus‑based saving, and emergency fund targeting — directly inform Odin's recommendations for budgeting strategies and user behavioral profiling. It also validates that income instability is the primary driver of reactive (not proactive) financial behaviors, which Odin's predictive and recommendation modules must accommodate.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Income volatility forces young Filipino professionals to adopt reactive budgeting strategies (e.g., week‑to‑week budgeting, manual tracking) rather than proactive long‑term planning.”  
- “Manual expense tracking using spreadsheets (e.g., Google Sheets) and structured allocation rules such as 50/20/30 or 30/70 are common among Filipino freelancers.”  
- “Emergency fund saving, typically 10% of each payment during surplus months, is the primary coping mechanism for income fluctuations.”  
- “Traditional credit scoring excludes irregular earners; financial products must use alternative income‑pattern models.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- The sample is freelancers in Metro Manila, not a general Filipino young professional population; generalizability to salaried professionals is limited.  
- Qualitative (N=50) — findings are thematic and contextual, not statistically generalizable; no controlled experiment.  
- No comparison with traditional employees; the “freelancer effect” vs. “irregular income effect” is not isolated.

## Limitations

- Qualitative design with 50 participants — small sample, not statistically generalizable.  
- Self‑reported interview data may suffer from recall bias or social desirability bias.  
- Geographic scope limited to Metro Manila; rural or other urban freelancers may differ.  
- No longitudinal tracking — coping strategies may change over time, but the study captures only a cross‑section.

## Remember This

- 🔑 **Income volatility** (seasonal dips, client cancellations) is the #1 challenge — more than low pay.  
- 💡 Freelancers save **10% of earnings** into emergency funds, often using surplus from large projects.  
- 📌 **Manual tracking (Google Sheets) and 50/20/30 rules dominate** — tools help, but they cannot solve irregular income.
