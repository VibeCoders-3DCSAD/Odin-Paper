## TL;DR

Financial education courses teach ageing Filipino domestic workers self‑responsibility and investment as a stopgap for missing state pensions and uncertain family care.

## Problem and Motivation

Aging migrant domestic workers face mandatory return to the Philippines with no state‑based social protection and unreliable kinship care. Little is known about how they plan for retirement after decades of remittances. Financial literacy programs have emerged as a privatised solution, yet their appeal and limits are understudied.

## Approach

- Ethnographic fieldwork in Singapore and Hong Kong (2018‑2022).
- Observed financial education courses run by NGOs, corporations (KPMG), co‑operatives, and grassroots groups.
- Interviews with 20+ Filipino domestic workers aged 45‑65, working 15‑35 years abroad.
- Also interviewed activists and women not participating in financial courses.

## Findings

- **Only 5% of overseas Filipinos in Singapore contributed to the Philippine state pension (SSS) before it became mandatory.**
- Women transform from “breadwinner” to “financially‑smart investor” narrative, learning to say no to kin.
- Financialisation cultivates self‑responsibility but remains entangled with kin, employers, and state loan schemes.
- Alternatives exist: political activism (calling for state accountability), land ownership, faith‑based resignation, and informal lending (*paluwagan*).

## Limitations

- Ethnographic sample limited to two city‑states; findings may not transfer to other migrant destinations.
- No longitudinal follow‑up on whether financial courses actually improve retirement outcomes.
- Singapore’s ban on public protests means activist narratives are under‑represented there.
- Relies on self‑reported narratives; actual financial behaviour could differ.

## Remember This

- 🔑 **5%** of overseas Filipinos paid into the state pension — most had nothing saved for retirement.
- 💡 Financial education shifts responsibility from state/kin to the individual, but doesn’t replace either.
- 📌 Alternatives include land, faith, and activism — financialisation is not universal.
