## TL;DR

Micro-entrepreneurs show high budgeting discipline (AWM 3.48–3.63) but face major challenges from debt and income volatility.

## Problem and Motivation

Micro-entrepreneurs in rural Philippine public markets lack structured financial management, making them vulnerable to economic shocks. Existing studies focus on broader MSMEs or urban vendors, leaving a gap for localized market contexts. Understanding their specific budgeting practices is essential for designing effective support systems.

## Approach

- Quantitative descriptive design with 68 randomly sampled micro-entrepreneurs in Maria Aurora Public Market.
- Structured survey using Likert scale (1–4) measuring four budgeting dimensions: income utilization, expense tracking, savings, financial planning.
- Two challenge dimensions measured: financial liability and income generation.
- Data analyzed using weighted means and frequency counts; strategic plan developed from low-ranking items.

## Findings

1. Income utilization AWM = **3.63** (Always) – strongest practice is separating business from personal funds (3.77).
2. Expense tracking AWM = 3.48 (Always) – but categorizing fixed vs variable costs ranks lowest (3.20, Often).
3. Savings AWM = 3.49 (Always) – using savings for reinvestment/emergencies top (3.64), bank deposits lowest (3.22).
4. Financial planning AWM = 3.57 (Always) – goal setting highest (3.77), sales forecasting lowest (3.42).
5. Financial liability is a Major Challenge (AWM 3.27) – managing multiple obligations ranks highest (3.42).
6. Income generation is a Major Challenge (AWM 3.40) – economic changes (inflation, fuel) top (3.48).

## Key Figures and Tables

- Table 1 (Income Utilization) → Highest AWM 3.77 for proper income allocation, showing strong fund separation discipline.
- Table 5 (Financial Liability) → AWM 3.27, with “managing multiple obligations” at 3.42 – the most critical hurdle.
- Table 7 (Strategic Plan) → Five interventions including cost classification, banking formalization, and “Salary Method”.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper provides empirical evidence on how Filipino micro‑entrepreneurs actually budget – high discipline in fund separation, reinvestment, and savings, but weak cost categorization and low formal banking usage. It validates that cultural obligations (household needs prioritized over business reinvestment) shape spending behavior. The identified gaps (e.g., no distinction between fixed/variable costs) directly inform Odin’s design requirements for simplified, education‑first budgeting features targeted at Filipino users.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Filipino micro‑entrepreneurs consistently separate business and personal funds, demonstrating a cultural norm of financial discipline (AWM 3.77).”
- “Expense categorization into fixed and variable costs is the weakest budgeting skill among Filipino public market vendors (AWM 3.20).”
- “Financial liability – especially managing multiple obligations (AWM 3.42) – is a major barrier to stable budgeting for Filipino micro‑entrepreneurs.”
- “Income volatility from economic changes (inflation, fuel prices) is the top income‑generation challenge (AWM 3.48).”

**Limits of relevance:**

- Sample is micro‑entrepreneurs in a public market, not general young professionals – but behavioral patterns may transfer.
- No testing of digital budgeting tools or algorithms – only manual practices.
- Geographic scope is a single municipality (Maria Aurora), limiting generalizability across all Philippine regions.

## Limitations

- Self‑reported survey data may overstate actual budgeting discipline (social desirability bias).
- Small sample (n=68) from one public market limits external validity.
- No qualitative component to explain *why* entrepreneurs avoid formal banking.
- Cross‑sectional design cannot track how budgeting practices change over time.

## Remember This

- 🔑 **Income utilization AWM = 3.63** – fund separation is the strongest practiced behavior.
