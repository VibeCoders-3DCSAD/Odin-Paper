## TL;DR

Filipino public teachers have high financial literacy but remain "financially coping," with emergency savings as the weakest area despite strong bill payment discipline.

## Problem and Motivation

Public school teachers face financial insecurity due to low wages, rising costs, and limited planning support. Prior studies showed good financial management but poor attitudes; no localized research examined both literacy and health in urban Quezon City districts.

## Approach

- Survey of 241 junior high teachers across 5 schools in District VI, Quezon City.
- Validated questionnaires: Financial Health Network (2024) and Surendar & Subramanya Sarma (2018).
- Measured financial knowledge, behavior, attitude, budgeting, tax, liquidity, insurance, investment, retirement.
- Non-parametric tests (Mann-Whitney U, Kruskal-Wallis H) for demographic comparisons.

## Findings

1. Overall financial literacy: **mean 4.02 / 5** (High). Highest subscore: financial knowledge (4.24). Lowest: retirement planning (3.75).
2. Financial health categorized as **"Financially Coping"** (mean 61.2 / 100). Spending: 63.17, Saving: 51.87, Borrowing: 72.40, Planning: 60.22.
3. Emergency fund item scored **3.72 / 5** — despite understanding liquidity importance (4.39), few have 6 months' expenses saved.
4. **Significant differences** in literacy by sex, age, number of children, income, education, position, experience, specialization.
5. Teachers have **no surplus income** at month end (spending item: 39.00 — "Financially Vulnerable").

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper provides the only empirically grounded profile of Filipino public school teachers' actual spending, saving, borrowing, and planning behaviors. It quantifies the gap between high financial literacy and poor financial health — a pattern that directly informs Odin's profiling and behavior classification design. The finding that teachers understand budgeting principles yet fail to maintain emergency funds or surplus income justifies the need for automated, low-friction nudges rather than education-only interventions.

**Directly justifies:**

- Filipino young professionals exhibit high declarative financial knowledge but low financial resilience — a profile dimension Odin must capture.
- Emergency savings adequacy (6 months' expenses) is the single weakest indicator even among financially literate working Filipinos.
- Bill payment history alone is a poor proxy for financial health; monthly surplus and long-term saving capacity are distinct, lower-performing dimensions.
- Borrowing for large purchases is common despite understanding of debt costs — argues for protected categories and overage detection on essential vs. discretionary spending.

**Limits of relevance:**

- Sample is public school teachers (age 41+, married, low-middle income) — not directly young professionals aged 22–35, though behavioral patterns may generalize.
- Study is descriptive; no intervention or algorithm tested — informs profiling requirements, not implementation.
- Geographic scope is Quezon City only — urban Metro Manila context may not represent all Filipino young professionals.

## Limitations

- Self-reported data may overstate financial literacy due to social desirability bias.
- Cross-sectional design — cannot measure change over time or causal relationships.
- Excludes substitute and administrative staff — limits generalizability within education sector.
- No qualitative component — reasons behind behavior (e.g., why emergency savings are low) remain unexplored.
