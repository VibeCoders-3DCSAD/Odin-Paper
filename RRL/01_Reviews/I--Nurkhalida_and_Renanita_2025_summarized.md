## TL;DR

Financial well‑being fully mediates the effect of financial behavior on stress among irregular income workers.

## Problem and Motivation

Irregular income workers face higher stress due to financial instability, yet prior research has not examined how financial behavior and financial well‑being jointly affect their stress levels. Understanding this relationship is critical for designing interventions that protect mental health in vulnerable populations. The paper fills this gap by testing whether financial well‑being mediates the behavior‑stress link.

## Approach

- Sample: 266 Indonesian irregular‑income workers aged 20–40 years (purposive sampling).
- Instruments: DASS‑21 (stress), Financial Management Behavior Scale (Dew & Xiao, 2011), Financial Well‑being Scale (Prawitz et al., 2006).
- Procedure: online survey after instrument adaptation and validation (CFA).
- Analysis: simple mediation using Hayes’ PROCESS macro (IBM SPSS 23).
- Monte Carlo simulation confirmed sample size gives statistical power ~0.80.

## Findings

1. Financial behavior positively predicts financial well‑being (β = 0.6566, p < .001).
2. Financial well‑being negatively predicts stress (β = –0.6761, p < .001).
3. The direct path from financial behavior to stress is **non‑significant** (β = –0.1039, p = .0564).
4. Indirect effect via financial well‑being is –0.5045 (95% CI [–0.6070, –0.4119]) — **full mediation**.
5. Financial behavior alone explains 30.01% of stress variance; adding well‑being raises that to 56.01%.

## Key Figures and Tables

- Table 8: Path coefficients → direct effect of behavior on stress is non‑significant (p = .0564); indirect via well‑being is significant.

## Relevance to Odin

**Topics:**

A.1 Spending and Budgeting Behavior of Filipino Young Professionals

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper provides empirical evidence that income irregularity shapes the relationship between financial behavior and psychological outcomes, with financial well‑being as the critical mediator. For Odin’s RRL, it supports the claim that irregular‑income users (common among young Filipino professionals with freelance or commission‑based work) cannot be modeled with the same behavior‑to‑stress direct path as fixed‑income users. It also justifies the need to measure and improve financial well‑being as an intermediate outcome rather than targeting stress reduction solely through financial literacy or budgeting tools.

**Directly justifies:**

- “Among irregular‑income workers, financial behavior does not directly reduce stress; instead, it operates through improvements in financial well‑being.”
- “Income typology (irregular vs. fixed) moderates the mediation pathway, making financial well‑being a necessary intermediate construct.”
- “Financial well‑being explains 56% of stress variance, underscoring its importance as both a design goal and an evaluation metric for personal finance systems.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- Sample is Indonesian, not Filipino — cultural and economic differences (e.g., remittance culture, SSS/Pag‑IBIG) may alter effect sizes.
- The study is cross‑sectional and self‑report; causal direction is assumed, not proven.
- No mobile‑first or algorithmic component — relevance is conceptual (behavioral‑psychological model), not technical.

## Limitations

- Online survey may exclude workers without internet access (sampling bias).
- Only internal variables (behavior, well‑being, stress) examined — external factors (social support, environment, socioeconomic status) omitted.
- Cross‑sectional design cannot confirm causal ordering.
‑ Instrument for financial behavior had to drop 5 items after CFA; reduced scale may lose nuance.

## Remember This

- 🔑 **Full mediation** — financial behavior only reduces stress *through* financial well‑being (direct effect p = .0564).
- 💡 **56% of stress variance** explained when well‑being is added versus 30% from behavior alone.
- 📌 **Irregular income matters** — the mediation model specifically fits workers with unpredictable earnings, not fixed‑salary populations.
