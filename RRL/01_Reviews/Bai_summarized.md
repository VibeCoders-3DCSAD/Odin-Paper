# Impact of Financial Literacy, Mental Budgeting, and Self-Control on Financial Well-Being with the Mediating Role of Investment Decision Making

## TL;DR
Financial literacy, mental budgeting, and self-control all improve financial well-being, with investment decisions partially mediating these effects.

## Problem and Motivation
Financial stress harms mental health and productivity, yet prior research examined financial literacy, mental budgeting, and self-control separately — not how they work together. Understanding their combined effect through investment decisions is needed for effective interventions.

## Approach
- Survey of 449 Chinese university students (55% male, 45% female; 60% business majors).
- PLS-SEM path analysis testing direct and mediated relationships.
- Variables: financial literacy (7 items), mental budgeting (4 items), self-control (9 items), investment decisions (9 items), financial well-being (7 items).
- Mediation tested via Baron & Kenny approach.

## Findings
1. Financial literacy has the **strongest direct effect** on financial well-being (β=0.299, p<.001).
2. Self-control directly affects financial well-being (β=0.182, p<.001).
3. Mental budgeting directly affects financial well-being (β=0.102, p<.001).
4. Investment decision making partially mediates all three relationships (indirect effects p=.034–.036).
5. All constructs met reliability thresholds (Cronbach's α: 0.71–0.82; AVE: 0.69–0.81).

**Contribution to Odin:**
This paper provides empirical evidence that mental budgeting (cognitive tracking without formal tools) directly improves financial well-being (β=0.102), and that investment/financial decision-making partially mediates the relationship between financial literacy and financial outcomes. For Odin, this justifies including mental budgeting support (e.g., automated category tracking, spend awareness features) even when users lack formal budgeting literacy. The partial mediation finding also suggests that improving users' financial decision quality (e.g., through recommendations) is a viable intervention path distinct from pure literacy education.

**Directly justifies:**
- Financial literacy has a direct, positive effect on financial well-being (β=0.299, p<.001) — knowledge matters even before behavior change.
- Mental budgeting (cognitive expense tracking) significantly improves financial outcomes (β=0.102, p<.001) — digital tools can substitute for or augment this cognitive process.
- Investment/financial decision making partially mediates the literacy→well-being relationship (indirect effect p=.034) — better decisions are a mechanism for better outcomes.
- Self-control directly predicts financial well-being (β=0.182, p<.001) and influences decision quality (β=0.152, p<.001) — apps should reduce impulsive spending friction.

**Limits of relevance:**
- Sample is Chinese university students, not Filipino young professionals (though both are emerging adults with similar financial learning curves).
- Focus on investment decisions as mediator — less relevant for basic budgeting apps; more for apps with savings/investment features.
- Cross-sectional; no causal claims about long-term behavior change.

## Limitations
- Student sample (university) limits generalizability to general population or employed adults.
- Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference despite mediation claims.
- Self-reported measures may overstate positive behaviors (social desirability).
- Chinese context may not fully generalize to Philippine financial behaviors or institutions.
