## TL;DR

Online buying behavior has a strong positive relationship with poor budget management among Filipino business students (r = 0.612).

## Problem and Motivation

Online shopping, driven by social media, instant gratification, and marketing tactics, may disrupt personal budgets — yet little is known about this effect on Business Administration students in Philippine higher education. Prior work lacked a quantitative focus on how specific online buying decisions (e.g., impulsive purchases, influencer promotions) affect budgeting practices. Understanding these patterns is essential for promoting responsible consumption and financial discipline among students with limited resources.

## Approach

- Descriptive-correlational design with 153 randomly selected Business Administration students from Bukidnon State University – Baungon Campus.
- Validated questionnaire adapted from Aiswarya and Mohan (2014) covering demographics, online buying perceptions, and budgeting practices.
- Four-point Likert scale (Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree) used for response quantification.
- Data analyzed using SPSS: descriptive statistics (frequency, mean, SD), Pearson r, and ANOVA.

## Findings

1. Strong significant relationship between online buying decisions and personal budgeting: **r = 0.612** (p < 0.05).
2. Most respondents (83%) are aged 18–24, 62% female, and 77% have monthly allowance ≤ ₱1,000.
3. Online buying decision means range 2.90–3.10 (Agree/High), with “likes and positive comments” highest at 3.10.
4. Personal budget mean is 3.15 (Agree/High); “necessary expenses” scores 3.32 (Very High).
5. Age significantly affects allowance, spending, and savings (p < 0.05).
6. Civil status significantly affects allowance and savings (p < 0.05).
7. Sex only significantly affects savings (p < 0.05).

## Key Figures and Tables

- Table 4: Pearson correlation → Online buying vs. personal budgeting, r = 0.612 (strong, significant).
- Table 5: ANOVA differences → Age and civil status drive variations in allowance, spending, and savings; sex affects only savings.

## Relevance to Odin

**Topics:**

1 - Spending and Budgeting Behavior of Filipino Young Professionals

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper provides empirical evidence that online buying decisions — driven by social media engagement, influencer promotions, and instant gratification — are strongly correlated (**r = 0.612**) with poor budget management among Filipino young adults. It confirms that age and civil status significantly affect financial behaviors, supporting the need for demographic tailoring in financial tools. The findings also highlight that students often fail to track allowance allocation, directly informing Odin’s requirement for budget tracking and alert mechanisms.

**Directly justifies:**

- Online buying decisions have a strong significant relationship (r = 0.612) with poor budget management among Filipino young adults.
- Social media engagement, “likes and positive comments,” and influencer promotions drive unplanned spending that disrupts monthly budgets.
- Age and civil status significantly affect allowance management and saving behaviors, indicating that financial interventions should be demographic‑adaptive.
- Young Filipinos with limited monthly allowances (≤ ₱1,000) remain highly susceptible to impulsive online purchases, underscoring the need for low‑friction, real‑time budget feedback.

**Limits of relevance:**

- Sample comprises Business Administration students, not young professionals with full‑time income; spending patterns may differ.
- Single campus in Bukidnon, Philippines; geographic scope limits generalization to other regions or income strata.
- Cross‑sectional survey design captures correlation, not causation; no longitudinal tracking of budgeting behavior over time.

## Limitations

- Sample restricted to one HEI and one degree program (Business Administration), reducing external validity.
- Data are self‑reported; respondents may understate impulsive spending or overstate budgeting adherence.
- No experimental manipulation — causal claims are not supported.
- Focuses on students only; findings may not apply to working professionals or older populations.
