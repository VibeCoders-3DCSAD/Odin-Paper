# Beyond Awareness: Examining Financial Behaviors Among Public School Teachers in the Philippines

## TL;DR
Filipino teachers show moderately positive financial behaviors driven by necessity, not mastery; seminars have zero measurable impact on behavior.

## Problem and Motivation
Financial literacy research focuses on knowledge, not actual behavior — creating a "knowledge-action gap." Teachers have stable jobs but constrained incomes, making them ideal for studying how structural limits shape financial practices. Prior studies ignored the psychological difficulty of routine financial tasks.

## Approach
- Mixed-methods: survey (N=335 public school teachers in Baguio) + follow-up interviews (n=9).
- Measured behavior frequency (4-point scale) and perceived difficulty across 5 domains: budgeting, saving, goals, making ends meet, choosing products.
- Compared groups by marital status (ANOVA), rank (t-test), and seminar attendance (t-test).
- Used Friedman test with Nemenyi post hoc for difficulty rankings.

## Findings
1. General behavior is **moderately positive (M=2.69)** — but this reflects *conscious constraint* (disciplined coping under scarcity), not financial security.
2. **No significant difference** between seminar attendees and non-attendees (both M=2.69, p=.991) — seminars have zero behavioral impact.
3. Single teachers outperform married teachers (p=.034); Master Teachers outperform non-Master Teachers (M=3.00 vs. 2.65, p=.002).
4. Budgeting is **most frequent but perceived as most difficult** (M=2.17 — moderately difficult).
5. Choosing financial products is perceived as **very easy (M=4.15)** despite low ownership (M=2.45) — a Dunning-Kruger effect.
6. Digital tracking tools are rarely used (income tracking M=1.95; expense tracking M=1.84).

## Definitions
| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
|----------------|--------------------------|
| Conscious constraint | Disciplined money management under structurally limited income — acting responsibly because you have to, not because you've mastered finance. |
| Knowledge-action gap | Knowing what to do financially but being unable to do it due to structural or psychological barriers. |
| OECD/INFE | International survey framework for measuring financial literacy across countries. |
| Dunning-Kruger effect (applied) | Limited exposure to complex products leads to overestimated competence — thinking it's easy because you don't know what you don't know. |

## Critical Citations

- [OECD/INFE, 2023] — Philippines scores below global average in financial literacy; behavior scores appear higher due to necessity-driven practices.

**Contribution to Odin:**
This paper provides direct empirical evidence from the Philippine context showing that financial behavior is shaped more by structural constraints (income level, household size, marital status, rank) than by financial knowledge or seminar attendance. It introduces "conscious constraint" — a behavioral pattern where users perform responsible budgeting and bill payment not from mastery but from survival necessity. This has direct implications for Odin's profiling logic: high behavioral scores should not be interpreted as financial health without assessing discretionary income and household burden. The paper also empirically demonstrates that one-off financial education has no measurable impact, supporting Odin's focus on automated nudges and just-in-time interventions over informational content.

**Directly justifies:**
- Filipino professionals with stable incomes may still exhibit "conscious constraint" — responsible budgeting driven by necessity, not financial mastery.
- Marital status and household size significantly affect financial behavior, with married individuals facing greater constraints (p=.034).
- Traditional financial literacy seminars have **zero measurable effect** on actual financial behavior (M=2.69 both groups, p=.991).
- Digital tracking tool adoption is very low among Filipino professionals (M=1.84–1.95), suggesting preference for low-friction, automated solutions.
- Budgeting is perceived as the most difficult financial task despite being most frequently performed — friction-reduction tools are needed.

**Limits of relevance:**
- Sample is public school teachers (Baguio City), not young professionals (though both are salaried employees with constrained disposable income).
- Focus on financial behavior frequency, not spending composition or category-level analysis.
- No evaluation of app-based or digital PFMS — only general tracking tool usage.

## Limitations
- Cross-sectional design; no causal claims about income changes over time.
- Single city (Baguio) — may not generalize to other Philippine regions or rural areas.
- Self-reported behavior may overestimate positive practices (social desirability bias).
- No direct measure of actual disposable income or debt-to-income ratios.

## Remember This
- 🔑 **Seminars don't work** — attendees and non-attend scored identical 2.69 (p=.991).
- 💡 **Budgeting is hardest** (2.17) despite being most frequent — friction, not skill, is the problem.
- 📌 **Single ≠ married** — single teachers outperform married (p=.034); demographics shape behavior more than knowledge.
