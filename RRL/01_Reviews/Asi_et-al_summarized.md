# Peer Influence and Adolescent Spending: Insights into High School Students' Buying Behavior

## TL;DR
Implicit peer pressure has a weak positive correlation (r=0.273) with impulsive buying among Filipino senior high school students.

## Problem and Motivation
Limited research directly links implicit peer pressure to impulsive buying among Filipino senior high school students. Impulsive spending harms financial stability, and peer influence is often assumed strong but untested in this context. No prior study quantified the relationship for Philippine adolescents.

## Approach
- Surveyed 108 students at one faith‑based school in Cavite, Philippines.
- Used adapted implicit peer pressure scale (Chang et al., 2019) and impulsive buying scale (Kim, 2003; Borromeo et al., 2022).
- Descriptive‑correlational design analyzed with Jamovi.
- Pearson correlation and linear regression tested the relationship.

## Findings
1. Implicit peer pressure mean = 2.24 (low, scale 1‑5).
2. Impulsive buying mean = 3.06 (moderate).
3. Pearson correlation **r = 0.273** (p = 0.004) – weak positive relationship.
4. Regression R² = 0.0745 – implicit peer pressure explains only **7.45%** of impulsive buying variance.

## Key Figures and Tables
- Table 1: Level of implicit peer pressure → highest item “spend more with friends” (3.39), lowest “need to spend to maintain status” (1.58).
- Table 2: Extent of impulsive buying → highest “struggle to resist good deal” (3.43) and “regret impulse purchases” (3.42).
- Table 3: Correlation matrix → r=0.273, significant at p<0.01.

## Definitions
| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
|----------------|--------------------------|
| Implicit peer pressure | Pressure to conform without direct request – fear of being left out [think: silent social expectation] |
| Impulsive buying | Unplanned, spur‑of‑the‑moment purchase with no pre‑planning |
| R² (coefficient of determination) | Proportion of variance in impulsive buying explained by peer pressure – 7.45% here |

**Contribution to Odin:**
This paper provides empirical evidence that implicit peer pressure is a weak but statistically significant predictor of impulsive spending among Filipino adolescents. For Odin’s user behavioral profiling (Topic 9), it suggests that peer influence should be included as a behavioral dimension but not over‑weighted relative to self‑control or financial literacy. For spending behavior (Topic 1), it quantifies that social conformity explains only a small fraction of variance, implying other factors (e.g., marketing, personal discipline) dominate.

**Directly justifies:**
- Peer pressure among Filipino youth explains only 7.45% of the variance in impulsive buying behavior.
- Implicit peer pressure has a weak positive correlation (r=0.273) with impulsive spending in a Philippine high school sample.
- Financial literacy and self‑control may outweigh peer influence in determining adolescent spending behavior.
- Surveys adapted for Filipino adolescents can measure implicit peer pressure and impulsive buying reliably.

**Limits of relevance:**
- Sample is senior high school students (ages 16‑18), not young professionals (22‑35) – different income and autonomy levels.
- Single faith‑based institution in Cavite – may not generalize to all Filipino contexts.
- Cross‑sectional design – cannot infer causality; other unmeasured variables likely dominate.

## Limitations
- Small sample (n=108) from one school limits generalizability.
- Self‑reported surveys may introduce social desirability bias.
- Only implicit peer pressure examined; explicit peer pressure may differ.
- No control for income, allowance size, or access to digital payments.

## Remember This
- 🔑 **r=0.273** – weak link, but statistically significant (p=0.004).
- 📉 Only **7.45%** of impulsive buying variance is explained by peer pressure.
- 🧠 Other factors (self‑control, literacy, marketing) likely matter more – don’t overfocus on peers.
