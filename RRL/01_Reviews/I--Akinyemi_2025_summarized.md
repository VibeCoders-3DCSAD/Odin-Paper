## TL;DR

Personalized financial coaching increases savings by 30-35% and reduces high-interest debt by 20-25% among low-income households within 12 months.

## Problem and Motivation

Wealth inequality persists in the US and Europe, with low-income households trapped by low savings and high debt. Traditional financial education provides knowledge but fails to change real-world behavior. No prior study empirically compared coaching’s impact across the US and Europe using mixed methods.

## Approach

- **Data**: 500 participants (New York, Smart Money Coaching) + 400 participants (Berlin, MyBudgetCoach).
- **Design**: Mixed‑methods, pre/post coaching with 12‑month follow‑up.
- **Analysis**: Regression on savings, debt, credit scores, investment participation; thematic coding of interviews (NVivo).
- **Comparison**: Market-driven (US) vs. social-welfare (Europe) financial systems.

## Findings

1. Monthly savings rose **30% in New York** ($200→$260) and **35% in Berlin** (€180→€243).
2. High‑interest debt fell **25% in NY** and **20% in Berlin**.
3. Credit scores improved by **+80 points** (NY: 600→680).
4. Investment participation doubled in NY (25%→50%) and nearly doubled in Berlin (30%→55%).
5. Berlin participants saved more (stronger savings culture); US participants reduced debt more (higher credit card use). Qualitative: Participants reported increased financial confidence and reduced reliance on predatory lending.

## Limitations

- Self‑reported financial data may introduce social desirability bias.
- 12‑month follow‑up does not capture long‑term sustainability of behavior changes.
- Urban only (New York, Berlin) — results may not generalize to rural or suburban areas.
- Coaching costs and scalability concerns not fully addressed.
