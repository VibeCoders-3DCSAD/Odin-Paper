## TL;DR

Non-migrant mothers sustain OFW family cohesion through intensive mothering and father-child mediation, but this also reproduces mediated patriarchy and financial surveillance.

## Problem and Motivation

Overseas Filipino Worker (OFW) migration disrupts family stability, but the crucial role of non-migrant mothers in maintaining cohesion remains overlooked. Existing studies focus on migrant mothers, not on wives left behind. Understanding how these mothers navigate role overload, emotional mediation, and financial adaptation is essential for supporting transnational families.

## Approach

- Qualitative descriptive phenomenology with 20 non-migrant mothers in Nueva Ecija, Philippines.
- Purposive sampling: legally married to an OFW, mother of an adolescent (10–19 years).
- Online in-depth interviews due to pandemic restrictions; thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2021).
- Homogeneity: varied remittance levels and educational attainment (12 of 20 had vocational/college education).

## Findings

1. **14 of 20 mothers are stay-at-home** and embrace intensive motherhood — sole responsibility for values and household, despite education and prior work experience.
2. Mothers act as *moral gatekeepers*: strict parenting to avoid social scrutiny (e.g., Cherry judged as “bad mother”).
3. **Mediated patriarchy** persists: fathers control finances and discipline from abroad; mothers must consult husbands on major expenses.
4. Irregular remittances (10k–30k PHP/month) force **70+%** of mothers into informal co-provider roles (farm labor, domestic work, online selling).
5. Transnational communication (daily calls via social media) strengthens emotional bonds but also enables **surveillance** — husbands monitor daily activities and childrearing.
6. Working mothers experience **maternal ambivalence** — they prioritize children’s welfare over paid work (e.g., Cherry skips farm work for school modules).

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| OFW | Overseas Filipino Worker — Filipino working abroad. |
| Transnational families | Families whose members live across national borders, maintaining ties via communication and remittances. |
| Mediated patriarchy | Fathers’ authority reproduced from afar through technology and financial control. |
| Intensive motherhood | Ideology that mothers must be solely responsible for children’s emotional and moral development. |
| Family cohesion | Emotional bonding, support, and shared values that keep a family unit intact despite separation. |
| Ambient co-presence | Constant awareness of distant family members through social media (Madianou, 2016). |

## Limitations

- Single province (Nueva Ecija) — findings may not generalize to urban or other rural regions.
- No triangulation with children or fathers — only mothers’ self-reports.
- Remote online interviews during pandemic may limit rapport, though referrals mitigated this.
- Referral sampling potentially introduces selection bias.

## Remember This

- 🔑 **14 of 20 mothers stay home** — intensive motherhood is the norm even when mothers are educated and experienced.
- 💰 Irregular remittances force **70+% into informal work** — mothers are co-providers, not passive recipients.
- 📞 Daily calls create *ambient co-presence* — but also enable financial surveillance and paternal control from abroad.
