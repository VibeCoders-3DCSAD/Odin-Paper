## TL;DR

Filipino faculty mix traditional (piggy bank, paluwagan) and modern (bank deposits, stocks) saving methods but struggle with rising costs and low financial literacy.

## Problem and Motivation

Faculty members at Sorsogon State University often resort to long‑term borrowings, leading to insolvency and diminished savings. Previous research highlighted low salaries, high debt, and lack of financial knowledge as general challenges, but no study examined the specific saving practices and obstacles of this population. Understanding these gaps is needed to design a tailored financial management model.

## Approach

- Mixed method: survey (n=40) + focus group discussions (FGDs) with faculty at SorSU Bulan Campus.
- Quantitative: profile variables (age, salary grade, income, expenses, savings percentage, saving modality).
- Qualitative: thematic analysis of FGD transcripts to identify saving practices and problems.
- Outcome: proposed Bongalonta’s Financial Model (budgeting → minimize debt → emergency fund → retirement investment).

## Findings

1. **60%** of respondents save **15%** of their income; 35% save 16‑30%.
2. Top saving methods: piggy bank (30%), time deposits (30%), followed by paluwagan, property, and stocks.
3. **82.5%** report monthly expenses above ₱12,000 (≈28.6% of income).
4. Budgeting, paluwagan, piggy bank, bank accounts, and securities are the common practices.
5. Problems identified: **rising utility costs**, unexpected daily expenses, **lack of financial literacy**, poor debt management (GSIS/Land Bank loans consume most salary), and low/delayed salaries.
6. Bongalonta’s model proposes four steps: create a budget, minimize debt, save for emergencies, invest in retirement funds.

**Contribution to Odin:**

This local study provides empirical data on how Filipino professionals (faculty members) actually save, spend, and manage cash flow using both traditional (piggy bank, paluwagan) and modern (bank deposits, stocks) instruments. It identifies concrete pain points – unexpected expenses, poor debt management, and lack of financial literacy – that Odin’s budgeting, anomaly detection, and financial education modules must address. The Bongalonta model (budget → minimize debt → emergency fund → retirement) directly informs the design of Odin’s budget recommendation and user onboarding flows.

**Directly justifies:**

- Filipino young professionals rely on both informal (paluwagan, piggy bank) and formal (bank, securities) saving mechanisms – Odin should integrate familiar cultural practices.
- “Increasing cost of utilities and unexpected day‑to‑day expenses” is the top problem – justifies anomaly detection for spending spikes (Topic 8/9).
- Only 60% of respondents save 15% of income; lack of financial literacy is a key barrier – Odin must include in‑app financial education.
- Low and delayed salaries cause cash‑flow uncertainty – supports Odin’s design for variable‑income budgeting and lump‑sum allocation.

**Limits of relevance:**

- Sample is limited to 40 faculty at one Philippine state university campus – may not generalize to all young professionals.
- Focuses on descriptive practices, not algorithmic evaluation – no direct ML or anomaly detection method is tested.
- Does not assess mobile‑first systems or digital PFMS – relevance is behavioral, not technical.

## Limitations

- Small sample size (n=40) and single institution limit generalizability.
- Self‑reported data (survey and FGDs) subject to recall and social desirability bias.
- Cross‑sectional design – cannot track changes in saving behavior over time.
- No validation of the proposed Bongalonta model – presented as a conceptual output only.

## Remember This

- 🔑 **60% save only 15%** – modest savings rate despite above‑average government salaries.
- 💸 Unexpected expenses and low literacy are the **top two barriers** – directly shape Odin’s feature priorities.
