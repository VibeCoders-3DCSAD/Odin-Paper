ABSTRACT

This study examines the economic survival strategies employed by micro-entrepreneurs operating

within  Philippine  tourism  enclaves  during  periods  of  economic  disruption.  Using  a  qualitative

secondary data analysis (QSDA) design situated within an interpretivist paradigm, the research

synthesizes  evidence  from  peer-reviewed  academic  literature,  government  reports,  and

institutional  studies  covering  the  period  2013  to  2024.  The  analysis  is  organized  around  four

research questions addressing the nature of economic disruptions, the survival strategies deployed

by micro-entrepreneurs, a typology of those strategies, and the policy and institutional conditions

that enable or constrain micro-enterprise survival. Findings reveal that Philippine tourism micro-

entrepreneurs face a structurally recurring and compounding pattern of disruptions  — spanning

pandemic events, natural disasters, policy interventions, and security crises — that progressively

depletes  their  adaptive  capacity  across  successive shocks. Despite severe structural constraints,

micro-entrepreneurs  deploy rich survival strategy portfolios  combining operational contraction,

livelihood diversification, social capital activation through culturally embedded mechanisms such

as  utang  na  loob,  bayanihan,  and  paluwagan,  and  selective  institutional  engagement.  A  four-

quadrant typology organized along temporal and resource-orientation axes reveals a resilience trap

in which the most vulnerable operators — informally registered, island-based, women-operated,

and  low-capitalized  —  are  structurally  confined  to  short-term  strategies  and  excluded  from

transformative  adaptation.  The  study  further  documents  a  policy  landscape  characterized  by

formalization  bias,  inter-agency  fragmentation,  and  disbursement  delays  that  systematically

exclude the most vulnerable micro-entrepreneurs from institutional support. Recommendations are

addressed  to  the  Department  of  Tourism,  Department  of  Trade  and  Industry,  DOLE,  Local

CHAPTER I: INTRODUCTION

Background of the Study

Tourism has long been recognized as a significant driver of economic growth and poverty

alleviation  in  developing  nations,  particularly  in  Southeast  Asia.  In  the  Philippines,  the  sector

contributes substantially to gross domestic product (GDP), employment generation, and foreign

exchange earnings. Yet beneath the macro-level statistics lies a more nuanced economic ecosystem

— one populated primarily by micro-entrepreneurs whose livelihoods are inextricably tied to the

ebb and flow of tourist arrivals. These actors — the sari-sari store operators, tricycle drivers, food

stall  owners,  souvenir  vendors,  and  informal  tour  guides  operating  within  tourism  enclaves  —

constitute the bedrock of community-level economic participation in destinations such as Boracay,

Palawan, Siargao, Bohol, and Batangas.

The Philippine tourism industry demonstrated robust performance in the years preceding

2020,  with  the  Department  of  Tourism  (DOT)  recording  over  8.2  million  international  visitor

arrivals in 2019, generating approximately PHP 482.15 billion in receipts (Department of Tourism

[DOT],  2020).  However,  the  onset  of  the  COVID-19  pandemic  in  early  2020  precipitated  an

unprecedented collapse of global tourism demand. International arrivals in the Philippines fell by

82% in 2020, devastating tourism-dependent communities and exposing the extreme vulnerability

of  micro-entrepreneurs  who  lacked  the  financial  buffers,  institutional  support,  and  adaptive

capacity available to larger firms (United Nations World Tourism Organization [UNWTO], 2021).

Economic  disruptions  in  tourism-dependent  economies  are  not  unprecedented.  Prior  to

COVID-19, Philippine tourism enclaves experienced disruptions from natural disasters including

Typhoon  Haiyan  (Yolanda)  in  2013,  the  Zamboanga  City  conflict  in  the  same  year,  and  the

Boracay closure ordered by President Rodrigo Duterte in 2018 for environmental rehabilitation.

Each  event  produced  cascading  effects  on  micro-entrepreneurial  activity,  yet  systematic

documentation  of  survival  strategies  employed  by  these  actors  remains  sparse  in  Philippine

academic literature (Alampay & Umezaki, 2019).

Internationally,  the  literature  on  small  and  micro-enterprise  resilience  during  economic

shocks has grown considerably. Scholars such as Wasilczuk (2000) and Doern et al. (2019) have

documented the adaptive strategies of small firms during crises, including cost reduction, product

diversification,  informal  credit  mobilization,  and  community-based  resource  pooling.  In  the

tourism  sector  specifically,  Dahles  and  Bras  (1999)  pioneered  scholarship  on  informal  tourism

entrepreneurship  in  developing  Asia,  arguing  that  micro-entrepreneurs  in  tourist  destinations

develop context-specific survival repertoires shaped by social networks, geographic position, and

institutional environments. More recent contributions by Novelli et al. (2018) and Farmaki et al.

(2020) have extended this analysis to crisis contexts, demonstrating that micro-enterprise survival

is  mediated  by  access  to  social  capital,  government  support  mechanisms,  and  the  structural

characteristics of destination economies.

In the Philippine context, research on tourism entrepreneurship has predominantly focused

on policy frameworks, destination competitiveness, and macro-economic impacts (Paguia, 2018;

Sabanal  &  Bautista,  2021).  Studies  specifically  examining  the  survival  strategies  of  micro-

entrepreneurs in tourism enclaves during periods of economic disruption remain limited, and those

that do exist tend to rely on cross-sectional survey data that capture snapshots rather than processes.

This  gap is particularly consequential given the structural characteristics  of Philippine tourism,

where  informal  and  micro-scale  economic  actors  constitute  the  majority  of  tourism-related

businesses in coastal and island destinations (Asian Development Bank [ADB], 2021).

Statement of the Problem

Despite the critical role of micro-entrepreneurs in sustaining local tourism economies, their

survival dynamics during economic disruptions remain underexplored in Philippine scholarship.

This study examines the economic survival strategies employed by micro-entrepreneurs operating

within Philippine tourism enclaves during periods of economic disruption through a qualitative

analysis  of  secondary  data  sources  including  published  research,  government  documents,  and

institutional reports. Specifically, it seeks to answer the following questions:

What economic disruptions have most significantly impacted micro-entrepreneurs

in Philippine tourism enclaves, as documented in existing literature, government records,

and institutional reports?

2.

What economic survival strategies are most frequently documented among micro-

entrepreneurs in Philippine tourism enclaves, and under what structural conditions — such

Theoretical Framework

This study is anchored on three complementary theoretical lenses that together provide a

robust  explanatory  architecture  for  understanding  micro-entrepreneurial  survival  in  tourism

enclaves during economic disruption.

The first is the Resilience Theory as articulated in entrepreneurship scholarship by Doern

et  al.  (2019)  and  further  developed  in  the  context  of  small  tourism  businesses  by  Dahles  and

Susilowati  (2015).  Resilience  theory  posits  that  firms  facing  adversarial  conditions  develop

adaptive  capacities  —  the  ability  to  absorb  shocks,  reorganize,  and  maintain  functionality  —

through the mobilization of internal resources (financial reserves, skills, networks) and external

supports (community solidarity, institutional assistance). In the tourism micro-enterprise context,

resilience is not merely an individual firm attribute but an emergent property of the broader socio-

economic ecosystem within which entrepreneurs operate.

The second theoretical anchor is the Sustainable Livelihoods Framework (SLF), originally

developed by Chambers and Conway (1992) and later institutionalized by the UK Department for

International  Development  (DFID,  1999).  The  SLF  conceptualizes  livelihood  strategies  —

including  entrepreneurial  activities  —  as  shaped  by  five  capital  assets:  human  capital  (skills,

Page 7

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

education,  health),  social  capital  (networks,  trust,  reciprocity),  physical  capital  (tools,

infrastructure), financial capital (savings, credit, remittances), and natural capital (environmental

resources).  For  micro-entrepreneurs  in  Philippine  tourism  enclaves,  natural  capital  —  coastal

environments, marine resources, scenic landscapes — is particularly salient both as an asset and

as a vulnerability, since tourism demand is fundamentally conditioned by environmental quality

and destination image.

The third theoretical lens is Informal Economy Theory, drawing on the foundational work

of Hart (1973) and its subsequent elaboration by De Soto (1989) and Portes et al. (1989). Informal

economy  theory  is  essential  for  contextualizing  the  majority  of  Philippine  tourism  micro-

entrepreneurs,  who  operate  outside  formal  regulatory  frameworks,  lack  access  to  institutional

credit, and depend on social and kinship networks for economic survival. The theory illuminates

how  informality  simultaneously  limits  the  adaptive  resources  available  to  micro-entrepreneurs

(exclusion from government relief programs, inability to access formal credit) and enables flexible,

low-cost  survival  strategies  (unregulated  operating  hours,  cost-sharing  arrangements,  barter

economies) unavailable to formal sector firms.

Together, these three frameworks generate a multi-level analytical architecture: Resilience

Theory  focuses  attention  on  adaptive  processes  at  the  firm  level;  the  Sustainable  Livelihoods

Framework  situates  firm-level  adaptation  within  a  broader  asset-vulnerability  context;  and

Informal Economy Theory illuminates the institutional and structural conditions that shape what

survival strategies are available to, and deployed by, tourism micro-entrepreneurs in the Philippine

context.

The conceptual framework of this study operationalizes the three theoretical lenses into an

analytical model specifically designed for the Philippine  tourism micro-enterprise context. The

framework is organized around four interrelated constructs.

The  first  construct  is  Economic  Disruption  Context,  which  encompasses  the  nature,

severity, duration, and geographic scope of disruptions affecting Philippine tourism enclaves. This

includes  pandemic  events  (COVID-19,  SARS),  natural  disasters  (typhoons,  earthquakes),

environmental policy interventions (Boracay closure), and global economic shocks. The disruption

context shapes the magnitude of economic stress experienced by micro-entrepreneurs and activates

survival responses.

The second construct is the Capital Asset Profile of micro-entrepreneurs, drawn from the

Sustainable Livelihoods Framework. This encompasses the five capital assets — human, social,

physical,  financial,  and  natural  —  as  they  are  configured  for  tourism  micro-entrepreneurs  in

specific  enclave  contexts.  Capital  asset  profiles  vary  significantly  across  geographic  locations

(island vs. coastal vs. urban tourism zones), sectors (food, transport, accommodation, retail), and

individual  characteristics  (gender,  age,  education,  years  in  business),  producing  heterogeneous

adaptive capacities.

The  third  construct  is  Survival  Strategy  Typology,  representing  the  central  analytical

output of this study. Building on Dahles and Bras (1999) and Novelli et al. (2018), this study posits

that survival strategies can be organized along two axes: temporal orientation (short-term crisis

response  vs.  long-term  adaptive  transformation)  and  resource  orientation  (internal  resource

mobilization  vs.  external  resource  acquisition).  This  produces  a  four-quadrant  typology:  (1)

internal-short-term strategies (cost cutting, asset liquidation, labor reduction); (2) internal-long-

term  strategies  (product  diversification,  skills  upgrading,  business  model  transformation);  (3)

external-short-term strategies (social network activation, informal credit, community solidarity);

and  (4)  external-long-term  strategies  (institutional  support  access,  policy  engagement,  market

repositioning).

The  fourth  construct  is  Institutional  and  Policy  Environment,  which  mediates  the

relationship between capital asset profiles and survival strategy adoption. Government programs

(MSME  support,  tourism  relief  funds),  LGU  policies,  access  to  microfinance,  and  industry

association membership all shape which survival strategies are feasible for micro-entrepreneurs in

specific enclave contexts.

The  analytical  flow  of  the  framework  proceeds  from  disruption  context  through  capital

asset profile and institutional environment to survival strategy adoption, with outcomes assessed

in  terms  of  business  continuity,  employment  retention,  and  livelihood  sustainability.  Feedback

loops between outcomes and capital asset profiles capture the dynamic, iterative nature of micro-

entrepreneurial adaptation over the course of economic disruptions.

Scope and Limitations

This  study  is  delimited  to  the  analysis  of  secondary  data  sources  —  including  peer-

reviewed academic publications, government reports, institutional studies, and policy documents

— pertaining to micro-entrepreneurship in Philippine tourism enclaves. The study does not involve

the  collection  of  primary  data  through  interviews,  surveys,  focus  groups,  or  ethnographic

observation. As such, the findings are bounded by the scope, quality, and representativeness of the

secondary data sources available and accessed.

Geographically,  the  study  focuses  on  Philippine  tourism  enclaves,  understood  as

geographic  concentrations  of  tourism  activity  in  coastal,  island,  and  nature-based  destinations.

Urban tourism contexts (Metro Manila, Cebu City) are considered only where secondary sources

explicitly  address  micro-entrepreneurship  dynamics  comparable  to  enclave  settings.  The  study

covers  economic  disruptions  occurring  from  2013  to  2024,  with  particular  emphasis  on  the

COVID-19  pandemic  period  (2020–2023),  which  represents  the  most  extensively  documented

disruption event in the secondary literature.

Several limitations constrain the conclusions that can be drawn from this study. First, the

secondary  literature  on  Philippine  tourism  micro-entrepreneurship  is  uneven  in  geographic

coverage,  with  certain  destinations  (Boracay,  Palawan)  better  represented  than  others  (Samar-

Leyte,  Camiguin,  Batanes).  This  unevenness  may  introduce  geographic  bias  into  the  survival

strategy typology developed. Second, secondary data sources vary in methodological rigor, with

some government reports and institutional studies employing limited sampling strategies or non-

transparent  analytical  procedures.  The  quality  of  conclusions  drawn  from  these  sources  is

necessarily constrained by their methodological limitations.

Third,  as  a  qualitative  secondary  data  analysis,  this  study  cannot  establish  causal

relationships between structural conditions and survival outcomes; it can identify associations and

patterns but not determine directionality or magnitude of effects. Fourth, rapidly evolving policy

environments — particularly in the post-pandemic recovery period — mean that some institutional

and policy findings may be superseded by developments occurring after the study's data coverage

period.

These limitations are acknowledged not to diminish the study's contribution but to situate

its findings appropriately and to identify directions for future primary research that can build on

the analytical framework developed here.

Tourism Micro-Entrepreneurship in Developing Economies

The  study  of  micro-entrepreneurship  in  tourism  contexts  has  developed  as  a  distinct

subfield within tourism studies over the past three decades. Dahles and Bras (1999) produced a

foundational analysis of small-scale tourism entrepreneurs in Indonesia, demonstrating that these

actors  develop  highly  context-specific  survival  strategies  shaped  by  their  position  within

destination power structures, their access to tourist flows, and their embeddedness in local social

networks.  Their  work  established  that  tourism  micro-entrepreneurs  are  not  simply  passive

participants in tourism economies but active agents who deploy creativity and social intelligence

to carve out economic niches within larger destination systems.

Ateljevic and Doorne (2000) extended this analysis to lifestyle entrepreneurship, arguing

that many small tourism business operators in developing economies are motivated not primarily

by  profit  maximization  but  by  non-economic  goals  including  cultural  preservation,  community

belonging, and quality of life maintenance. This insight is significant for understanding survival

strategy choices: micro-entrepreneurs who are deeply embedded in community and place are more

likely to deploy community solidarity-based survival strategies and less likely to exit the market

during disruptions, even when continued operation is economically irrational in the short term.

Scheyvens  (2007)  and  Bolwell  and  Weinz  (2008)  examined  the  relationship  between

tourism micro-entrepreneurship and poverty alleviation in developing economies, finding that the

linkages between tourism growth and micro-enterprise income are highly mediated by structural

factors  including  market  access,  infrastructure  quality,  and  the  distribution  of  power  within

destination  supply  chains.  Their  work  highlights  that  micro-entrepreneurs  in  tourism  enclaves

frequently  occupy  the  lowest-margin  segments  of  tourism  value  chains,  making  them  highly

vulnerable  to  demand  shocks  and  limiting  their  capacity  to  accumulate  the  financial  reserves

needed to weather economic disruptions.

More recent contributions by Farmaki et al. (2020) and Alonso et al. (2021) have examined

micro-enterprise responses to crises including the 2008 global financial crisis and the COVID-19

formation as emerging survival strategies among small tourism operators. However, these studies

Economic Disruptions and Small Enterprise Survival

The  literature  on  small  enterprise  survival  during  economic  disruptions  has  identified

several consistent patterns across diverse crisis contexts. Doern et al. (2019), in a systematic review

of entrepreneurship and crisis literature, identified four primary categories of firm-level response

to  economic  shocks:  reactive  responses  (immediate  cost  reduction,  operational  contraction);

adaptive  responses  (product  and  market  modifications);  proactive  responses  (opportunity

recognition and exploitation within the crisis context); and exit responses (temporary suspension

or  permanent  closure).  The  distribution  of  responses  across  these  categories  is  shaped  by  firm

characteristics (size, age, sector, formalization status) and environmental factors (crisis severity,

institutional support availability, market structure).

Wasilczuk (2000) found that social capital — particularly strong ties to family, community,

and  business  networks  —  is  among  the  most  powerful  predictors  of  small  enterprise  survival

during economic disruptions. This finding has been consistently replicated in subsequent studies

across diverse geographic and sectoral contexts (Frankenberger et al., 2013; Herbane, 2019), and

is  particularly  relevant  to  the  Philippine  context  where  kinship  networks  (utang  na  loob,

bayanihan) constitute a fundamental organizing principle of economic life.

Williams and Vorley (2015) examined the survival strategies of small enterprises following

economic crises in transition economies, finding that institutional voids  — gaps in government

support  systems,  credit  markets,  and  regulatory  frameworks  —  both  constrain  formal  adaptive

strategies and create space for informal survival mechanisms. This dialectical relationship between

formal  institutional  absence  and  informal  adaptive  capacity  is  a  recurring  theme  in  developing

economy  entrepreneurship  literature  and  is  directly  applicable  to  the  Philippine  tourism  micro-

enterprise context.

In the specific context of tourism, Hall et al. (2017) developed a comprehensive framework

for  understanding  tourism  enterprise  responses  to  crises,  distinguishing  between  first-order

responses (immediate operational adjustments), second-order responses (strategic repositioning),

and third-order responses (systemic transformation). Their framework suggests that the majority

of  micro-enterprises  in  developing  economy  tourism  contexts  are  constrained  to  first-order

responses by their limited resource bases, and that access to institutional support is the primary

determinant of whether firms can achieve second- or third-order adaptive responses.

The Philippine Tourism Economy and Micro-Enterprise Landscape

The Philippine tourism sector is characterized by significant structural dualism: a formal

sector  dominated  by  large  hotel  chains,  airlines,  and  tour  operators,  and  an  informal  sector

comprising  millions  of  micro  and  small  enterprises  that  provide  the  majority  of  ground-level

tourism services (ADB, 2021). The DOT (2020) estimated that approximately 92% of tourism-

related  businesses  in  the  Philippines  qualify  as  micro-enterprises  under  the  MSMED  Council

definition (capitalization of less than PHP 3 million), with the majority concentrated in food and

beverage services, transportation, and souvenir retail.

Paguia  (2018)  conducted  a  detailed  analysis  of  tourism  micro-enterprise  dynamics  in

Palawan, finding that the majority of micro-operators in the destination had capitalization of less

than PHP  500,000, operated without formal business  registration, lacked  access to institutional

credit, and depended on tourist season income to cover annual household expenses. Her findings

highlight the extreme financial fragility of tourism micro-entrepreneurs in enclave destinations and

their limited capacity to maintain operations through extended demand disruptions.

The  Boracay  closure  of  2018  provided  a  rare  natural  experiment  for  studying  micro-

enterprise responses to sudden, government-imposed tourism disruptions.  Sabanal and Bautista

(2021) analyzed the impacts of the six-month closure on micro-entrepreneurs in the destination,

documenting significant livelihood displacement, forced migration, and enterprise exit among the

most vulnerable operators. However, they also documented remarkable resilience and adaptation

among  better-networked  and  more  financially  resourced  operators,  including  pivot  to  domestic

supply chains, participation in government livelihood programs, and development of new product

offerings targeting the post-closure rehabilitated destination.

The  COVID-19  pandemic  represented  a  shock  of  unprecedented  severity  for  Philippine

tourism.  The  DOT  (2021)  estimated  that  over  5.7  million  jobs  in  tourism-related  sectors  were

affected,  with  micro-enterprises  bearing  a  disproportionate  share  of  employment  losses.

The policy landscape for tourism micro-enterprise support in the Philippines is fragmented

across multiple agencies including the DOT, DTI, DOLE, and LGUs, with coordination challenges

limiting the effectiveness of support programs during disruptions (PIDS, 2021). The Magna Carta

for Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (Republic Act 9501, as amended by RA 10644) provides

the  primary  legislative  framework  for  MSME  support,  but  its  provisions  are  predominantly

oriented toward formal sector firms and do not adequately address the specific vulnerabilities of

informally operating tourism micro-entrepreneurs.

Microfinance institutions, including the People's Credit and Finance Corporation (PCFC)

and  a  network  of  rural  banks  and  cooperatives,  provide  some  financial  access  for  micro-

entrepreneurs,  but  tourism-specific  microfinance  products  remain  limited  (Bangko  Sentral  ng

Pilipinas  [BSP],  2022).  The  collapse  of  tourism  income  during  disruptions  simultaneously

increases  the  credit  needs  of  micro-entrepreneurs  and  reduces  their  creditworthiness  under

standard  microfinance  assessment  criteria,  creating  a  financing  gap  precisely  when  it  is  most

needed.

At  the  local  government  level,  LGU  responses  to  tourism  disruptions  have  varied

considerably  across  destinations,  with  some  (notably  Siargao  and  Bohol)  demonstrating  more

proactive and community-centered support approaches than others (Alampay & Umezaki, 2019).

LGU capacity for  effective  micro-enterprise support during disruptions  is constrained by fiscal

resources,  administrative  capacity,  and  the  quality  of  local  governance,  producing  significant

geographic variation in survival outcomes across Philippine tourism enclaves.

The review of related literature reveals several significant gaps that this study is designed

to address. First, while international literature on tourism micro-enterprise resilience is growing,

the  Philippines  remains  comparatively  understudied  relative  to  its  significance  as  a  developing

economy  tourism  destination.  The  existing  Philippine-focused  literature  is  geographically

concentrated in a small number of destinations (Boracay, Palawan), leaving the dynamics of micro-

entrepreneurship in less prominent tourism enclaves largely undocumented.

Second,  existing  studies  predominantly  employ  quantitative  methods  (surveys,

econometric  analysis)  that  capture  the  scope  and  magnitude  of  disruption  impacts  but  are  less

effective at illuminating the processes by which micro-entrepreneurs construct and deploy survival

strategies. Qualitative analysis of these processes — drawing on the rich documentary evidence

available in government reports, institutional studies, and academic case studies — can generate

the process-level insights necessary for effective policy design.

Third, while survival strategies during individual disruption events (particularly COVID-

19) have been studied, cross-disruption comparative analysis — examining how survival strategy

patterns  differ  across  disruption  types  (pandemic,  natural  disaster,  policy  intervention)  —  is

virtually  absent  from  the  Philippine  literature.  Such  comparative  analysis  is  essential  for

developing generalizable frameworks applicable to future disruption events.

Fourth, the role of gender in mediating survival strategy access and effectiveness among

Philippine  tourism  micro-entrepreneurs  has  received  very  limited  scholarly  attention,  despite

evidence  from  other  developing  economy  contexts  that  women-owned  micro-enterprises  face

systematically  different  vulnerability  profiles  and  adaptive  constraints  (ILO,  2020).  This  gap

represents both an analytical lacuna and a policy blind spot.

Research Synthesis

The  synthesis  of  literature  reviewed  in  this  chapter  supports  several  overarching

conclusions that ground the analytical framework of this study and guide its empirical analysis.

The survival of tourism micro-enterprises during economic disruptions is neither random

nor  purely  a  function  of  individual  entrepreneurial  talent;  it  is  systematically  shaped  by  the

interplay of capital asset configurations, social network embeddedness, institutional environment

quality, and the nature of the disruption itself. Micro-entrepreneurs with more diverse capital asset

portfolios — particularly those with stronger social capital and some degree of financial reserve

— consistently demonstrate greater adaptive capacity across disruption contexts.

The informal status of the majority of Philippine tourism micro-entrepreneurs is a double-

edged  sword:  it  enables  the  deployment  of  flexible,  low-cost  survival  strategies  unavailable  to

formal sector firms, while simultaneously excluding entrepreneurs from the formal institutional

support systems most likely to enable substantive adaptive transformation. Policy interventions

that  fail  to  engage  the  informal  character  of  tourism  micro-enterprise  ecosystems  will

systematically miss their most vulnerable target population.

Survival  strategies  are  not  uniformly  distributed  across  tourism  enclave  micro-

entrepreneurs; they are differentiated by sector, geography, gender, and social network position in

ways that generate significant heterogeneity in outcomes even within the same disruption event

and  the  same  destination.  A  typological  approach  —  organizing  observed  strategies  into

analytically  coherent  categories  —  is  therefore  essential  for  generating  policy-relevant  insights

that can be targeted to the specific circumstances of different micro-entrepreneur populations.

Page 20

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

Finally, the literature consistently reveals that the post-disruption recovery period — not

the crisis peak itself — is the critical window during which micro-entrepreneur survival or exit is

determined.  Survival  strategies  that  maintain  minimum  operational  viability  through  the  crisis

period  create  the  platform  for  recovery-stage  adaptation;  strategies  that  sacrifice  operational

continuity  for  short-term  resource  conservation  risk  permanent  enterprise  exit  even  after  the

disruption  resolves.  This  temporal  dynamic  is  a  central  analytical  theme  that  guides  the

interpretation of secondary data evidence throughout this study.

Introduction

This chapter presents the methodological design of the study, detailing the philosophical

assumptions,  research  design,  data  sources,  data  collection  procedures,  analytical  framework,

trustworthiness measures, and ethical considerations that govern the conduct of the research. The

methodology  is  deliberately  constructed  to  align  with  the  qualitative  nature  of  the  inquiry,  the

secondary  data  character  of  the  evidence  base,  and  the  interpretive  goals  of  the  research  —

specifically,  the  construction  of  an  empirically  grounded  typology  of  micro-entrepreneurial

survival strategies in Philippine tourism enclaves during economic disruption.

Methodological  transparency  is  a  foundational  commitment  of  this  study.  Because

qualitative  secondary  data  analysis  occupies  a  distinct  position  within  the  research  methods

landscape — neither a traditional literature review nor a primary qualitative study — it is essential

Page 21

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

to  articulate  clearly  the  procedures  and  criteria  that  govern  data  selection,  interpretation,  and

synthesis. This chapter provides that articulation in sufficient detail to enable readers to assess the

credibility, transferability, and confirmability of the study's findings.

Research Design

Qualitative Research Paradigm

This study is situated within the interpretivist research paradigm, which holds that social

reality  is  not  a  fixed,  objective  phenomenon  awaiting  discovery  but  is  constructed  through  the

meanings that social actors — in this case, micro-entrepreneurs, policy makers, and researchers —

assign to their experiences and circumstances (Creswell & Poth, 2018). The interpretivist paradigm

is appropriate for this study because the central research questions are fundamentally concerned

with meaning-making: how micro-entrepreneurs understand and respond to economic disruption,

how  researchers  and  policy  analysts  have  characterized  their  strategies,  and  what  patterns  of

meaning can be discerned across diverse documentary sources.

The ontological position of the study is constructivist: it holds that the survival strategies

documented in secondary sources are not simply neutral descriptions of objective behaviors but

are shaped by the theoretical frameworks, conceptual categories, and methodological choices of

the  researchers  and  document  authors  who  produced  them.  The  analytical  task  of  this  study  is

therefore not simply to aggregate findings but to interpret them — to engage critically with the

frameworks through which they were generated and to construct a more integrated understanding

than any individual source provides.

The  epistemological  position  is  that  knowledge  about  micro-entrepreneurial  survival

emerges  from  the  systematic,  reflexive  engagement  with  the  documentary  record  produced  by

Page 22

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

scholars, government agencies, and institutions who have studied these phenomena directly. As

Bowen (2009) argues, documentary analysis conducted within an interpretivist framework is not

a second-best substitute for primary research but a methodologically legitimate approach that can

generate insights unavailable through primary data collection — particularly in contexts where the

phenomena of interest are distributed across time, geography, and organizational settings that no

single primary study can encompass.

Qualitative Secondary Data Analysis

The specific research design employed in this study is Qualitative Secondary Data Analysis

(QSDA), a methodology that involves the systematic collection, critical appraisal, and interpretive

analysis of qualitative data generated by prior researchers or documentary sources (Heaton, 2004;

Smith,  2008).  QSDA  is  distinguished  from  quantitative  meta-analysis  by  its  commitment  to

preserving  the  contextual  richness  and  interpretive  complexity  of  source  materials,  rather  than

reducing them to numerical effect sizes or aggregated statistics.

Within the QSDA tradition, this study employs what Heaton (2004) terms an amplified

analysis  approach:  rather  than  reanalyzing  raw  primary  data  from  previous  studies  (which  is

unavailable), the study analyzes the published findings, interpretations, and conclusions of prior

research  alongside  the  documentary  evidence  contained  in  government  reports,  institutional

studies, and policy documents. This approach is appropriate when the research question requires

synthesis across a broader evidence base than any single primary study addresses, and when the

goal  is  theoretical  integration  and  typology  construction  rather  than  the  generation  of  new

empirical data.

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

The QSDA design aligns with the study's research questions in three specific ways. First,

the focus on documenting patterns across disruption events (RQ1) requires a cross-study synthesis

that  primary  data  collection  from  a  single  disruption  context  cannot  provide.  Second,  the

construction  of  a  survival  strategy  typology  (RQ3)  requires  sufficient  breadth  of  evidence  to

identify variations as well as patterns — breadth achievable only through systematic analysis of

multiple  secondary  sources.  Third,  the  identification  of  policy  gaps  (RQ4)  is  inherently  a

secondary data task, requiring engagement with the documentary record of government programs,

institutional assessments, and policy evaluations.

Data Analysis

Thematic Analysis

The  primary  analytical  method  employed  in  this  study  is  Thematic  Analysis  (TA)  as

theorized by Braun and Clarke (2006) and further developed in their reflexive thematic analysis

framework  (Braun  &  Clarke,  2019).  Thematic  analysis  is  appropriate  for  this  study  because  it

provides  a  flexible  yet  rigorous  method  for  identifying,  analyzing,  and  interpreting  patterns  of

meaning  across  diverse  qualitative  data  sources  —  precisely  the  analytical  task  required  when

synthesizing findings from a heterogeneous secondary data corpus.

The  thematic  analysis  proceeds  through  six  phases  as  described  by  Braun  and  Clarke

(2006). In Phase 1 (familiarization), each source document is read in full, with initial impressions,

observations, and questions recorded in analytical memos. In Phase 2 (initial coding), meaningful

units of text — passages, sentences, or document sections that bear on the research questions —

are identified and assigned descriptive codes. Coding is conducted inductively from the data, with

codes remaining close to the language and framing of the source documents at this stage.

In  Phase  3  (theme  generation),  initial  codes  are  grouped  into  candidate  themes  by

identifying patterns of similarity, contrast, and conceptual relationship across the coded data. In

Phase 4 (theme review), candidate themes are reviewed against the coded data and the full corpus

Page 28

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

to  assess  their  coherence,  distinctiveness,  and  evidentiary  grounding.  Themes  that  do  not  meet

these  standards  are  refined,  merged,  or  discarded.  In  Phase  5  (theme  definition  and  naming),

surviving  themes  are  defined  with  precision,  and  theme  names  are  selected  to  capture  their

essential meaning. In Phase 6 (writing up), themes are woven into the analytical narrative of the

findings  chapter,  with  evidence  from  source  documents  integrated  to  support  each  thematic

interpretation.

Document Analysis

Complementing thematic analysis, this study employs document analysis as described by

Bowen (2009) and Yin (2018) to engage with the specific character of documentary secondary

sources. Document analysis involves not only extracting content from documents but attending to

their context of production — who produced the document, for what purpose, for what audience,

and under what institutional conditions — since these contextual factors shape what the document

says and does not say, and how its content should be interpreted.

For government reports and institutional studies, document analysis attends particularly to

the framing of micro-enterprise issues, the categories employed to classify survival strategies, and

the implicit normative assumptions that shape policy recommendations. These analytical moves

are  essential  for  the  fourth  research  question,  which  requires  not  simply  cataloguing  policy

responses but critically assessing their adequacy and identifying their silences and gaps.

event or destination context as a 'case' and systematically comparing patterns of survival strategy

Trustworthiness and Rigor

The trustworthiness of qualitative research is assessed against four criteria established by

Lincoln and Guba (1985): credibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmability. This study

addresses each criterion through specific methodological strategies.

Credibility  —  the  qualitative  analogue  of  internal  validity  —  is  established  through

prolonged engagement with the secondary data corpus, triangulation across source types (cross-

referencing  findings  from  academic  studies  against  government  reports  and  institutional

assessments),  member  checking  through  peer  review  of  analytical  interpretations,  and  negative

case  analysis  (actively  seeking  evidence  that  disconfirms  emerging  themes  and  typological

categories). Triangulation across source types is particularly important in this study, as it guards

against the risk of over-reliance on any single source perspective.

Transferability  —  the  qualitative  analogue  of  external  validity  —  is  addressed  through

thick description of the analytical context, source corpus, and findings, enabling readers to assess

the  applicability  of  the  study's  conclusions  to  their  own  contexts.  The  typology  of  survival

strategies developed in this study is explicitly presented as an analytical framework rather than a

universal law, with the conditions and contexts that shape its applicability described in sufficient

detail to guide informed application by practitioners and researchers.

Dependability  —  the  qualitative  analogue  of  reliability  —  is  established  through  the

documentation of analytical procedures in sufficient detail to enable an external auditor to assess

the  consistency  and  appropriateness  of  the  analytical  decisions  made  throughout  the  study.  An

audit  trail  comprising  search  logs,  inclusion/exclusion  decision  records,  coding  frameworks,

analytical memos, and theme development records is maintained throughout the research process.

Confirmability  —  the  qualitative  analogue  of  objectivity  —  is  addressed  through

reflexivity:  the  researcher's  positionality,  theoretical  commitments,  and  potential  biases  are

explicitly  acknowledged  and  their  potential  influence  on  analytical  decisions  is  examined

throughout the research process. As a business scholar with knowledge of the Philippine economic

context  and  familiarity  with  micro-enterprise  literature,  the  researcher  brings  both  relevant

expertise  and  potential  interpretive  assumptions  to  the  analysis;  reflexive  practice  requires  that

these be made visible rather than treated as transparent.

The COVID-19 pandemic emerges unambiguously from the secondary data as the most

severe and extensively documented disruption of the study period. The Department of Tourism

(2021) recorded an 82% decline in international visitor arrivals in 2020, with receipts collapsing

from PHP 482.15 billion in 2019 to approximately PHP 82.4 billion — a contraction that translated

directly into near-total revenue elimination for enclave micro-entrepreneurs whose livelihoods are

structurally dependent on tourist expenditure. The Philippine Institute for Development Studies

(2021)  estimated  that  over  5.7  million  workers  in  tourism-related  sectors  were  affected  by  the

pandemic, with micro and small enterprises accounting for a disproportionate share of job losses

due to their lower financial resilience and more limited access to government relief programs.

Natural disasters constitute the second major disruption category, with Typhoon Haiyan

(Yolanda) in November 2013 and the Bohol earthquake of October 2013 representing the most

severely documented events. Alampay and Umezaki (2019) describe the compound effect of these

two disasters — occurring within weeks of each other — on Eastern Visayas and Central Visayas

tourism  ecosystems,  noting  that  the  physical  destruction  of  micro-enterprise  assets  was

effects lasting well beyond the immediate post-disaster period. This demand suppression dynamic

— whereby destination image damage persists long after physical infrastructure has been restored

—  is  a  recurring  pattern  across  natural  disaster  disruptions  in  the  secondary  data  corpus  and

represents  a  structurally  distinct  challenge  for  micro-entrepreneurs  compared  to  the  immediate

physical damage caused by disasters.

Policy-induced disruptions are represented most prominently by the Boracay island closure

of April to October 2018, ordered by President Rodrigo Duterte for environmental rehabilitation.

Sabanal and Bautista (2021) document the closure's devastating impacts on micro-entrepreneurs,

estimating  that  approximately  17,000  workers  in  tourism-related  livelihoods  were  directly

displaced during the six-month period. Critically, their analysis reveals significant heterogeneity

in closure impacts: operators with more diversified livelihood portfolios, stronger off-island social

networks, and higher pre-closure capitalization demonstrated markedly higher survival rates than

the most vulnerable operators — predominantly women, elderly workers, and those with the lowest

capitalization — who were most likely to exit the market permanently during the closure period.

1.2 Compound and Sequential Disruption Dynamics

A significant finding emerging from the cross-case analysis is the compound disruption

dynamic:  the  tendency  for  multiple  disruption  events  to  overlap  or  occur  in  rapid  succession,

producing cumulative impacts that exceed the sum of their individual effects. The Taal Volcano

eruption of January 2020, which displaced thousands of Batangas tourism micro-entrepreneurs,

was  followed  within  weeks  by  the  onset  of  the  COVID-19  pandemic,  creating  a  compounded

disruption from which recovery was effectively impossible for many operators who had already

Page 36

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

exhausted their financial buffers responding to the volcanic emergency (DTI, 2020; PHIVOLCS,

2020).

The secondary data also reveals a sequential disruption pattern in which recovery from one

event is disrupted by subsequent shocks before micro-entrepreneurs have been able to rebuild their

capital asset bases. Dahles and Susilowati (2015) observe this pattern in their analysis of Southeast

Asian tourism micro-enterprise resilience, noting that each successive shock erodes the social and

financial capital stocks that micro-entrepreneurs depend upon for survival, producing a progressive

depletion of adaptive capacity that makes later disruptions increasingly difficult to weather even

when  they  are  of  similar  objective  severity  to  earlier  events.  This  finding  has  significant

implications for the design of recovery support programs: single-event interventions fail to address

the cumulative depletion of micro-entrepreneur capital assets that sequential disruptions produce.

1.3 Differential Vulnerability by Sector and Location

The secondary data consistently demonstrates that vulnerability to economic disruption is

not  uniformly  distributed  across  tourism  micro-entrepreneurs  in  Philippine  enclaves  but  is

systematically patterned by sector, geographic location, and enterprise characteristics. Transport

operators — tricycle drivers, boat operators, and jeepney owners serving tourist routes — emerge

as  among  the  most  severely  impacted  by  demand  disruptions,  because  their  income  stream  is

almost entirely dependent on tourist footfall with virtually no domestic substitution market during

lockdown or closure periods (PIDS, 2021).

Food  and  beverage  micro-operators  demonstrate  relatively  greater  resilience  during

disruptions, primarily because food demand does not disappear entirely even when tourist flows

collapse  —  households  and  communities  continue  to  require  food  services,  creating  a  residual

Page 37

domestic market that transport and souvenir retail operators cannot access. However, the income

generated from this domestic market is typically insufficient to cover the costs of a tourism-scale

operation, meaning that food operators survive disruptions at a reduced operational scale rather

than maintaining full viability (Paguia, 2018).

Geographically,  island-based  micro-entrepreneurs  —  particularly  those  in  island

destinations  accessible  only  by  sea  or  air  —  face  structurally  distinct  vulnerability  profiles

compared to coastal mainland operators. The physical isolation of island enclaves amplifies the

income  impact  of  demand  disruptions  (there  is  no  nearby  alternative  market  to  serve)  while

simultaneously raising the costs of survival strategies that require access to off-island resources,

credit markets, or government programs (Paguia, 2018; ADB, 2021). This geographic vulnerability

dimension  is  consistently  underappreciated  in  national-level  policy  analyses,  which  tend  to

aggregate  micro-enterprise  impacts  without  disaggregating  by  the  physical  accessibility  of

destination contexts.

Section 2: Documented Survival Strategies and Structural Conditions (RQ2)

2.1 Overview of Survival Strategy Landscape

The thematic analysis of the secondary data corpus identified a rich and diverse repertoire

of  survival  strategies  deployed  by  Philippine  tourism  micro-entrepreneurs  during  economic

disruptions.  Consistent  with  the  theoretical  framework  developed  in  Chapter  I,  these  strategies

operate at multiple levels — individual enterprise, household, community, and institutional — and

are deployed in combinations that vary across disruption types, geographic contexts, and enterprise

characteristics.  Rather  than  being  discrete,  mutually  exclusive  choices,  survival  strategies  are

Page 38

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

typically  deployed  as  interconnected  portfolios  in  which  different  strategies  are  sequenced  and

combined in response to the evolving demands of the disruption context.

The  following  subsections  present  the  major  survival  strategy  clusters  identified  in  the

thematic analysis, organized by their primary operational logic and resource orientation. For each

cluster,  documentary  evidence  from  the  secondary  data  corpus  is  integrated  to  substantiate  the

thematic interpretation and to illuminate the structural conditions under which specific strategies

are most commonly deployed.

2.2 Operational Contraction Strategies

Operational  contraction  —  the  reduction  of  enterprise  scale,  cost  base,  and  scope  —

emerges from the secondary data as the near-universal first-order response of Philippine tourism

micro-entrepreneurs to economic disruptions. The immediacy of this response reflects the acute

financial vulnerability of micro-entrepreneurs who typically lack the financial reserves to maintain

full  operations  through  extended  periods  of  reduced  revenue.  The  Philippine  Institute  for

Development Studies (2021) found that the majority of tourism micro-enterprises surveyed during

the COVID-19 pandemic had pre-crisis savings sufficient for fewer than two months of operating

expenses, making rapid cost reduction an existential necessity rather than a strategic choice.

Operational contraction strategies documented in the secondary corpus include: temporary

suspension  of  business  operations;  reduction  of  operating  hours;  layoff  or  wage  reduction  of

employed family or non-family workers; reduction of product range to lowest-cost and highest-

margin  items;  deferral  of  equipment  maintenance  and  capital  expenditure;  and  liquidation  of

business assets to generate immediate cash flow. Sabanal and Bautista (2021) document a variant

specific to the Boracay closure context: the subletting of commercial premises by operators who

Page 39

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

could not maintain rent obligations, converting a business cost into a modest income stream during

the closure period.

A critical finding from the cross-case analysis is that the depth and duration of operational

contraction is strongly mediated by pre-crisis capitalization. Operators with higher pre-disruption

asset bases are better able to calibrate their contraction — reducing scale while maintaining core

capabilities — rather than being forced into complete operational suspension. This distinction is

consequential for recovery: operators who maintain even minimal operational continuity through

disruption periods retain the organizational routines, customer relationships, and supplier networks

needed to facilitate rapid recovery once disruptions resolve, while those who undergo complete

suspension must effectively rebuild their operations from scratch.

2.3 Livelihood Diversification Strategies

Livelihood diversification — the extension of economic activity into sectors or markets

outside  the  primary  tourism  service  —  represents  the  second  most  consistently  documented

survival strategy in the secondary data corpus. Diversification strategies are particularly significant

analytically because they bridge the boundary between survival and transformation: operators who

successfully diversify during disruptions may emerge with more resilient livelihood portfolios than

they held pre-disruption, converting crisis into an inadvertent catalyst for structural improvement.

The secondary data documents diversification strategies across three primary dimensions.

The first is product and service diversification within the food and beverage sector: operators who

previously  served  primarily  tourist  clientele  pivoting  to  serve  domestic  residential  customers

through delivery services, catering, and community-oriented menus priced for local rather than

tourist purchasing power. The DOT (2021) notes that this pivot was facilitated in many enclave

Page 40

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

communities  by  the  simultaneous  growth  of  digital  food  ordering  platforms,  which  provided

infrastructure for reaching domestic customers that had not previously been accessible to small

operators.

The  second  diversification  dimension  involves  sectoral  transition:  operators  exiting

tourism-related  services  entirely  during  disruptions  and  engaging  in  agricultural  production,

fishing, handicraft manufacturing, or informal retail trade targeting non-tourist markets. Alampay

and Umezaki (2019) document this pattern in post-Haiyan Eastern Visayas communities, where

former tourism transport operators returned to smallholder farming and fishing during the extended

disruption  of  tourist  demand,  effectively  reverting  to  pre-tourism  livelihood  strategies  that

remained  embedded  in  community  knowledge  and  social  networks  despite  years  of  tourism

engagement.

The third diversification dimension is digital platform adoption — a strategy that gained

particular  salience  during

the  COVID-19  period  when  physical  mobility  restrictions

simultaneously eliminated in-person tourism service delivery and expanded the digital consumer

market.  BSP  (2022)  financial  inclusion  data  indicates  significant  growth  in  GCash  and  digital

payment  adoption  among  micro-enterprises  during  the  pandemic  period,  with  enclave  tourism

operators among the adopters. However, the secondary data also documents significant barriers to

effective  digital  adoption  among  the  most  vulnerable  micro-entrepreneurs,  including  limited

digital literacy, smartphone access constraints, and the absence of tourism-specific e-commerce

infrastructure in many enclave destinations.

2.4 Social Capital Activation Strategies

The  activation  of  social  capital  networks  —  kinship,  community,  and  patron-client

relationships  —  is  among  the  most  distinctively  Filipino  survival  strategies  documented  in  the

secondary  data  corpus,  and  is  consistently  identified  as  a  critical  mediating  variable  in  micro-

enterprise  survival  during  disruptions.  The  cultural  specificity  of  Philippine  social  capital

mechanisms  —  utang  na  loob  (debt  of  gratitude),  bayanihan  (community  solidarity),  and

paluwagan (informal rotating savings and credit associations) — means that these strategies cannot

be straightforwardly generalized from international literature but require contextually grounded

analysis.

Utang na loob networks provide access to informal credit and material support from family

members, neighbors, and long-term business partners that would be unavailable through formal

credit  channels,  particularly  for  informally  operating  micro-entrepreneurs  who  lack  the

documentation required for institutional loans. Paguia (2018) finds that in Palawan island tourism

communities, informal credit mobilized through utang na loob networks constituted the primary

financial  survival  mechanism  for  the  majority  of  micro-entrepreneurs  during  tourism  demand

disruptions,  with  formal  credit  playing  a  negligible  role  due  to  its  inaccessibility  to  informal

operators. This finding underscores the centrality of social capital in Philippine micro-enterprise

survival and the consequent importance of policies that reinforce rather than undermine the social

networks that sustain these informal credit mechanisms.

Bayanihan community solidarity — the collective mobilization of community labor and

resources for shared goals — is documented particularly in the context of natural disaster recovery,

where  community-level  resource  sharing  and  mutual  assistance  enables  micro-entrepreneurs  to

restore damaged assets and resume operations more rapidly than they could through individual

effort alone. Alampay and Umezaki (2019) describe bayanihan mobilization in post-Haiyan Leyte

communities as a critical accelerant of micro-enterprise recovery, enabling a pace of reconstruction

that  formal  government  recovery  programs,  constrained  by  bureaucratic  processes  and  funding

disbursement delays, could not match.

Paluwagan  participation  —  the  mobilization  of  accumulated  informal  savings  through

rotating credit associations — provides a structured mechanism for accumulating lump-sum capital

during  disruptions  when  regular  income  flows  are  suppressed.  The  secondary  data  documents

paluwagan use as both a crisis response mechanism (drawing existing rounds early to generate

immediate cash) and a recovery financing tool (participating in new rounds to accumulate capital

for  post-crisis  operational  resumption).  PIDS  (2021)  notes  that  paluwagan  groups  in  several

Boracay  operator  communities  served  as  de  facto  microfinance  institutions  during  the  closure

period, providing capital access that government programs were unable to deliver with comparable

speed and flexibility.

Section 4: Policy and Institutional Environment (RQ4)

4.1 Overview of the Policy Landscape

The  secondary  data  corpus  documents  a  complex  and  fragmented  policy  landscape  for

tourism micro-enterprise support in the Philippines, characterized by multiple overlapping agency

Page 49

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

mandates, inconsistent program coverage, and persistent gaps between policy intent and program

delivery. The primary agencies with mandates relevant to tourism micro-enterprise support — the

Department  of  Tourism,  the  Department  of  Trade  and  Industry,  the  Department  of  Labor  and

Employment, the National Economic and Development Authority, and Local Government Units

—  operate  largely  independently  without  a  coherent  inter-agency  framework  for  coordinated

micro-enterprise support during economic disruptions. PIDS (2021) characterizes this institutional

fragmentation as one of the primary structural barriers  to effective government response to the

COVID-19 impacts on tourism micro-enterprises, noting that operators frequently fell between the

mandates of different agencies without receiving comprehensive support from any.

The  legislative  foundation  for  MSME  support  —  Republic  Act  9501  (Magna  Carta  for

MSMEs),  as  amended  by  RA  10644  (Go  Negosyo  Act)  —  provides  a  broad  framework  for

enterprise support but is predominantly oriented toward formally registered businesses with access

to  banking  relationships  and  institutional  credit.  The  specific  vulnerabilities  of  informally

operating  tourism  micro-entrepreneurs  —  their  exclusion  from  formal  financial  systems,  their

dependence on seasonal tourist  income,  and their concentrated exposure  to  destination-specific

demand  shocks  —  are  not  adequately  addressed  within  this  legislative  framework,  creating  a

structural gap between what the policy system is designed to support and what tourism enclave

micro-entrepreneurs actually need.

Section 5: Integrated Analytical Framework and Theoretical Implications

5.1 The Micro-Entrepreneur Survival Ecosystem

The synthesis of findings across the four research questions enables the articulation of an

integrated  analytical  framework  for  understanding  micro-entrepreneurial  survival  in  Philippine

tourism enclaves — a framework that goes beyond the individual components addressed in each

research question to capture the dynamic systemic relationships among disruption context, capital

asset profiles, survival strategy repertoires, and institutional environments.

The  framework  conceptualizes  micro-entrepreneur  survival  as  an  ecosystem  property

rather than an individual firm property: it emerges from the interaction of individual enterprise

characteristics  (capital  asset  profiles,  formalization  status,  sector,  and  operator  characteristics),

community-level  social  structures  (kinship  networks,  bayanihan  solidarity,  paluwagan

institutions),  destination-level  infrastructure  (physical  accessibility,  market  diversity,  LGU

capacity), and national institutional environment (policy frameworks, relief programs, financial

inclusion  infrastructure).  No  single  level  of  this  ecosystem  can  produce  sustainable  micro-

enterprise resilience independently; effective resilience requires coherent action across all levels

simultaneously.

The findings reveal a consistent structural tension at the core of this ecosystem: the survival

strategies  most  accessible  to  the  most  vulnerable  micro-entrepreneurs  (Quadrants  I  and  III  —

contraction  and  social  capital  activation)  are  precisely  those  that  are  least  capable  of  enabling

genuine  adaptive  transformation,  while  the  strategies  most  capable  of  enabling  transformation

(Quadrants II and IV — internal transformation and institutional engagement) are least accessible

Page 54

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

to  the  most  vulnerable  operators.  This  structural  tension  constitutes  what  may  be  termed  a

resilience trap: vulnerability and adaptive incapacity are mutually reinforcing, with each disruption

event depleting the capital asset stocks that would enable access to transformative strategies, and

with policy systems that fail to engage informal operators leaving the most vulnerable without the

institutional support needed to break the cycle.

5.2 Theoretical Contributions

The findings of this study make several contributions to the theoretical frameworks that

anchor  the  research.  With  respect  to  Resilience  Theory,  the  study  contributes  the  concept  of

compounded  resilience  depletion  —  the  progressive  erosion  of  adaptive  capacity  through

sequential  disruption  events  —  as  a  mechanism  not  adequately  captured  in  existing  resilience

frameworks, which tend to treat resilience as a relatively stable organizational property rather than

a dynamically depleted resource. The secondary data strongly suggests that resilience is not a fixed

attribute of micro-enterprises but a stock that is consumed during disruptions and must be actively

replenished through both individual enterprise investment and institutional support.

With respect to the Sustainable Livelihoods Framework, the study contributes empirical

grounding for the claim that in Philippine tourism enclave contexts, social capital — particularly

the kinship-embedded forms of social capital specific to Filipino cultural contexts — functions as

the  primary  survival  asset  for  the  most  vulnerable  micro-entrepreneurs,  compensating  for

deficiencies in financial capital that the formal institutional system fails to address. This finding

has implications for the SLF's treatment of social capital as a generic asset category: the specific

cultural forms of social capital in Philippine contexts (utang na loob, bayanihan, paluwagan) have

distinctive operational logics and limitations that generic social capital concepts do not capture.

With respect to Informal Economy Theory, the study's findings reinforce and extend the

dialectical  understanding  of  informality  as  simultaneously  enabling  and  constraining.  The

secondary data documents with particular clarity the COVID-19 context revealing how informality

becomes  most  consequential  precisely  during  crisis  periods  —  when  the  need  for  institutional

support  is  greatest  and  the  exclusionary  effects  of  informal  status  are  most  damaging.  This

temporal  dynamic  —  informality  as  a  manageable  constraint  during  normal  operations  but  a

critical  vulnerability  during  disruptions  —  is  an  important  refinement  of  standard  informal

economy frameworks that tend to treat informality as a stable structural condition rather than a

dynamically significant variable.

5.3 Summary of Key Findings

In summary, the analysis of the secondary data corpus across the four research questions

yields  the  following  principal  findings.  First,  Philippine  tourism  micro-entrepreneurs  face  a

recurring,  compound  pattern  of  economic  disruptions  that  systematically  depletes  their  capital

asset bases and adaptive capacity, with the COVID-19 pandemic representing the most severe but

not  qualitatively  unique  instance  of  a  well-established  disruption  pattern.  Second,  micro-

entrepreneurs  deploy  rich  and  contextually  adaptive  survival  strategy  portfolios  that  combine

operational  contraction,  livelihood  diversification,  social  capital  activation,  and  institutional

engagement in ways that reflect their specific capital asset profiles, social network embeddedness,

and institutional access. Third, survival outcomes are highly heterogeneous and are systematically

mediated  by  geography,  sector,  formalization  status,  gender,  capitalization,  and  social  capital

depth in ways that produce structural disadvantage for the most vulnerable operators. Fourth, the

policy  and  institutional  environment  for  tourism  micro-enterprise  support  in  the  Philippines  is

fragmented, formalization-biased, and inadequately responsive to  the specific vulnerabilities of

Introduction

This  concluding  chapter  synthesizes  the  findings  of  the  study  into  a  coherent  set  of

conclusions  that  directly  address  each  of  the  four  research  questions,  articulates  the  broader

micro-enterprise resilience in the Philippines. The chapter closes with reflections on the limitations

of the current study and a research agenda for future scholarship that can build upon, test, and

extend the analytical framework developed here.

The study set out to examine, through qualitative secondary data analysis, the economic

survival strategies employed by micro-entrepreneurs operating within Philippine tourism enclaves

during periods of economic disruption. Across four chapters of analysis  — theoretical framing,

literature  review,  methodology,  and  results  —  a  consistent  and  sobering  picture  has  emerged:

Philippine tourism micro-entrepreneurs are among the most economically exposed actors in the

national economy, facing recurring and compound disruptions with severely limited capital asset

buffers, operating within a policy landscape that is structurally misaligned with their needs, yet

demonstrating  remarkable  adaptive  resourcefulness  through  the  deployment  of  culturally

embedded  social  capital  mechanisms  and  context-specific  diversification  strategies.  The

conclusions  drawn  from  this  picture  carry  implications  that  extend  well  beyond  the  academic

literature to the design of policy, the allocation of institutional resources, and the strengthening of

communities whose livelihoods depend on the vitality of Philippine tourism enclaves.

Conclusions

Conclusion 1: Philippine Tourism Micro-Entrepreneurs Face a Structurally Recurring and
Compounding Pattern of Economic Disruption (RQ1)

The first and most foundational conclusion of this study is that economic disruption is not

an exceptional or aberrant condition for Philippine tourism micro-entrepreneurs but a structurally

recurring feature of their operating environment. The secondary data analysis documents six major

disruption  events  across  the  2013–2024  study  period  —  spanning  pandemic,  natural  disaster,

policy intervention, and security crisis categories — with an average inter-disruption interval of

less than two years. This frequency of disruption means that many tourism micro-entrepreneurs

face each new shock without having fully recovered their capital asset bases from the preceding

one,  producing  a  progressive  depletion  of  adaptive  capacity  that  standard  disruption-response

frameworks  —  designed  around  the  assumption  of  a  single  shock  followed  by  a  full  recovery

period — fundamentally fail to address.

The COVID-19 pandemic represents the most severe instance of this disruption pattern but

is qualitatively continuous with it: it produced larger-scale versions of the same mechanisms —

demand  collapse,  asset  depletion,  social  capital  strain,  policy  access  barriers  —  that  natural

disasters and the Boracay closure had previously activated at smaller scales. This continuity has

important  analytical  implications:  it  means  that  the  survival  strategies  and  policy  responses

developed  in  the  context  of  natural  disaster  and  policy-induced  disruptions  provide  directly

applicable lessons for pandemic response and for the design of resilience frameworks capable of

addressing future disruption events of whatever type.

Critically, the compound disruption dynamic — the tendency for multiple events to overlap

or  occur  in  rapid  succession  —  demands  a  fundamental  rethinking  of  how  micro-enterprise

Page 59

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

resilience is conceptualized and supported. Resilience is not a fixed organizational property; it is

a dynamic stock that is consumed during disruptions and must be actively replenished between

them.  Policy  frameworks  that  respond  to  each  disruption  as  if  it  were  the  first  —  without

accounting for the cumulative depletion of micro-entrepreneur capital assets through successive

shocks — will systematically underestimate the support needed and fail to prevent the progressive

marginalization of the most vulnerable operators.

Conclusion 2: Micro-Entrepreneurs Deploy Rich, Culturally Embedded Survival Strategy
Portfolios That Are Systematically Constrained by Structural Conditions (RQ2)

The second major conclusion is that Philippine tourism micro-entrepreneurs are not passive

victims  of  economic  disruption  but  active  and  resourceful  agents  who  deploy  sophisticated

survival strategy portfolios  combining operational contraction, livelihood  diversification, social

capital activation, and selective institutional engagement. The culturally specific forms of social

capital  mobilized  in  Philippine  tourism  enclave  communities  —  utang  na  loob  networks  for

informal  credit,  bayanihan  solidarity  for  collective  resource  mobilization,  and  paluwagan

associations  for  structured  savings  accumulation  —  represent  adaptive  mechanisms  of

considerable sophistication and resilience that are not adequately recognized or supported within

existing policy frameworks.

However,  the  resourcefulness  of  micro-entrepreneurs  is  exercised  within  structural

conditions that systematically constrain the range and depth of available survival strategies. The

most  transformative  adaptive  strategies  —  livelihood  diversification  through  digital  platforms,

skills upgrading, business model restructuring, and institutional program access — are precisely

those  most  inaccessible  to  the  most  vulnerable  operators:  the  informally  registered,  the  low-

capitalized,  the  island-isolated,  and  the  women-operated.  This  structural  inequality  in  survival

Page 60

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

strategy access means that economic disruptions do not simply reduce income uniformly across

the micro-enterprise sector; they actively deepen pre-existing inequalities, concentrating market

exit among the already most vulnerable while better-resourced operators consolidate their market

positions in the post-disruption recovery.

The documentation of social capital activation strategies  — and their central role in the

survival of the most vulnerable operators — points toward a critical policy implication: the social

networks that sustain informal credit and community solidarity mechanisms are not merely cultural

assets  but  economic  infrastructure.  Policy  interventions  that  erode  these  networks  —  through

forced displacement, community fragmentation, or the substitution of community-based support

with poorly targeted institutional programs — carry real and measurable costs for micro-enterprise

survival that rarely appear in formal economic impact assessments.

Conclusion 3: The Survival Strategy Typology Reveals a Resilience Trap That Systematically
Excludes the Most Vulnerable Operators from Transformative Adaptation (RQ3)

The four-quadrant typology of survival strategies constructed in Chapter IV constitutes the

primary  theoretical  contribution  of  this  study  and  grounds  the  third  major  conclusion:  that  the

structure of the survival strategy landscape in Philippine tourism enclaves is characterized by a

resilience  trap  in  which  the  most  vulnerable  operators  are  confined  to  short-term,  non-

transformative strategies (Quadrants  I and III),  while the long-term transformative strategies in

Quadrants  II  and  IV  remain  structurally  inaccessible  to  them.  This  is  not  a  product  of

entrepreneurial failure or motivational deficiency; it is a structural consequence of the interaction

between capital asset deprivation and institutional exclusion.

The  mediating  variable  analysis  reveals  that  vulnerability  is  not  one-dimensional  but

intersectional:  operators  who  are  simultaneously  island-based,  informally  registered,  women-

Page 61

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

operated,  and  low-capitalized  face  compounding  disadvantages  that  no  single-dimension

intervention  can  address.  The  most  effective  resilience-building  interventions  will  therefore  be

those that address multiple vulnerability dimensions simultaneously — combining formalization

support  with  credit  access,  digital  skills  training  with  market  connectivity,  and  community

solidarity strengthening with institutional program access — rather than those that address single

dimensions in isolation.

The typology also illuminates an important temporal dynamic: the post-disruption recovery

period  —  not  the  crisis  peak  itself  —  is  the  critical  window  during  which  survival  or  exit  is

ultimately  determined.  Operators  who  maintain  even  minimal  operational  continuity  through

disruption  peaks  —  through  the  deployment  of  Quadrant  I  and  III  strategies  —  retain  the

organizational capabilities needed to execute Quadrant II and IV transformative strategies during

the recovery window. Policy support that arrives only during or after the peak disruption period

misses the acute survival window when Quadrant I and III strategies are being stretched to their

limits and when timely institutional support could preserve the operational continuity on which

recovery depends.

Conclusion 4: The Policy Landscape Is Structurally Misaligned with the Needs of Tourism
Micro-Entrepreneurs, Particularly Informal Operators (RQ4)

The  fourth  and  most  policy-consequential  conclusion  is  that  the  existing  policy  and

institutional  framework  for  tourism  micro-enterprise  support  in  the  Philippines  is  structurally

misaligned with the characteristics and needs of the sector it purports to serve. This misalignment

operates  along  several  dimensions  simultaneously.  The  formalization  bias  of  relief  and  credit

programs excludes the majority of enclave micro-operators from their principal mechanisms of

support.  The  fragmented  inter-agency  landscape  produces  coordination  failures  that  leave

Page 62

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

operators  falling  between  institutional  mandates.  The  disbursement  and  processing  delays  of

existing programs render assistance post-crisis for many operators, arriving after the acute survival

window has closed. And the near-complete absence of tourism-specific policy instruments — in a

country  whose  tourism  sector  is  dominated  by  informal  micro-enterprises  —  reflects  a  policy

architecture  designed  for  the  formal  economy  rather  than  the  informal  economy  that  actually

constitutes Philippine tourism at the community level.

The finding that LGU-level innovation — most clearly demonstrated in Siargao and Bohol

— consistently outperforms national program delivery in terms of speed, targeting, and community

relevance suggests that the most promising pathway for policy improvement lies not in the design

of new national programs alone but in the systematic strengthening of LGU capacity to serve as

the primary interface between tourism micro-entrepreneurs and the institutional support system.

LGUs  with  genuine  knowledge  of  their  local  micro-enterprise  populations,  embedded

relationships  with  community  organizations,  and  the  administrative  flexibility  to  adapt  support

mechanisms  to  local  conditions  are  better  positioned  than  national  agencies  to  address  the

heterogeneous, context-specific survival needs of enclave micro-entrepreneurs — provided they

are adequately resourced and mandated to do so.

Priority Recommendation: The Informal Operator Inclusion Imperative

While all recommendations in Table 7 are analytically grounded and practically actionable,

the single most consequential recommendation across all stakeholder groups is the elimination of

formalization  requirements  as  preconditions  for  crisis  relief  and  support  program  access.  This

recommendation is supported by the most consistent finding in the secondary data corpus — the

systematic  exclusion  of  the  majority  of  tourism  enclave  micro-entrepreneurs  from  government

support programs due to their informal operating status — and addresses the primary mechanism

through  which  policy  misalignment  translates  into  preventable  enterprise  exit  and  livelihood

destruction during disruptions.

Implementing this recommendation does not require the elimination of formalization as a

policy  goal;  rather,  it  requires  the  decoupling  of  formalization  from  crisis  relief  eligibility.

Operators who cannot navigate formal registration processes under normal conditions certainly

cannot do so under crisis conditions. Practical mechanisms for achieving this decoupling include:

the  acceptance  of  barangay  certification  or  community  leader  attestation  as  documentation  for

crisis  relief  access;  the  creation  of  simplified  emergency  business  registration  pathways  with

waived fees during declared disaster periods; and the use of LGU micro-enterprise registers —

which can capture informal operators not covered by DTI registration databases — as the primary

targeting instrument for crisis support delivery.

Recommendation for Future Research

Beyond the policy recommendations addressed to government agencies  and LGUs, this

study  generates  a  research  agenda  that  the  academic  community  should  prioritize  in  order  to

provide  the  primary  empirical  evidence  base  that  secondary  data  analysis  alone  cannot  supply.

Five specific research directions emerge as most urgently needed.

1.  Primary  qualitative  research  —  including  life  history  interviews,  participatory  action

research,  and  longitudinal  ethnographic  studies  —  with  tourism  micro-entrepreneurs  in

underrepresented  Philippine  destinations:  Samar-Leyte,  Camiguin,  Batanes,  Zamboanga

Peninsula, and the Caraga region. The geographic concentration of the existing literature

in  Boracay  and  Palawan  limits  the  generalizability  of  current  findings  and  leaves  the

survival  dynamics  of  micro-entrepreneurs  in  less  prominent  destinations  effectively

invisible to researchers and policy makers.

2.  Gender-disaggregated  primary  research  on  tourism  micro-enterprise  survival  in  the

Philippines, with specific attention to the intersectional vulnerabilities of women-operated

micro-enterprises  in  island  and  coastal  enclave  contexts.  The  near-complete  absence  of

Page 68

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

gender analysis in the Philippines-specific secondary corpus represents both a scholarly

lacuna and a policy blind spot with real consequences for the women who constitute the

majority of food and retail micro-enterprise operators in Philippine tourism enclaves.

3.  Longitudinal panel studies tracking tourism micro-enterprise survival, exit, and recovery

across multiple disruption cycles in the same destinations. The cross-sectional character of

most  existing  studies  prevents  the  analysis  of  the  compound  disruption  dynamics  and

resilience  depletion  processes  that  the  secondary  data  synthesis  identifies  as  central  to

understanding long-term micro-enterprise vulnerability. Longitudinal designs are essential

for capturing these temporal dynamics.

4.  Comparative studies examining the differential effectiveness of LGU-level and national-

level micro-enterprise support programs during disruptions, with specific attention to the

institutional,  fiscal,  and  governance  conditions  that  enable  LGU-level  innovation.  The

Siargao and Bohol cases suggest that local institutional capacity is the critical variable in

micro-enterprise support effectiveness, but the conditions that produce this capacity — and

the policy levers that could replicate it across more LGUs — are not yet well understood.

5.  Action research in partnership with tourism micro-enterprise communities to design, pilot,

and evaluate community-based resilience building programs that strengthen social capital

networks, build financial literacy and savings discipline, and improve digital market access

— the three intervention areas most consistently identified in the secondary data as key

enablers of Quadrant II and IV survival strategies among vulnerable operators.

Theoretical Implications and Contributions to Knowledge

Page 69

Beyond the Beach: Micro-Entrepreneurship Survival in Philippine Tourism Enclaves

Beyond its policy contributions, this study makes a number of theoretical contributions to

the  scholarly  literatures  that  frame  it  —  contributions  that  have  implications  for  how  tourism

micro-enterprise  resilience  is  conceptualized,  studied,  and  addressed  in  academic  and  policy

discourse more broadly.

The concept of compounded resilience depletion — introduced in Chapter IV and grounded

in the compound disruption dynamics documented in the secondary data — advances Resilience

Theory's  treatment  of  adaptive  capacity  as  a  dynamic  stock  rather  than  a  stable  property.  This

conceptual  refinement  has  practical  implications:  if  resilience  is  depleted  through  successive

disruptions,  then  resilience-building  is  not  a  one-time  investment  but  an  ongoing  maintenance

imperative,  and  policies  designed  to  build  resilience  only  in  response  to  individual  disruption

events  will  systematically  underinvest  in  the  between-disruption  replenishment  of  adaptive

capacity stocks.

The application of the Sustainable Livelihoods Framework to Philippine tourism enclave

micro-entrepreneurs  highlights  the distinctive role of culturally specific social capital forms  —

utang  na  loob,  bayanihan,  paluwagan  —  as  livelihood  assets  that  operate  through  mechanisms

distinct  from  the  generic  social  capital  constructs  of  Western  academic  literature.  This  finding

invites  a  broader  methodological  reflection:  the  direct  application  of  theoretical  frameworks

developed in Western or international contexts to Philippine and Southeast Asian realities requires

careful  attention  to  the  cultural  specificity  of  the  mechanisms  through  which  capital  assets  are

accumulated,  mobilized,  and  deployed.  Culturally  grounded  adaptation  of  frameworks  is  not  a

concession to particularism but a methodological necessity for generating contextually valid and

practically actionable knowledge.

The  study's  demonstration  that  qualitative  secondary  data  analysis  can  generate

substantive, policy-relevant insights about micro-entrepreneurial survival — without primary data

collection — contributes to the methodological literature on QSDA and documentary analysis in

tourism  and  entrepreneurship  research.  In  contexts  where  resource  constraints,  geographic

dispersion,  and  the  informality  of  research  subjects  make  primary  data  collection  challenging,

rigorous  QSDA  offers  a  legitimate  and  valuable  methodological  pathway  that  the  research

community should more fully recognize and deploy.

Limitations and Directions for Future Research

This study's conclusions and recommendations are offered with full acknowledgment of

the limitations that bound their scope and transferability, as detailed in Chapter I. The reliance on

secondary data means that the findings reflect the phenomena as documented by prior researchers

and  institutional  authors,  not  as  experienced  directly  by  micro-entrepreneurs  themselves.  The

geographic unevenness of the secondary data corpus — concentrated in Boracay, Palawan, and

Bohol  —  may  introduce  destination-specific  biases  into  the  typology  and  mediating  variable

analysis. The absence of primary data on gender dynamics limits the depth of gender analysis that

the study can offer, despite the clear policy importance of this dimension.

These limitations are not grounds for dismissing the study's contributions but for situating

them appropriately and for committing to the primary research agenda that can address them. The

survival strategy typology developed here is presented not as a finished theoretical product but as

a  working  analytical  framework  whose  generalizability,  completeness,  and  predictive  validity

must be tested through primary empirical research across a broader range of Philippine tourism

enclave contexts. Each of the five research directions identified in the recommendations section

above  represents  a  specific  pathway  for  building  the  primary  evidence  base  that  can  validate,

refine, or challenge the framework this study has constructed.

The broader aspiration of this work — and the aspiration that should animate the research

agenda  it  generates  —  is  the  development  of  a  genuinely  Philippine,  genuinely  community-

grounded  understanding  of  tourism  micro-enterprise  resilience:  one  that  begins  from  the  lived

realities of the tricycle drivers, sari-sari store operators, boat owners, and food stall vendors whose

livelihoods constitute the human foundation of Philippine tourism, and that generates knowledge

capable  of  strengthening  those  livelihoods  against  the  disruptions  that  are,  as  this  study  has

documented, a structurally recurring feature of the environment in which they operate.

Closing Statement

This study began with the observation that beneath the macro-level statistics of Philippine

tourism — the arrival figures, the GDP contributions, the foreign exchange receipts — lies a more

complex and more human economic reality: millions of micro-entrepreneurs in coastal and island

communities whose livelihoods are tethered to the rhythms of tourist demand and whose resilience

is tested repeatedly by disruptions they did not cause and cannot individually prevent. The analysis

conducted across five chapters has rendered that reality more visible, more analytically tractable,

and more amenable to informed policy action than it was before.

The  micro-entrepreneurs  documented  in  this  study  are  not  footnotes  to  the  Philippine

tourism economy; they are its ground floor. The sari-sari store operator in Boracay who pivoted to

community delivery during the closure, the boat operator in Siargao who organized a paluwagan

to survive the pandemic, the food stall vendor in Bohol who activated her utang na loob network

to  resume  operations  after  the  earthquake  —  these  actors  demonstrate  the  very  adaptive

resourcefulness that resilience frameworks celebrate, often without any of the institutional support

that  those  frameworks  assume.  They  deserve  a  policy  architecture  commensurate  with  their

contribution and responsive to their vulnerability. This study is offered as a contribution, however

partial, toward making that architecture a reality.
