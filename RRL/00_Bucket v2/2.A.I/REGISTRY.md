## I--Amrith_2026

### Description

Explores the financial preparations and aspirations of aging Filipino migrant domestic workers in Singapore and Hong Kong as they approach retirement, highlighting a shift toward self-responsibility for financial security.
- Financial Literacy Courses: Focuses on migrant women increasingly enrolling in 'financial literacy' courses led by various actors (state, corporate, and NGO) to prepare for precarious futures with limited social protection.
- Financialized Aspirations: Analyzes how these courses generate new financialized aspirations and cultivate a 'purpose-driven' ethos revolving around self-responsibility, which exists alongside existing interdependencies and alternative imaginaries of social protection.

## I--Ziegler_et-al_2023

### Description

Examines how individual households, consumers, and micro, small and medium enterprise (MSME) customers use digital alternative finance channels across key countries in the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) region, including the Philippines.
- Digital Lending Models: Focuses on various digital finance models, including peer-to-peer (P2P)/marketplace consumer and business lending, as well as balance-sheet consumer and business lending.

## L--Amado_2026

### Description

Examines the financial well-being, financial management practices, and coping strategies of teachers who receive salaries twice a month, providing insight into budget adherence and financial stability in a specific professional group.
- Financial Management Strategies: Identifies proactive financial management practices among teachers, such as budgeting, prioritizing essential expenses, and seeking supplementary income, which relates to cash flow patterns and budget adherence.
- Role of Financial Literacy: Concludes that financial literacy is critical for strengthening teachers' financial resilience and survival strategies, suggesting a relationship between knowledge and the ability to manage finances effectively.

## L--Bongalonta_et-al_2024

### Description

Identifies participation in “paluwagan” as a common traditional savings practice among Sorsogon State University faculty members, alongside budgeting and using piggy banks.
- Paluwagan Structure: Defines the "paluwagan" system as a traditional Rotating Savings and Credit Association (ROSCA) in the Philippines, involving group contributions and pooled funds received in turns, used for savings and access to credit.
- Paluwagan Consequences: Documents problems associated with the "paluwagan" system, including potential default rates (ranging from 5% to 50%) and the unequal distribution of resources due to the sequential nature of receiving pooled funds.

## L--Carpizo_et-al_2024

### Description

Explores the evolution of a livelihood project into the "Paluwagan Family Cooperative," documenting the transformation of a community initiative into a specific form of rotating savings and credit association (ROSCA).
- Loan Access and Crisis Support: Documents that members of the Paluwagan Family Cooperative have access to loan opportunities and describe the Paluwagan as a source of support during times of crisis, indicating its function as an informal financial mechanism and safety net.
- Economic Benefits for Members: Reveals that the cooperative offers economic benefits such as alternative income sources, loan opportunities, and purchase discounts, demonstrating the financial consequences of participation for members' budget management and income.

## L--Lopez_2025

### Description

Documents that micro-entrepreneurs activate social capital through culturally embedded mechanisms such as *utang na loob*, *bayanihan*, and *paluwagan* as part of their survival strategy portfolio.
- Identification of Paluwagan: Identifies *paluwagan* (rotating savings and credit associations) as one of the key informal financial mechanisms used by micro-entrepreneurs in Philippine tourism enclaves.
- Context of Use: Focuses on the economic survival strategies of micro-entrepreneurs, including the use of informal mechanisms, during periods of economic disruption.

## L--Rosario_2025

### Description

Documents that Bontoc households in the Philippines use informal financial mechanisms, including *paluwagan* (a rotating savings group), *supon*, and *og-ogfo*, as alternatives to mainstream financial services like banks and salary loans.
- Paluwagan Structure and Function: Describes *paluwagan* as a rotating savings and credit association (ROSCA) where members contribute a fixed amount periodically, and each member receives the lump sum in turn, providing liquidity without interest or institutional requirements, sustained solely by trust and accountability.
- Cultural Influence on Financial Behavior: Explains that financial behavior is shaped by indigenous values, kinship obligations, and collective reciprocity, with practices like *paluwagan* and *og-ogfo* operating through mutual trust and shared responsibility, relying on reputation and reciprocity instead of collateral to access resources.
