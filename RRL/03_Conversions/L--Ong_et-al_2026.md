ABSTRACT
This study examines how myopic decision-making influences the business performance of
micro-enterprises operated by low-income households in Manila, with access to finance as
a  moderating  variable.  The  research  addresses  the  critical  question:  How  does  myopic
influence  micro-enterprise  business
decision-making  of
performance when moderated by access to finance? Through quantitative analysis of micro-
entrepreneurs  in  Manila,  the  study  reveals  that  myopic  decision-making  significantly
impacts business performance (p-value < 0.001), particularly when entrepreneurs prioritize
immediate rewards over long-term  benefits.  Access  to  finance of low-income households
have a significant positive effect on the business performance of micro-enterprises (p-value
= < 0.001). The research finds that proper access to finance can moderate the negative effects
of myopic decision-making on business outcomes (p-value = 0.005). The study concludes
that micro-entrepreneurs from low-income households often make short-sighted decisions
and have financial constraints due to immediate survival pressures, which hinder business
growth  and  perpetuate  economic  vulnerability.  Therefore,  developing  targeted  financial
literacy programs for micro-entrepreneurs, improving access to formal financial institutions,
implementing government support systems for micro-enterprises, and creating interventions
focused on long-term business planning are some measures that low-income households with
micro-enterprises need to aim to help them overcome myopic decision-making tendencies
and enhance their business performance through better access to financial resources.

1. INTRODUCTION

1.1 Background of the Study

Being  a  third-world  country,  a  notable  portion  of  the  population  in  the  Philippines  lives
below the poverty line and low-income households. According to a survey conducted by the
Social Weather Stations in December 2023, approximately 13 million Filipino households,
or 47% of the population, still identify as poor (Piatos, 2024). For many of these individuals,
micro-enterprises  and  self-employment  serve  as  their  means  of  livelihood  to  secure  their
necessities in life (Orbeta et al., 2020). These small businesses provide a source of income
and economic stability for families who might have limited employment opportunities. The
low-income  household’s  reliance  on  small-scale  businesses  explains  the  prevalence  of
numerous  small  businesses  in  the  country.  These  micro-enterprises  are  usually  small
neighborhood convenience stores, street vendors, shoemakers, and other similar ventures.

Undoubtedly, MSMEs play a vital role in the economic landscape of the city of Manila, the
country’s capital city. Micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) play a crucial role in
the  Philippine  economy,  accounting  for  99.59%,  generating  62.4%  of  the  country's  total
employment, contributing 36% of gross value added, and accounting for 25% of total exports
in 2022, according to the Philippine Statistics Authority’s (PSA) List of Establishments (LE).
Of the registered business establishments, 90.48% constitute micro-enterprises (Department
of  Trade  and  Industry,  2022).  Although  these  enterprises  are  relatively  small  in  terms  of
capital, workforce, and revenue, they are the backbone of the local economy. In the city of
Manila, where the population density and economic activity are among the highest in the
country, MSMEs are essential for providing goods and services that cater to the diverse needs
of the urban population. As previously stated, they range from small retail shops and service
providers to more sophisticated businesses, contributing to the area's vibrant and dynamic
economy.

These  micro-enterprises  are  mainly  characterized  by  a  hands-on,  owner-driven  approach
(Alemda  &  Pobre,  2012).  This  means  that  these  enterprises  are  often  managed  by  the
business owners themselves. They play the role of both the strategic decision-makers and
operational  managers.  However,  these  enterprises’  expansion  and  sustainability  are  often
limited due to inadequate formal access to finance. They face numerous hurdles they have
to  overcome  to  obtain  proper  access  to  finance,  such  as  extensive  documentation
requirements,  minimum  loan  amounts,  and  difficulty  in  getting  approved.  Due  to  these
laborious  processes,  micro-entrepreneurs  pursue  alternative  informal  financing  sources.
These substitutes are usually loans taken from their families or friends, which typically come
with higher interest rates compared to formal lending institutions. However, this also comes
at a cost, as this will further hinder the growth of these enterprises, as micro-entrepreneurs
who borrow at higher rates also have to pay more expensive fees. This lessens their capital
to reinvest into their business, making it harder for them to expand as they also have to pay
daily for necessities.

Having proper access to finance significantly influences the growth and performance of all
businesses  as  it  allows  business  owners  to  have  extra  capital  to  expand  and  boost  the
performance  of  their  businesses.  However,  the  microentrepreneurs’  challenging  way  of
having access to proper finance institutions only makes it hard for them to deal with their
micro-enterprises  and  their  lives.  Their  lack  of  liquidity  prohibits  them  from  exploring
numerous opportunities that could improve their way of life.

Their challenges do not stop with having access to finance; another factor that prohibits them
from boosting their business performance is myopic decision-making. Myopia, in business,
is a phenomenon where short-term gains are prioritized over long-term benefits, significantly
impacting business performance. The tendency to favor immediate rewards often leads to
decisions that, while beneficial in the short run, can be detrimental to the business’s future
stability  and  growth.  This  phenomenon  has  been  widely  studied  in  various  contexts,
including entrepreneurship, corporate finance, and environmental decision-making. For low-
income  individuals,  the  constant  focus  on  prioritizing  short-term  needs  will  prevent
microentrepreneurs from making strategic long-term investments and decisions that could
help their businesses flourish. Additionally, spatial myopia, or one’s failure to consider the
macroenvironment,  further  intensifies  the  negative  effects  of  myopic  decision-making  on
businesses.

Myopic decision-making can be aggravated by resource scarcity, financial constraints, and
the  pressing  need  to  address  immediate  survival  concerns.  Continuing  to  pursue  this
cognitive bias will eventually make the growth of an enterprise stagnant or worse. In Metro
Manila, where many micro-entrepreneurs operate under financial constraints and immediate
survival pressures, this short-termism is prevalent. This results in inadequate savings, poor
financial planning, and insufficient money to reinvest into the business. These factors hinder
business growth and perpetuate the poverty cycle and economic vulnerability among micro-
entrepreneurs in low-income households.

entrepreneurs and assess how these decisions impact the performance of their enterprises.

1.6 Scope and Limitation

Micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) play a vital role in the Philippine economy,
accounting for over 99% of all business establishments in the country. In particular, micro-
enterprises are defined as businesses with assets under ₱3 million and less than 10 employees.
Many of these micro-enterprises are located in the city of Manila. However, myopic decision-
making and lack of access to formal financing institutions can affect these enterprises' business
performance and growth. This study aims to analyze the impact of myopic decision-making
by  micro-entrepreneurs  from  low-income  households  on  the  business  performance  of  their
micro-enterprises, with access to finance as a moderating variable.

This study focuses on low-income households that own and operate micro-enterprises within
the urban areas of the city of Manila. It aims to analyze the impact of myopic decision-making
by micro-entrepreneurs on the business performance of their micro-enterprises. In addition,
the study explores access to finance as a moderating factor between myopic decision-making
and business performance, while also examining the influence of external factors on myopic
behavior. The socio-economic context of the city of Manila is also considered for its potential
influence on myopic decision-making and micro-enterprise performance. However, the scope
of the study explicitly excludes micro-enterprises located outside of Manila, respondents who
do not operate sari-sari stores, and households that do not fall under the low-income threshold.
It also excludes articles that investigates the relationship between myopic decision-making,
access to finance, and business performance, as no prior work has investigated the combined
effect and interaction of these three factors simultaneously. Additionally, the study does not
cover other factors beyond myopic decision-making and access to finance that may impact
micro-enterprise performance. Medium or large-scale enterprises that do not qualify as micro-
enterprises are excluded, and the analysis does not rely on comprehensive historical financial

2. REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE

This section of this research focuses on an in-depth review of the literature on the challenges
and  factors  influencing  minor  and  small  enterprises  (MSEs)  in  low-income  countries,
particularly focusing on access to finance and myopic decision-making. It emphasizes the
critical role of access to finance in determining the performance and growth of MSEs from
low-income households. Entrepreneurs from this socioeconomic class often face barriers to
accessing formal loaning institutions like banks, which hinders their ability to invest in their
businesses and respond to possible market opportunities. This lack of access is attributed to
several factors, including strict collateral requirements, lack of financial documentation, and
the perception of MSEs as high-risk borrowers.

This section also focuses on exploring the phenomenon of myopic decision-making among
low-income  entrepreneurs.  This  is  because  poverty  and  resource  scarcity  are  linked  to
tendencies to prioritize immediate gratification over long-term benefits, which is driven by
uncertainty about the future and pressing financial needs. This leads to suboptimal financial
choices that prohibit their efforts in building creditworthiness and securing formal financing
from formal financial services.

Oftentimes, the only choice left for low-income entrepreneurs is informal financial services.
Usually, although these are more costly and exploitative, they are the only option for them
due to their exclusion from formal financial institutions. This highlights informal financing,
such as high interest rates, which slows their enterprises from growing at a controlled rate.
Furthermore,  the  lack  of  awareness  of  these  individuals  regarding  the  benefits  of  formal
financial  services  and  the  absence  of  financial  literacy  further  contribute  to  their  being
included in loaning institutions that could make their enterprises earn more.

It is important to note that although many studies have been conducted to examine access to
finance and myopic decision-making separately, there is a lack of comprehensive research
integrating these concepts and their impact on business performance, particularly in micro-
enterprises owned by low-income households.

3.3 Operational Definition of Terms

•  Access  to  Finance  -  Access  to  finance  is  defined  as  the  availability  of  financial
services  in  the  form  of  demand  deposits,  credit,  payments,  or  insurance  (Ye  &
Kulathunga, 2019). For Bikrenmaier & Fu (2017), access to finance is the household
use of formal financial services, including the ability to access transactions, credit,
and investment products and services from formal financial institutions.

•  Access to Formal Lending Institutions - Access to Formal Lending Institutions are
institutions wherein they are regulated by the government and offer financial services
such as credit or loans and investment products (Birkenmaier & Fu, 2017). Formal
lending institutions typically require collateral and documentation in order to have
access to a credit line.

•  Access  to  Informal  Credit  -  Informal  credits  are  fundings  from  informal  or  non-
institutional  financial  firms  considered  Alternative  Financial  Services  like  payday
loans and pawnshop loans—and although more costly and unfavorable (Birkenmaier
& Fu, 2017), offer easier access due to limited collateral, low loan amounts, and short
repayment tenures.

•  Barriers to Access - Amadasun & Mutezo (2022) define access barriers as critical
factors that constrain SMEs from obtaining and understanding financial resources for
growth, with collateral requirements and financial literacy further hindering access
to sufficient and affordable capital (Osano & Languitone, 2016).

•  Business Performance - Business performance is a holistic and evolving measure of
an organization’s effectiveness, efficiency, and success in achieving short- and long-
term  objectives—encompassing  financial  health,  operational  efficiency,  customer
satisfaction, market competitiveness, and strategic goal attainment (Franco-Santos et
al.,  n.d.)—and  requires  both  financial  and  non-financial  metrics  for  assessment
(Napwanya & Chinyamurindi, 2021), enabling organizations to adapt, improve, and
make data-driven decisions (Taouab & Issor, 2019).

•  Collateral Requirements - Collateral requirements are one of the barriers to access to
finance. Collateral requirements are usually properties or other valuable items that
are used to guarantee the repayment of the loan or credit. The collateral is usually
100% or more of the credit or loan amount (Osano & Languitone, 2016).

•  Competitive  Myopia  -  Competitive  myopia,  one  of  four  dimensions  of  strategic
myopia,  involves  a  narrow  focus  on  immediate  threats  from  nearby  competitors,
often  causing  managers  to  misidentify  competitors,  overlook  less  obvious  but

•  Temporal  Myopia  -  Temporal  myopia,  a  dimension  of  strategic  myopia,  is
characterized by a preference for short-term outcomes, leading managers to prioritize
immediate financial objectives at the expense of long-term investments and strategic
capability development (Czakon et al., 2023).

6. CONCLUSION & RECOMMENDATION

The  study  examines  how  myopic  decision-making  affects  micro-enterprises  in  Manila,
particularly focusing on short-term gains over long-term objectives. This pattern is especially
prevalent  among  micro-entrepreneurs  from  low-income  households,  who  frequently
prioritize immediate financial needs over strategic growth investments.

The impact of myopic decision-making on micro-entrepreneurs in Manila is evident through
four distinct types of behavior that significantly affect their business operations. Competitive
myopia affects 30% of respondents who sometimes ignore competitors' bargaining power,
while 21% frequently overlook competitive forces. Cooperative myopia manifests in 33%
of  businesses  rarely  considering  collaboration  opportunities  with  potential  partners.
Temporal myopia is evident in 38% of respondents reducing human resources spending for
immediate gains, and 44% cutting R&D expenditures. Learning myopia appears in 37% of
participants who disregard long-term objectives.

Financial  access  and  business  performance  are  closely  linked,  with  access  to  finance
emerging  as  a  critical  factor  in  entrepreneurial  success.  Micro-entrepreneurs  with  limited
access to formal financing typically rely on informal credit sources, including family loans
and local moneylenders, often with exploitative terms. Those who secure formal financing
through savings accounts, small loans, or government grants show a greater likelihood of
investing in staff training, customer acquisition, and product improvements.

The long-term impact of combining myopic decision-making with limited financial access
creates  a  cycle  that  hinders  sustainable  business  growth.  Entrepreneurs  under  constant
financial  pressure  tend  to  make  decisions  favoring  immediate  rewards  over  long-term
investments. This approach not only reduces business growth potential but also reinforces
dependency  on  short-term  financial  strategies  that  weaken  competitive  positioning  and
operational resilience.

Recommendations  for  micro-entrepreneurs  include  participating  in  MSME  development
programs  through  DTI  and  LGU  offices,  developing  comprehensive  three-year  business
plans, and implementing proper financial record-keeping systems. For sari-sari store owners
specifically,  utilizing  Sari.PH  Pro,  a  free  ERP  mobile  application,  can  provide  digital
inventory  tracking,  sales  monitoring,  and  customer  credit  recording  capabilities.  This
platform has demonstrated success with ₱8 billion in sales from over 8 million transactions
in 2023, showing 20% year-over-year growth.
