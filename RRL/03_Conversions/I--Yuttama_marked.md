ABSTRACT
Objective: Examine how e-payment usage influences consumer spending and cash management, and
assess the moderating role of financial literacy in an emerging-market context. Research Design &
Methods: Quantitative survey of active e-payment users in Central Java, Indonesia. Constructs were
measured with Likert-scale items. Relationships among e-payment usage, consumer behavior, cash
management, and financial literacy (moderator) were analyzed using PLS-SEM with established
validity and reliability procedures. Findings: E-payment facilitates more frequent and effortless
purchasing by lowering psychological frictions at checkout, which can elevate impulsive spending. At
the same time, its built-in records and dashboards support better oversight of income–expense flows,
budgeting, and planning. Financial literacy shapes these outcomes: higher literacy helps curb excessive
spending and strengthens the use of app features for monitoring, budgeting, and goal setting. Overall,
the impact of e-payment is dual and contingent on users’ financial knowledge and self-regulation.
Implications & Recommendations: Pair cashless initiatives with digital financial-literacy programs.
Embed in-app nudges such as spend alerts, weekly summaries, category caps, and goal tracking. Tailor
guidance for low-literacy segments and encourage responsible defaults. Contribution & Value
Added: Provides emerging-market evidence clarifying when and how e-payments promote or
undermine financial discipline, integrates behavioral-finance and financial-literacy perspectives via
moderation analysis, and offers actionable design and policy insights to enhance consumer financial
well-being.

economies, many governments and financial institutions worldwide are actively promoting digital
payment adoption to enhance financial inclusion and economic efficiency.

The widespread adoption of e-payment systems has raised concerns about their influence on consumer
behavior and personal financial management. Digital payments have been linked to increased consumer
spending, as they reduce the psychological barriers associated with cash transactions, leading to higher
impulsivity and lower spending awareness, as noted by Hampson et al. (2021). On the other hand, e-
payment systems offer enhanced financial tracking tools, allowing users to monitor their expenses and
manage their cash flow effectively, which aligns with findings from Liu & Zhang (2021). However, the
extent to which e-payment encourages excessive spending or improves financial discipline remains
inconclusive, particularly in emerging markets like Indonesia.
While prior studies have largely treated e-payment as a direct driver of spending or cash management
outcomes, this relationship may not be uniform across all consumers. The influence of e-payment on
financial behavior depends substantially on individual differences in financial literacy. Financial literacy
acts as a moderating mechanism that determines how consumers interpret, evaluate, and respond to the
convenience and automation of digital transactions (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014; OECD, 2018).

Individuals with higher literacy levels are more likely to leverage e-payment functions such as
transaction records, budget reminders, and spending analytics for informed decision-making and self-
control (Fernandes et al., 2014; Zhavoronok et al., 2022). Conversely, those with lower financial literacy
may perceive digital payment ease as an invitation to spend impulsively, lacking the awareness to

Moreover, financial literacy has been identified as a key factor influencing financial decision-making,
yet its role in moderating the effects of e-payment usage on consumer behavior and cash management
remains underexplored. Individuals with higher financial literacy may utilize e-payment tools for better
financial control, whereas those with limited financial knowledge may struggle with over-spending and
poor money management (Graña-Alvarez et al., 2024). Understanding how financial literacy moderates
the relationship between e-payment usage, spending behavior, and cash management is crucial for
designing effective financial policies and consumer education programs.
Several studies have examined the impact of digital payments on consumer behavior and financial
management, yet key gaps remain in the literature. Usman et al. (2025) explored the relationship
between digital payment adoption and consumer spending, highlighting that e-payment facilitates
impulse buying but did not assess its impact on financial planning or savings behavior. Laukkanen
(2016) investigated consumer adoption of mobile and internet banking but did not examine how digital
payment behavior affects personal cash management. Liu & Zhang (2021) discussed the opportunities
and challenges of digital payments in financial management, yet their study lacked an empirical analysis
of consumer financial habits in developing economies like Indonesia. Furthermore, Hampson et al.
(2021); Ly & Ly (2024) provided theoretical and empirical insights into how digital payment
mechanisms influence spending behavior and trust, but limited research has explored the moderating
role of financial literacy in shaping these effects. This study addresses this gap by examining how
financial literacy influences the relationship between e-payment usage, spending behavior, and cash
management in Central Java, offering new insights into how digital financial behavior varies across
different consumer segments.
This study aims to bridge the existing research gap by investigating the influence of e-payment usage
on consumer behavior and cash management, with a specific focus on the moderating role of financial
literacy. The key research objectives are to examine the impact of e-payment usage on consumer
spending behavior, particularly in terms of impulse purchases and financial awareness, to analyze the
effect of e-payment on cash management by assessing whether digital payment systems enhance
financial discipline or lead to weaker financial control, and to evaluate the moderating role of financial
literacy in determining whether financially literate individuals mitigate the risks of overspending and
maximize the benefits of e-payment for financial planning.
This study makes significant contributions to both theoretical and practical domains. Theoretically, it
extends the literature on behavioral economics and financial literacy theory by providing empirical
evidence on how e-payment influences financial behavior. Unlike previous studies, this research
introduces financial literacy as a key moderating factor, offering new insights into how knowledge and
awareness shape digital financial decisions. From a practical perspective, the findings provide valuable
implications for policymakers, financial institutions, and e-payment providers. Governments and
financial regulators can use these insights to develop targeted financial literacy programs aimed at
promoting responsible digital spending habits. Banks and FinTech companies can leverage these
findings to design smarter financial management tools, such as spending alerts, automated budgeting
features, and personalized financial education within e-wallet applications. Furthermore, consumers can
benefit from a better understanding of how e-payment behaviors impact their financial well-being,
enabling them to make informed financial choices in an increasingly digital economy.

LITERATURE REVIEW
A Boundary-Condition View
Previous studies on digital payments have predominantly adopted a linear model, assuming a
straightforward relationship between e-payment usage and financial outcomes such as consumer
spending or cash management (Usman et al., 2025). While these studies have established that e-payment
increases convenience and transaction efficiency, the results regarding its behavioral impact remain
inconclusive. Some researchers found that e-payment stimulates impulsive spending by lowering the
psychological “pain of paying” (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008), whereas others emphasized its potential to
enhance budgeting and cash monitoring through digital transaction records (Liu & Zhang, 2021; Ly &
Ly, 2024). These inconsistencies indicate that the linear approach cannot fully explain why e-payment
may lead to both overspending and improved financial discipline across different user groups.
This limitation presents a significant research opportunity. Rather than assuming uniform behavioral
effects, it is essential to consider conditional factors that may alter how e-payment influences consumer
decisions. Prior research rarely integrates psychological and cognitive determinants such as financial
knowledge, awareness, and self control into the model (Fernandes et al., 2014; Liu & Zhang, 2021).
Consequently, there remains a gap in understanding when and for whom e-payment promotes financial
well-being or financial risk.
To address this gap, the present study introduces financial literacy as a moderating construct, positioning
it as a boundary condition that determines the strength and direction of e-payment’s behavioral effects.
Financial literacy is not only a knowledge-based capability but also a self-regulatory mechanism that
enables individuals to interpret financial information, evaluate risks, and make informed digital
transactions (OECD, 2018). Individuals with high financial literacy are expected to utilize e-payment
tools (e.g., spending dashboards, alerts, and budgeting features) to support better financial control, while
those with low literacy may exhibit impulsive or excessive spending patterns.
Theoretically, this research integrates two complementary perspectives. Behavioral Economics Theory
explains how reduced transaction friction and lower payment salience in digital systems can distort
spending judgment and increase purchase frequency (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008). Meanwhile, Financial
Literacy Theory posits that financial knowledge enhances rational decision-making, budgeting, and
savings behavior (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014). By combining these frameworks, the study proposes a
moderated theoretical model, where financial literacy serves as a boundary condition that shapes
whether e-payment adoption leads to impulsive spending or disciplined cash management.
This integrated model extends previous linear frameworks by explicitly mapping the research gap
between technology-driven convenience and human cognitive control, thereby contributing a more
nuanced explanation of digital financial behavior in emerging markets such as Indonesia. It also
establishes the conceptual foundation for the study’s hypotheses and structural model, which empirically
test how financial literacy moderates the dual behavioral effects of e-payment usage.
E-Payment and Consumer Behavior
The adoption of electronic payment (e-payment) systems has transformed how consumers engage in
financial transactions, shifting purchasing behaviors from cash-based spending to digital transactions
(Dahlberg et al., 2015). Previous studies suggest that e-payment increases transaction frequency and
impulse buying by reducing the psychological friction associated with spending money (Hampson et al.,
2021). Unlike cash transactions, which involve a tangible exchange of money, digital payments create
a psychological detachment from financial loss, making it easier for consumers to make frequent and
unplanned purchases. This phenomenon aligns with Behavioral Economics Theory, which argues that

the "pain of paying" is minimized in digital transactions, leading to higher spending tendencies (Thaler
& Sunstein, 2008).
Usman et al. (2025) found that the convenience of e-payment systems increases consumer confidence
in spending, particularly in the context of online retail and mobile commerce. Similarly, Laukkanen
(2016) highlighted that digital payment adoption is driven by ease of use, transaction speed, and
perceived security, which encourage consumers to transition from traditional payment methods.
However, while e-payment facilitates economic transactions, its influence on spending control and
financial awareness remains a subject of debate. Some studies suggest that e-payment can lead to
financial mismanagement, particularly among individuals with low financial literacy, as they may
engage in excessive discretionary spending without tracking their transactions effectively. A consistent
body of work finds that card-based, mobile, and e-wallet payments lower psychological barriers at
checkout (reduced salience, deferred pain, one-tap convenience), which is associated with higher basket
sizes and greater incidence of impulse purchases compared with cash. This effect is often stronger in
settings where payment is instantaneous and authentication is streamlined. Nevertheless, heterogeneity
emerges across user segments and contexts, suggesting that individual capabilities and self regulatory
resources can moderate the magnitude of the spending increase. Building on this stream, we treat e-
payment usage as a driver of greater spending propensity via friction-reduction channels.

E-Payment and Cash Management
In contrast to concerns that digital transactions increase spending, research has also highlighted the
positive impact of e-payment on financial management. Liu & Zhang (2021) found that e-payment
adoption enables users to track their spending patterns more effectively, as digital transactions provide
automated records, categorized expense tracking, and financial alerts. These features enhance budgeting,
cash flow monitoring, and long-term financial planning. Unlike cash-based transactions, which require
manual tracking, e-payment systems integrate real-time financial data, allowing users to analyze their
spending habits and adjust their financial decisions accordingly.
Furthermore, digital transactions encourage structured financial behavior by offering tools such as
spending limits, automated bill payments, and financial goal-setting features. Research by Ly & Ly
(2024) emphasized that e-payment users exhibit higher financial self-awareness, particularly when
leveraging digital banking applications with personalized financial insights. However, the extent to
which e-payment enhances financial control depends on user engagement with financial management
tools, as individuals who do not actively monitor their transactions may still face financial discipline
challenges. Beyond convenience, e-payment ecosystems supply automated tracking (timestamps,
merchant/category tags, dashboards, alerts) that can scaffold budgeting, reconciliation, and bill
planning. Empirical studies link digital transaction records to improved monitoring and planning
behaviors, although the strength of these benefits depends on how effectively users interpret and act on
the information surfaced by apps. We therefore conceptualize e-payment usage as enabling better cash
management through information visibility and routine prompts.

The Moderating Role of Financial Literacy
Financial literacy has been widely recognized as a critical factor in financial decision-making,
influencing how individuals manage their expenses, savings, and investment choices. According to
Financial Literacy Theory Lusardi & Mitchell (2014), individuals with higher financial knowledge are
more likely to engage in responsible financial behaviors, such as budgeting, expense tracking, and
informed spending decisions. In the context of e-payment adoption, financial literacy serves as a
moderating factor that determines whether digital transactions lead to financial discipline or excessive
spending (OECD, 2018).
Zhavoronok et al. (2022) found that financially literate individuals are more likely to utilize e-payment
tools strategically, leveraging digital tracking features to optimize their cash flow and spending patterns.

METHODS
Research Design
This study employs a quantitative research approach to examine the causal relationships between e-
payment usage, consumer behavior, and cash management, with financial literacy as a moderating
variable. A survey-based research design was utilized to systematically collect and analyze empirical
data, ensuring objectivity in measuring the proposed relationships. A structured questionnaire was
distributed to residents of Central Java who actively use electronic payment systems. The study follows
a positivist paradigm, enabling statistical generalization of the findings to a broader population. Through
hypothesis testing, this research aims to provide empirical evidence on how e-payment adoption
influences financial behavior and how financial literacy moderates these effects (Lusardi & Mitchell,
2014; Putri et al., 2024).
Research Location
The study was conducted in Central Java, Indonesia, a region experiencing rapid growth in digital
financial services. The increasing government initiatives promoting cashless transactions make Central
Java a relevant case study for understanding the behavioral and financial implications of e-payment
adoption (Wiranatakusuma & Latief, 2024). The study focused on individuals who regularly engage in
digital transactions, as they provide valuable insights into the influence of electronic payments on
consumption patterns and personal cash management.
Population and Sampling
The target population comprises individuals in Central Java who use e-payment systems for daily
transactions. Given the study’s objectives, a purposive sampling technique was employed to ensure that
participants met specific criteria. Eligible respondents were required to be at least 18 years old, ensuring
that they had financial decision-making autonomy (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014), and to be active users of
e-payment services, including mobile banking, e-wallets, or digital payment platforms.

Pre-Test and Instrument Validation
Before conducting the full-scale survey, a pre-test was carried out with 67 respondents to evaluate the
clarity, reliability, and validity of the questionnaire. Based on pre-test feedback, minor revisions were
made to ensure that all items were clearly understood by respondents (Presser et al., 2004). Additionally,
a Focus Group Discussion (FGD) was conducted with financial experts and researchers to refine
ambiguous items, ensuring that the instrument accurately captured respondents’ perceptions (Krueger,
2014).
To ensure the validity and reliability of the measurement instruments, multiple statistical tests were
performed. Convergent validity was assessed using Average Variance Extracted (AVE), with a
minimum threshold of 0.50 to confirm that the items within each construct were sufficiently correlated.
Discriminant validity was examined using the Fornell-Larcker criterion and the Heterotrait-Monotrait
(HTMT) ratio, ensuring that constructs were empirically distinct from one another (Henseler et al.,
2015). Reliability analysis was conducted using Cronbach’s Alpha and Composite Reliability (CR), with
a threshold of 0.70 or higher, indicating consistent internal reliability across the measured variables.

Data Analysis Technique
The study employed Partial Least Squares - Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM) for data analysis,
using SmartPLS 4 software. This method was chosen due to its capability to handle complex models
with latent constructs and its suitability for analyzing moderating effects (Hair et al., 2021; Sarstedt et
al., 2014).
Data analysis began with an examination of the measurement model (outer model) to verify that the
observed indicators accurately reflected their respective latent constructs. Factor loadings were assessed
to ensure that each indicator contributed significantly to its construct (Hair et al., 2021). The structural
model (inner model) was then evaluated to examine the strength and direction of the relationships
between variables. Path coefficients were analyzed to determine the statistical significance of the
proposed hypotheses, while the coefficient of determination (R²) was used to measure the proportion of
variance explained by the independent variables. Effect size (ꬵ2) was also calculated to assess the
practical significance of predictor variables.
Hypothesis  testing  was  conducted  using  Bootstrapping  with  5,000  resamples  to  determine  the
significance of relationships within the model. Hypotheses were considered statistically supported if the
t-statistic exceeded 1.96 and the p-value was less than 0.05 (Henseler et al., 2015). Furthermore, a
moderation analysis was performed to examine whether financial literacy strengthened or weakened the
relationships  between  e-payment  usage,  consumer  behavior,  and  cash  management  (Chin,  2009;
Sarstedt et al., 2019).

DISCUSSION
To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to comprehensively examine the dual role of e-
payment usage in shaping consumer behavior and cash management, while also investigating financial
literacy as a moderating factor. By employing PLS-SEM analysis on a large sample of respondents in
Central Java, Indonesia, this study provides empirical evidence that, while e-payment adoption increases
spending behavior, it also enhances financial management efficiency. Furthermore, our findings
highlight that financial literacy serves as a critical buffer, mitigating the risk of excessive spending and
maximizing the benefits of digital financial tools. These results contribute to the ongoing discourse in
behavioral economics, digital finance, and financial literacy research, offering both theoretical
advancements and practical recommendations for consumers, policymakers, and financial institutions.
Interpretation of Key Findings
The Influence of E-Payment on Consumer Behavior
The findings reveal that e-payment usage significantly increases consumer spending, supporting the
notion that digital payment systems encourage higher transaction frequency and impulse purchasing.
This result aligns with Behavioral Economics Theory (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008), which posits that
individuals experience less "pain of paying" when using digital payment methods compared to cash.
The psychological detachment from physical money reduces the cognitive awareness of expenditure,
leading consumers to spend more effortlessly (Prelec & Loewenstein, 1998).
These findings are consistent with prior research on digital payment adoption and impulse buying
behavior (Usman et al., 2025). The frictionless nature of e-payment, combined with instant transaction
processing, alters financial decision-making patterns, making consumers more likely to make unplanned
purchases. Moreover, e-payment platforms often incorporate reward-based mechanisms, such as
cashback incentives and promotional discounts, which further reinforce spending behavior (Liébana-
Cabanillas et al., 2018). The instant gratification effect associated with digital transactions creates a
habit loop, where frequent users become conditioned to making purchases with minimal financial
reflection.
However, the impact of e-payment on spending behavior is not uniform across all users. The results
indicate that financially literate individuals exhibit greater spending control despite using e-payment
systems. This suggests that the ability to critically evaluate financial decisions serves as a protective
mechanism, preventing users from engaging in excessive or impulsive spending (Lusardi & Mitchell,
2014). Unlike financially illiterate individuals, who may perceive e-payment as a tool for convenience
and consumption, financially literate users are more likely to view digital transactions as part of a
broader financial strategy.
The Influence of E-Payment on Cash Management
In contrast to concerns that e-payment reduces financial discipline, our findings suggest that e-payment
usage positively affects cash management, enhancing an individual’s ability to track, analyze, and
regulate financial transactions. This result aligns with Liu & Zhang (2021), who argued that digital
financial tools improve expenditure monitoring and budgeting accuracy. Unlike cash transactions, which
lack automated tracking features, e-payment systems provide real-time expenditure records, categorized
spending breakdowns, and predictive financial analytics, enabling users to develop more structured
financial habits (Liébana-Cabanillas et al., 2018).
Our findings indicate that e-payment users engage in more systematic financial planning, leveraging
digital tracking tools to enhance financial awareness. The availability of transaction histories allows
users to identify spending patterns, set budgeting constraints, and adjust financial decisions accordingly
(Mora, 2022). This contradicts the traditional belief that cash-based spending leads to superior financial
management; rather, digital transaction records facilitate a data-driven approach to personal finance,
allowing individuals to make informed decisions based on actual expenditure trends (Lusardi &
Mitchell, 2014).

However, potential drawbacks exist. Over-reliance on digital financial tracking may reduce manual
financial discipline, as users may become less engaged in active financial decision-making and instead
rely solely on automated tools for budgeting. This aligns with the concern raised by Alshebami & Al
Marri (2022) that digital financial platforms may create a false sense of financial control, leading users
to overlook long-term financial planning considerations.
The Moderating Role of Financial Literacy
A key contribution of this study is the identification of financial literacy as a moderating factor that
influences the relationship between e-payment, consumer behavior, and cash management. The results
indicate that higher financial literacy weakens the positive relationship between e-payment and
consumer spending, meaning that individuals with greater financial knowledge are less susceptible to
the impulsive spending tendencies associated with digital transactions. This finding aligns with
Financial Literacy Theory (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014), which posits that financial knowledge improves
self-control, decision-making, and long-term financial planning.
Our findings corroborate prior studies that suggest financially literate individuals are more likely to use
budgeting tools effectively (Fernandes et al., 2014; Zhavoronok et al., 2022). Unlike financially
uneducated users, who may use e-payment passively, financially literate users actively engage with
financial management features, such as spending categorization, automated budgeting, and transaction
alerts. This enhances their ability to regulate expenses, optimize savings, and make informed financial
choices.
Furthermore, financial literacy strengthens the positive effect of e-payment on cash management,
suggesting that knowledgeable individuals maximize the financial advantages offered by digital
financial systems. While e-payment itself provides tools for financial management, its effectiveness
depends on the user’s ability to interpret and utilize financial information strategically (OECD, 2018).
CONCLUSION
This study provides empirical evidence on the dual influence of e-payment usage on consumer behavior
and cash management, highlighting the moderating role of financial literacy. The findings indicate that
digital payment systems, while reducing the psychological barriers to spending and encouraging
impulsive purchases, also enhance users’ ability to monitor, plan, and manage their finances through
automated tracking and budgeting features. Crucially, financial literacy shapes these behavioral
outcomes: individuals with higher literacy demonstrate stronger self-control and use digital tools more
effectively for financial discipline, whereas those with lower literacy are more prone to impulsive and
unplanned spending.
Theoretically, this study enriches the literature on behavioral finance, consumer psychology, and
financial literacy by introducing a moderated model that integrates behavioral, cognitive, and
technological perspectives. The results extend Behavioral Economics Theory by confirming that the
reduced “pain of paying” in digital transactions drives higher spending tendencies. At the same time,
they strengthen Financial Literacy Theory by positioning literacy not merely as a form of knowledge
but as a self-regulatory mechanism that mitigates financial risks and enhances decision-making in digital
environments. This study thus challenges the conventional assumption that e-payment inherently
undermines financial discipline, showing instead that its impact depends on the interplay between
financial literacy and user self-regulation.
Practically, the findings offer valuable insights for multiple stakeholders. For consumers, cultivating
digital financial literacy is essential to optimize the benefits of e-payment while maintaining spending
control. For policymakers, the results underscore the importance of integrating financial education and
digital literacy initiatives within broader financial inclusion programs. Governments and financial
institutions should collaborate to design targeted interventions that foster responsible digital financial
behavior. For e-payment providers and banks, the study highlights opportunities to embed behavioral
design features such as spending alerts, automated budgeting recommendations, and gamified savings
incentives into digital platforms to promote user engagement and financial well-being.

Despite its contributions, the study is subject to several limitations. Its geographical focus on Central
Java restricts the generalizability of findings to regions with different financial cultures and
technological infrastructures. The reliance on self-reported data may also introduce social desirability
bias, leading to underreporting of impulsive behavior. Furthermore, the study concentrates primarily on
financial literacy as a moderating factor, excluding other behavioral and contextual variables such as
income, digital literacy, and psychological biases. Future research should employ cross-regional and
longitudinal designs to capture behavioral dynamics over time and examine emerging influences such
as AI-based financial advisors, cashback mechanisms, and gamified saving systems.
Overall, this study advances theoretical and practical understanding of digital financial behavior by
demonstrating that e-payment is neither inherently beneficial nor detrimental. Its effects are contingent
upon users’ financial literacy and capacity for self-regulation. Strengthening digital financial literacy,
therefore, represents a crucial step toward ensuring that technological innovation contributes to more
responsible and sustainable financial practices in the digital economy.
