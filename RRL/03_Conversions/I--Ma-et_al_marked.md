Consumers semi-intertemporally make intertemporal decisions:
insights from the payday effects*
Chao Ma† Yuqing Gu‡ Juin Kuan Chong§
Abstract
Analyzing the transaction data of a retail chain selling storable products and targeting upper-
middle-class customers, we find that, besides making larger expenditures on a payday, even on a
non-payday, customers make larger expenditures as long as it is their first trip to the retail chain
since a payday. Thereafter, the per-trip expenditures decrease over trips within the monthly
paycheck cycle until an upward jump on the first trip since the next payday. This pattern suggests
that consumers without facing monthly liquidity constraints may self-impose a monthly mental
budget. Their daily expenditure decisions follow a rule of thumb trying not to overspend beyond
the mental budget. They renew the mental budget on paydays and the salience of paydays also
causes overshoots in expenditures.
Keywords: Intertemporal Decision, Mental Accounting, Payday Effect, Projection Bias, Salience,
Self-Control, Storable Products

1. Introduction
Many traditional economic models assume that consumers rationally maximize long-horizon
intertemporal utilities subject to an intertemporal budget constraint. In reality, however, on the one
hand, consumers are neither capable of solving such complicated models nor completely naively
myopic; on the other hand, they may neither be insusceptible to temptation for higher present
consumption nor be fully impatient.
Analyzing the individual-level transaction data of the retail chain of a global cosmetic and
personal-care brand targeting upper-middle-class customers, we find that, besides making larger
expenditures on a payday, even on a non-payday, customers make larger expenditures as long as
it is their first trip to the retail chain since a payday. Thereafter, the per-trip expenditures decrease
over trips within the monthly paycheck cycle until an upward jump on the first trip since the next
payday.
This empirical pattern suggests that consumers may have month-specific mental accounting.
They budget their spending every month targeting a rule-of-thumb saving rate for their incomes in
the month even if they do not face a monthly liquidity constraint. A paycheck receipt on a payday
causes consumers to renew their monthly mental budgets and hence we observe an upward jump
on the first trip since the payday even if the trip occurs on a non-payday. On later trips within the
paycheck cycle, because they have already partially used the money in their monthly mental budget
on the previous trips within the paycheck cycle, they cognitively turn up their intrinsic device of
self-control and reduce expenditures to avoid overspending beyond the target saving rate of the
paycheck.
One possible motive to follow this rule of thumb is to trade off between commitment and
flexibility (Amador et al., 2006). Without facing an actual monthly liquidity constraint, solving
the intertemporal utility maximization problem in a long horizon under an intertemporal budget
constraint could generate a better consumption path than self-imposing a monthly mental budget
constraint. However, if consumers suffer from temptation for higher present consumption, they
need this rule of thumb as an internal device of self-control to counteract the overconsumption
2

from temptation. As implied by the theoretical model in Amador et al. (2006), an optimal
commitment is imposing a minimum level of savings in each period.1
We also find that, given that it is the first trip since a payday, the expenditure is dramatically
larger if the trip is on the payday than if the trip occurs one day after the payday. This empirical
pattern suggests that paycheck receipt on paydays not only causes customers to renew their
monthly mental budgets but also generates a salience effect leading to an excess downturn of the
internal device of self-control and an overshoot in expenditures on paydays. On the one hand,
salience of paydays may be necessary for enabling consumers to use paydays as milestones to
decompose a long-horizon intertemporal problem into simpler periodical problems and renew their
periodical mental budgets. On the other hand, salience of paydays causes overshoots in
expenditures.
This study mainly contributes to four strands of literature. First, it contributes to the literature
on self-control. Theoretical studies have illustrated the benefit of using external commitment
devices to resolve the self-control problem. The external commitment devices either restrict
consumers’ choice sets (Amador et al., 2006) or alter the costs or benefits of their actions (Krusell
et al., 2010; Hong and Huang, 2024). Many empirical studies have examined the effectiveness of
external commitment devices.2 In contrast, while many theoretical studies have proposed that
consumers may use intrinsic commitment devices (e.g., cognitive control, self-signaling, creating
memory biases, and strategically avoiding information) to overcome self-control problems,3 little
research has empirically examined intrinsic commitment devices because intrinsic devices are
difficult to be observed.

Our empirical findings suggest that consumers not facing monthly budget constraints may
have a monthly mental budget and follow a rule of thumb trying not to overspend within a month
in their daily expenditure decisions. They use paydays as milestones to renew their monthly mental
budgets. This intrinsic commitment device is analogous to the external device discussed in Amador
et al. (2006) that restricts consumers’ choice sets of consumption paths. They theoretically proved
that the optimal commitment device trading off between maintaining flexibility and counteracting
temptation should impose a minimum level of savings in each period.
The second strand of literature we contribute to is the literature on mental accounting. Most
existing studies examined whether consumers have separate mental budgets across different
product brackets (e.g., Heath and Soll, 1996; Hastings and Shapiro, 2013; Abeler and Marklein,
2017; Ma, 2021). In contrast, we provide evidence that consumers have separate mental budgets
across periods and use regular paycheck receipts as the landmarks to separate these periods, instead
of solving a complicated long-horizon intertemporal utility maximization problem under one
intertemporal budget constraint. Our empirical results based on storable-product purchases
indicate that consumers’ month-specific mental accounts group expenditures made in a month
instead of grouping consumption in a month because a storable product purchased in the current
month will be partially consumed in later months.4 5 6
Our empirical findings also suggest that consumers may decompose a monthly mental

The third strand of literature that our study contributes to is the literature on the payday
effects. Most empirical studies examined only instantaneous-consumption brackets and found that
consumers make larger expenditures only on paydays.7 In contrast, we study a retail chain brand
that sells storable products and find that, besides making larger expenditures on a payday, even on
a non-payday, customers make larger expenditures as long as it is their first trip to the retail chain
since a payday. Thereafter, the per-trip expenditures decrease over trips within the paycheck cycle
until an upward jump on the first trip since the next payday. We document that paycheck receipt
on paydays triggers two reactions: monthly mental-budget renewing and salience.
One possible reason why the existing studies found that, after the spike on a payday, the
daily instantaneous consumption is flat afterward instead of decreasing over days is that most
instantaneous-consumption products are essential products. In contrast, we examine a brand selling
discretionary storable products. While the daily quantity usage after purchasing a product of this
brand is quite stable, a customer’s dollar-value consumption rate of the entire brand can vary
substantially by purchasing more varieties and higher-quality varieties.

storable products is that the results could be driven by stockpiling. Given that the daily usage of

However, for this cosmetic and personal-care brand, there are multiple channels through

Compared to bank or credit card transaction data (widely used in the payday-effect literature),
which only record the merchant name for each purchase, one advantage of our retail chain
transaction data is that detailed information on each product purchased is recorded. Therefore, we
can examine whether the payday effect leads to more variety seeking, new-product trying, mistake
making, and product upgrading and hence rule out the possibility that the payday effect on storable-
product purchases is merely driven by stockpiling.

intertemporal decision making. Traditional intertemporal utility maximization models usually
assume that consumers are fully rational and capable of solving complicated dynamic
programming problems, such as the intertemporal consumption-saving decision models in the
macroeconomic literature and the dynamic discrete choice models of storable product purchases
in the industrial organization literature.8 However, on the one hand, consumers are neither
sufficiently sophisticated to solve such complicated models nor completely naively myopic; on the

Based on our empirical findings, we propose a simple model according to which consumers
semi-intertemporally make intertemporal decisions. Instead of determining daily expenditures by
solving a long-horizon intertemporal utility maximization problem at a daily granularity,
consumers decompose the problem into three steps. First, they roughly determine monthly
aggregate expenditures by solving a long-horizon problem at a monthly granularity. Second, at the
beginning of a month, they divide the predetermined aggregate expenditures for the month into
different bracket-specific mental accounts. Third, on a trip to a store that sells products belonging
to a certain bracket-specific mental account, they make trip-level expenditure decisions and follow
a rule of thumb by trying not to overspend beyond the predetermined monthly bracket-specific
mental budget. The expenditures on storable products are completely allocated to the mental
budget of the current month (paycheck cycle), even if the products will be partially consumed in
the following months. This self-imposed restriction on the choice set of consumption paths
provides two benefits: first, consumers can avoid solving complicated models while still achieving
suboptimal outcomes; second, they can trade off between maintaining flexibility and counteracting
temptation.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the background and
the data. Section 3 provides prerequisite analyses: we document that consumers make more
expenditures on paydays unconditionally, which is driven by making more expenditures on
paydays conditional on shopping instead of a greater likelihood to shop on paydays; and we
disconfirm the liquidity effect and stockpiling. Section 4 presents the main analyses: we first
examine the difference in expenditures between the first trip since a payday and later trips within
the paycheck cycle, providing evidence for monthly mental-budget renewals; then, we examine
the difference between the first trips within a paycheck cycle that occurs on the payday and that
occur on non-paydays, providing evidence for salience of paydays that generates overshoots on
paydays. In Section 5, we build a stylized theoretical model to formally explain our empirical
findings. Then, we conclude in Section 6.

2. Background and data
2.1. The stores
The chain stores that we examine in this study are a retail chain of a global cosmetic and personal-
care brand targeting upper-middle-class customers. The brand operates in more than 70 countries,
with its headquarters located in England. It sells products in eight categories: men and women
fragrance, home fragrance, makeup, facial skin care, hair care, body care, bath and shower, and
accessories. The brand positions itself as an internationally green company, aiming at improving
the quality of people’s lives by producing environmentally friendly and natural products. The
brand targets upper-middle-class consumers, who are unlikely to face monthly liquidity constraints.
Our data cover all the individual-level transaction records from 2011 to 2015 of this retail
chain within a Southeast-Asian country. The retail chain has approximately 300 stores and more
than 600,000 member customers within this country. For each transaction record, the data have
detailed information about the member ID, what products are purchased, the listed prices of the
products and the discount rates, the store location, the time of purchase, the payment method (cash
vs. credit card), etc. For each product sold, the data have detailed information on the product
characteristics, including the category, subcategory, and stock-keeping unit (SKU) of the product
and the version, volume, and ingredients.
The data in this study have a unique combination of five merits compared to other data used
in the literature on the payday effect. The combination of these merits makes our analyses feasible.
First, the retail chain does not have special promotion or sales targeting at paydays: we confirm
this with the CEO of this retail chain in this Southeast-Asian country; in addition, given that we
can observe the daily prices in the data, we run a regression of the daily discount rate averaged
across products and do not find that the discount rates are higher on paydays than on non-paydays
(see Table A.1 in Appendix A). Thus, we can rule out the possibility that the large expenditure on
paydays is driven by lower prices. We also control for daily prices in our regressions. Many
existing studies use bank account or credit card transaction data. One advantage of those data is

3.3. Disconfirming the liquidity effect
One possible explanation for the larger expenditures on paydays is that paychecks received on a
payday can provide a substantial amount of liquidity to financially constrained consumers.
However, our results are unlikely to be driven by this effect.
First, the retail chain examined in this study belongs to a global cosmetic and personal-care
brand. In general, products in the cosmetic and personal-care bracket either do not fall into the
necessity product group (such as fragrance and makeup) or fall into that group but have many
brands with a wide range of qualities. Most products in this group sold by the retail chain in our

Second, to rule out the possibility that the results are driven by more occasional purchases

at the stores and provide her demographic, contact, and address information. 67.5% of the

Third, we divide cities in this country into high-average-income and low-average-income
cities based on the city-average income. The difference in the magnitude of the payday effect
between the two groups of cities is neither statistically nor economically significant (See Table
A.2 in Appendix A). This result further confirms that customers of this brand in this country are

5. A stylized model
To explain the empirical findings in this study, in this section, we develop a stylized theoretical
model to formalize the mechanisms of monthly mental budgeting, salience, and projection bias.
5.1. Monthly mental budgets
we propose a framework according to which consumers semi-intertemporally make intertemporal
decisions. In the model, instead of determining daily expenditures by solving a long-horizon
intertemporal utility maximization problem at a daily granularity, consumers decompose the
problem into three stages. First, they roughly determine monthly aggregate expenditures by
solving a long-horizon problem at a monthly granularity. Second, at the beginning of a month,
they divide the predetermined aggregate expenditures for the month into different bracket-specific
mental accounts. Third, on a trip to a store that sells products belonging to a certain bracket-specific
mental account, they make the trip-level expenditure decisions and follow a rule of thumb trying
not to overspend beyond the predetermined monthly bracket-specific mental budget.

5.5. Potential extension
As shown in Figure 5, besides the substantially larger expenditure on paydays, the
expenditure on the first trip of a paycheck cycle on non-paydays is roughly flat instead of
monotonically decreasing over days, suggesting that customers allocate expenditures on this brand
into a mental account separate from other expenditures. Although daily overall expenditures on
other products (especially instantaneous-consumption products) will gradually exhaust a
consumer’s overall monthly mental budget over time, the unspent money in the monthly mental
account for this specific brand will not change until she makes an expenditure on this brand.
However, there is a weak pattern in Figure 5 that the expenditures on the first trips on non-
paydays slightly decrease first and then slightly increase later. This empirical pattern might be
driven by two competing forces: weak interdependence among bracket-specific mental accounts
and precautionary saving for expenditure shocks. While we are reluctant to make too much out of
this empirical result because the pattern is week, we would like to point out a direction to extend
the theoretical model by incorporating these two components.
5.5.1. Interdependence among bracket -specific mental accounts
The pain of spending a dollar on this product bracket may depend not only on the unspent money
in the monthly mental budget for this product bracket (denoted as ℎ ≤ ং ) but also on the unspent

money in the entire monthly mental account is larger. Consequently, the expenditure on this
product bracket on the first trip within a paycheck cycle will be smaller if the first trip occurs on a
later day in the cycle. The reason is that the daily expenditure on the instantaneous-consumption
bracket makes ܪ gradually decrease every day.
5.5.2. Expense shocks
If mental accounts are completely independent of each other, the potential overall expenditure
shocks in the future should not affect the consumer’s current purchase decisions on the
instantaneous-consumption product and the storable product. The reason is that the consumer

If the consumer would not let her mental account of savings fully absorb the expense shocks,
she may reduce the current expenditures on the instantaneous-consumption product and the
storable product. On a later day within a paycheck cycle, given that the number of days (exposed
to expense shocks) remaining within the cycle is smaller, the aggregate unrealized uncertainty
within the cycle is smaller. Accordingly, the reduction in expenditures on the instantaneous-
consumption product and the storable product to leave a cushion within the monthly mental
account of ℂ for the uncertainty should be smaller.41 Therefore, the expenditure on the first trip of

6. Conclusion
Analyzing the individual-level transaction data of the retail chain of a global cosmetic and
personal-care brand (storable products) targeting upper-middle-class customers, we find that,
besides making larger expenditures on a payday, even on a non-payday, customers make larger
expenditures as long as it is their first trip to the retail chain since a payday. Thereafter, the per-
trip expenditures decrease over trips within the monthly paycheck cycle until an upward jump on
the first trip since the next payday.
This empirical pattern suggests that consumers may have month-specific mental accounting.
They budget their spending every month targeting a rule-of-thumb saving rate for their incomes in
the month even if they do not face a monthly liquidity constraint. A paycheck receipt on a payday
causes consumers to renew their monthly mental budgets and hence we observe an upward jump
on the first trip since the payday even if the trip occurs on a non-payday. On later trips within the
paycheck cycle, because they have already partially used the money in their monthly mental budget
on the previous trips within the paycheck cycle, they cognitively turn up their intrinsic device of
self-control and reduce expenditures to avoid overspending beyond the target saving rate of the
paycheck.
This self-imposed restriction on the choice set of consumption paths provides two benefits.
First, consumers can avoid solving complicated models while still achieving suboptimal outcomes.
Second, they can trade off between maintaining flexibility and counteracting temptation for higher
present consumption. As implied by the theoretical model in Amador et al. (2006), an optimal
commitment device should impose a minimum level of savings in each period.
We also find that, given that it is the first trip after the payday of a paycheck cycle, the
expenditure is dramatically larger if the trip is on the payday than if the trip occurs one day after
the payday. This empirical pattern suggests that paycheck receipt on paydays not only causes
customers to renew their monthly mental budgets but also generates a salience effect leading to an
excess downturn of the internal device of self-control and an overshoot in expenditures on paydays.
35

One the one hand, salience of paydays may be necessary for enabling consumers to use paydays
as milestones to decompose a long-horizon intertemporal problem into simpler periodical
problems and renew their periodical mental budgets. On the other hand, salience of paydays causes
overshoots in expenditures.
While mental-budget renewing has similar effects on cash and credit card users, cash users
are less susceptible to salience of paydays than credit card users. Additionally, projection bias can
be triggered by salience of paydays but not by mental-budget renewing.
Consumers further decompose a monthly mental budget into bracket-specific mental
accounts. We only observe that expenditures on this brand decrease over trips within a paycheck
cycle (as shown in Table 4) but do not observe that, after the initial drop from the payday to one
day after the payday, the expenditure on the first trip of a paycheck cycle continues to decrease
over days within the paycheck cycle. Although daily overall expenditures on other products
(especially instantaneous-consumption products) will gradually exhaust a consumer’s overall
monthly mental budget over time, the unspent money in the monthly mental account for this
specific brand will not change until she makes an expenditure on this brand.

Appendix B. Disconfirming present bias
The overshoots in expenditures on paydays are unlikely to be driven by changes in the level of
present bias. Present bias operates on the timing of utility of consumption rather than on the timing
of purchases or on the timing of monetary payments (see O’Donoghue and Rabin, 2015).
First, the brand only sells storable products (e.g., shampoo, fragrance, makeup) instead of
perishable products or instantaneous-consumption products. A product purchased today is to be
consumed in the next multiple months. Suppose that a customer is making a decision of whether
to purchase a shampoo in this brand today or tomorrow. Most likely, she still has an unfinished
shampoo at home, and thus her decision does not affect her utility of consumption today. Although
there is a possibility that she has used up her shampoo at home and hence strongly prefers to
purchase another shampoo today instead of waiting until tomorrow, the probability of running out
the shampoo at home should be identical across a payday and a no-payday.
Second, if a customer makes a decision of whether to purchase a new variety that she has
not ever purchased before (either a fragrance (makeup) with a different flavor (color) from the
current one in use or a product in another category that she has never purchased before), she may
desire to start the consumption flows of this new variety from today instead of waiting until
tomorrow. However, this effect can only play an ignorable role in influencing the customer’s
decision. Suppose that the customer uses fragrance every day and is considering purchasing a new
variety of fragrance that takes one month to finish. Following the present bias literature, if she
purchases and starts to use it today, the present value of the utility flows associated with the
