Ang tagapagtaguyod na
anak for Filipino adults:
an exploratory research
Thessa Lonica Villamor Quindoza and Marie Chiela Malcampo
Faculty of Behavioral and Social Sciences, Philippine Normal University,
Manila, Philippines, and
Teresita Rungduin
Research, Extension, and Quality Assurance, Philippine Normal University,
Manila, Philippines

Abstract
Purpose – In Filipino culture, children often take on the role of family “breadwinner,” providing financial
support and sharing family responsibilities. However, this role is typically viewed narrowly as that of a financial
provider. To address this, this study explores the “Tagapagtaguyod na Anak,” aiming to offer a more nuanced
and culturally relevant understanding of the broader responsibilities and dynamics involved in this role within
Filipino families.
Design/methodology/approach – This exploratory study focused on single adult children aged 18–29 years and
middle-aged parents aged 40–60 years old. Data were collected through semi-structured interviews and
analyzed thematically to explore the phenomenon of “Tagapagtaguyod na anak.”
Findings – The findings reveal that “tagapagtaguyod na anak” provide financial, emotional and social support to
their families. This role is shaped by parental expectations and the child’s volition. This role develops out of
necessity and is influenced by the child’s understanding of family needs, a sense of responsibility, family values
and gratitude (Utang na Loob). Implications for tagasalo personality and societal norms and intergenerational
family dynamics are also discussed.
Originality/value – This study delved into the concept of “tagapagtaguyod na anak” in Filipino families,
distinguishing it from the “tagasalo personality” identified by Carandang (1987) and Udarbe (2001).
It highlights the distinct responsibilities of this role, emphasizing the influence of cultural norms, gender
expectations and socioeconomic factors. Hence, it offers valuable insights into the Filipino family dynamics.
Keywords Tagapagtaguyod, Filipino adults, Sense of responsibility, Filipino values
Paper type Research paper

phenomenon of “breadwinner.” Breadwinners are the main source of income in a household.
They typically pay the majority of household expenses and provide financial support for their
dependents (Kagan, 2023). Breadwinners appear to be prevalent in Filipino households. It is
anchored on the core value of being family-oriented where working adults provide and support
the financial needs of the family (Inquirer, 2019). This is also linked to the instilled belief that
families should always come first, and throughout adulthood, one must support the family
financially, emotionally and mentally (Cardona, 2020). These illustrate how Filipino families
are closely knit, and there is a traditional relationship that causes Filipinos to prioritize family
(Arellano-Carandang, 1979; Miralao, 1997; Tarroja, 2010; Gozum, 2020). As such, it is
unsurprising that the Philippines is one of the nations in Asia with a collectivist culture and a
strong family structure (Alesina & Giuliano, 2013).

Related to the breadwinning phenomenon, this research investigated the phenomena of
“tagapagtaguyod na anak.” The term “tagapagtaguyod” is employed in conjunction with and
as a Filipino translation of the phenomenon of “breadwinner.” “Tagapagtaguyod” is a noun
defined as the “tanging inaasahan o paunang sandigan” (Wordhippo, n.d.). The root word
“taguyod” means to provide for and support (Tagalog.com Dictionary, n.d.b). In the context of
the Filipino family, this term significantly defines how the breadwinner is, considering that
breadwinners support and provide for their family’s needs, while “Na” is the component that
corresponds to “of” (Kaut, 1961). This study also uses the term “anak” as a translation of the
English word, child. “Anak” is a gender-neutral name for one’s offspring, either “son” or
“daughter” (Tagalog.com Dictionary, n.d.a).

Tagasalo theory, first coined by Carandang (1987), served as the basis for this study.
Carandang referred to the term “tagasalo” as a syndrome that affects Filipino children and
prompts them to assume the role of caregiver or the one who leads the family in times of need.
Every family has a tagasalo, which is shaped by societal expectations that female members of
the family, particularly first-born or middle-born ones, are often tasked with household chores
early in life. Carandang described tagasalo as gender-specific and tied to birth order, often
leading to traits of passivity, compulsiveness, dependency and self-consciousness. However,
Udarbe (2001) described tagasalo as a personality rather than a syndrome. She identified nine
themes that described tagasalo children who have (1) a sense of responsibility; (2) the ability
to listen; (3) the ability to mediate conflict; (4) a need for harmony; (5) a need for control; (6)
perceive parents as central; (7) have the capacity for caring; (8) Tagasalo as siblings and (9)
capacity for emotional distance. The “tagapagtaguyod na anak” supports the Tagasalo theory’s
claim that children serve as the family’s foundation when parents are absent or unable to care
for them. It is especially significant in light of Udarbe’s assertion that the role of family
tagasalo is gender-neutral and not based on birth order because any member of the family who
is emotionally, physically and financially capable can impact the dynamics of the family.
Hence, a Filipino family’s “tagapagtaguyod na anak” may possess a tagasalo personality and
serve a variety of roles beyond simply giving financial support.

Given the paucity of research and understanding on the phenomenon of “tagapagtaguyod
na anak” in Filipino households, as well as the lack of contextualization of the breadwinner
role within Filipino families, this study explored how “tagapagtaguyod na anak” is understood
by Filipino adults, specifically those who are middle-aged parents and single adult children. It
may contribute to a deeper and contextual understanding of the “tagapagtaguyod na anak” that
exists in a Filipino family.

Methods
Design
This qualitative research utilized an exploratory design to discover the nature of a problem and
help gain a deeper knowledge of the phenomena (Cooper & Schindler, 2013). Thus, this
method is used in this study as it focuses on exploring Filipinos’ perceptions of
“tagapagtaguyod na anak” rather than their personal experiences of this phenomenon.

Participants
In total, 16 Filipino adults from Metro Manila were selected to explore perspectives on the
tagapagtaguyod na anak role. Participants were grouped into two age ranges: 18–29 for single
adult children and 40–60 for middle-aged parents. The selection criteria included age, civil
status and residence, but factors such as sex, birth order, occupation and socioeconomic status
were not considered. In this context, “children” refers to those aged 18–29, interpreted through
the Filipino term anak, meaning “offspring,” which encompasses both “son” and “daughter”
without implying literal childhood. Legally, this age range falls within the Philippine “youth”
category, defined as ages 15–30 (Republic Act No. 8044, 1995), marking a developmental
phase toward responsible adulthood (Guiao, 2024). Hence, their role as tagapagtaguyod na
anak becomes more clearly defined within the family, where they play a significant role in
supporting family dynamics. Additionally, single adults were interviewed online via Google
Meet, while middle-aged parents participated in in-person interviews, providing insights into
both generations’ views on the tagapagtaguyod na anak role.

Instruments
This study utilized a demographic questionnaire as a screening tool to verify if the participants
fall
to the research participant criteria. Subsequently, an interview questionnaire was
employed, initially containing 10 open-ended questions. However, upon the study’s pilot
testing, three sub-questions and one main question were added. Conducting a pilot test
validated the content, organization and appropriateness of the interview questions. An
example of the interview question is, “How will you define the phenomena, ‘tagapagtaguyod
na anak’? (Ano ang iyong depinisyon ng ‘tagapagtaguyod na anak’?).”

Data gathering procedure
A purposive sampling technique was employed to gather participants via Facebook and
Messenger, where they were asked to respond to a demographic questionnaire. Others were
recruited through direct approaches or in-person meetings. Participants were screened to
verify age eligibility and consent to participate before signing an informed consent form and
proceeding with the semi-structured interview.

Data analysis
This study employed thematic analysis, which is defined by Braun and Clarke (2006) as a
systematic method for identifying, organizing and understanding patterns of meaning (themes)
found in the data set. The process involved familiarizing with the data, coding, theme
development, reviewing themes, detailed investigation and creating thematic maps and writing
the formal report. Subsequently, the data analyzed were validated by two of the participants and
a peer to ensure that the themes generated reflect their understanding of the phenomenon of the
“tagapagtaguyod na anak.” This process led to structured qualitative data gathering and analysis.

Ethical considerations
The researcher adhered to ethical guidelines throughout the data collection process to ensure
the study’s quality and integrity. The research ensured participants’ voluntary involvement,
informed consent and confidentiality. Anonymity was strictly maintained according to the
Data Privacy Act of 2012, ensuring that no identifying information would appear in published
or reported documents.

A. Understanding of Filipino adults on “tagapagtaguyod na anak”
Definition of
tagapagtaguyod. The findings indicated that for Filipino adults,
“tagapagtaguyod” is defined as nagbibigay, nagpapasan, namamahala and nag-aahon.
Among these themes, the majority of Filipino adults define it as nagbibigay or someone who
provides for the family’s basic needs that may be a result of the parent’s incapability to provide
for the family. This definition reflects Carandang’s description of “tagasalo” who serves as the
person who takes care of the family or helps when in need (Carandang, 1987 as cited by
Tarroja, 2010). This definition is similar to how tagapagtaguyod is defined as nagpapasan or
bearing the responsibility of providing the family financial, emotional, spiritual and social
support. They can also act as the family’s leader or namamahala, guiding both their siblings
and the family as a dependable figure. Being regarded as a dependable person could
demonstrate how the “tagapagtaguyod” is defined as someone who lifts (nag-aahon) the
family from poverty. These definitions encompass financial and economic support aligned
with the phenomenon of a breadwinner, who serves as the primary source of income within a
household. They often pay the majority of household expenses and assist their dependents
financially (Kagan, 2023). These definitions highlight emotional and social support, consistent
with Atenas et al. (2018), who define pagsalo as the act of the tagasalo (catcher), where young
adults assume familial responsibilities and provide emotional support. In general,
“Tagapagtaguyod na Anak” refers to a person who is responsible for providing the family
with financial, emotional and social support. They guide the family relationships and help in
improving their quality of life.

Birth order and gender of tagapagtaguyod na anak.

Birth order. Filipino adults believe
that the eldest children or “panganay na anak” typically fulfill the role of “tagapagtaguyod na
anak” as they are often the first to graduate from college, obtain employment and assume
financial responsibilities for the family. This aligns with Carillo’s (2016) study of “panganay
na anak” often becoming the breadwinners of their families, which they do so as a form of
pagpaparaya or self-sacrifice.

However, three participants contended that being a “tagapagtaguyod na anak” is typically
associated with middle children, based on their personal experiences and birth order. One
participant observed that middle children often aim to please the family and prioritize its needs.
She suggested that eldest children, being more nurtured, might feel less obligated to help
during times of need. In contrast, middle children, feeling like the “second-best,” seek their
parents’ approval and appreciation. This reflects the claim of Carandang (1987), as cited by
Udarbe (2001), that it is typically the middle children who feel unappreciated and may behave
in ways deemed acceptable to gain attention.

Gender. Filipino adults believe that any gender can assume the role of “tagapagtaguyod na
anak” when circumstances demand it, reflecting a shift from traditional gender roles to an
emphasis on individual qualities, skills and maturity. This supports Udarbe’s (2001) claim that
the “tagasalo” role is not gender-specific, making both the “tagapagtaguyod na anak” and
“tagasalo” roles inclusive of all genders. Nevertheless, Filipino adults still perceive women as
more likely to fill this role. It supported Carandang’s (1987) as cited by Udarbe (2001) claim that
a “tagasalo” child is often the family’s female child. Additionally, it resonates with Bernardo,
Clemente and Liem (2014) that Filipino women appear to value social connections, familial ties,
mobility, independence, morality, social consciousness and benevolence more than men.

Characteristics of tagapagtaguyod na anak. The findings indicated a range of characteristics
that Filipino adults expect for a “tagapagtaguyod na anak” to possess. It includes being
responsible, self-sacrificing, self-sufficient and excellent.

Responsible. The majority of the Filipino adults described “tagapagtaguyod na anak” as
responsible, which entails being hardworking, persistent, resourceful, strong-minded and
disciplined. These characteristics are essential for a “tagapagtaguyod na anak” to embody, as
this role encompasses a big responsibility that may affect the family. It explains how the
characteristics of “tagasalo” and “tagapagtaguyod na anak” are intertwined since they both
reveal a sense of responsibility. These characteristics are then embodied for the benefit of the
family (Udarbe, 2001).

One of the Filipino adults emphasized that a “tagapagtaguyod na anak” is discerning of
their parent’s needs. They are emotionally and morally responsive toward their family.
It serves as an illustration of how a “tagapagtaguyod na anak” values their parents, which is
pertinent to the tagasalo personality that perceives parents as central. They strive to maintain
harmony and do various duties to please their parents and relieve them from the burden of
responsibility (Udarbe, 2001). For middle children, being discerning might be a way to gain
parental acceptance and appreciation, while for eldest and youngest children, it helps maintain
a positive connection with their parents.

Self-sacrificing. Being self-sacrificing entails having the willingness to put their desires
and goals aside for the benefit of their family. The capacity for caring is one of the themes
Udarbe (2001) found in tagasalo personality. This theme encompasses thoughtfulness,
generosity, and approachability. This self-sacrificing trait aligns closely with the tagasalo
personality, highlighting a deep drive to extend beyond oneself and support others.

Self-sufficient. For two Filipino adults, a “tagapagtaguyod na anak” is self-sufficient.
These children have the resources necessary to meet their necessities as well as those of their
families. They handle their responsibilities independently and avoid relying on others. This
finding is consistent with Udarbe’s (2001) tagasalo personality of the need for control.
Tagasalo individuals are expected to take full responsibility for their own lives. Additionally,
self-sufficiency is linked to the capacity for emotional distance, as tagasalo individuals often
distance themselves from their challenges, relying on their judgment to avoid feelings of
hopelessness and uncertainty while managing family burdens.

Excellent. Being an excellent child is one of the characteristics that a “tagapagtaguyod na
anak” possesses from the perspective of Filipino adults. These children excel in their
education, perceived as role models for their younger siblings and cousins and are expected to
exhibit proper behavior. Through their competence, knowledge and skills, it becomes an
indicator of how well a child can fulfill their responsibilities (Kelan, 2008).

Qualifications of tagapagtaguyod na anak. For Filipino adults, a “tagapagtaguyod na anak”
may or may not be a college graduate, but they must be financially independent. This means
having a stable income to contribute significantly to family expenses and provide immediate
support during financial difficulties.

A “tagapagtaguyod na anak” can also be a working student who supports both their family’s
finances and their educational needs. As noted by Burgos et al. (2018), not all parents can
afford their children’s education, leading some children to support themselves while studying.
Hence, children becoming their family’s breadwinners while studying as a result of financial
problems, affecting both their educational aspirations and their lives (Pregoner, Accion,
Buraquit, & Amoguis, 2020).

In contrast, three of the Filipino adults argued that being a “tagapagtaguyod na anak” has no
qualifications and depends solely on the child’s acceptance and initiative to assume
responsibility. This may be attributed to the idea that regardless of a child’s financial capacity
to provide for their family’s needs, it comes under their sense of responsibility toward their
family.

Roles of tagapagtaguyod na anak. The primary role of the “tagapagtaguyod na anak,”
according to Filipino adults, is providing financial support. It may be through managing the
household finances such as paying utility bills and providing daily needs such as food.
Furthermore, they take on the role of either fully financing their siblings’ education or
collaborating with their parents to financially support their siblings’ educational needs.

The role of “tagapagtaguyod na anak” extends to being involved with family decision-

B. Factors influencing a child to be the family’s tagapagtaguyod
Extrinsic factors.
Poverty. The primary factor influencing Filipino children to become the
family’s “tagapagtaguyod na anak” is poverty. In a country where 14 million families are
economically disadvantaged (Pinlac, 2023), the need for financial support is prevalent.
Filipino children often take on this role to meet their family’s expectations, driven by their
economic situation to provide practical and financial support (Fuligni, Tseng, & Lam, 1999;
Garo-Santiago et al., 2009). Thus, poverty significantly contributes to the emergence of
“tagapagtaguyod na anak” in Filipino households, motivating them to improve their family’s
standard of living.

Change in family structure. A child may take on the role of the “tagapagtaguyod na anak”
due to changes in the family structure such as parental separation, parent loss or growing up in
a broken home. In addition, when there are younger siblings involved, there exists a

phenomenon known as “parentification,” where a child feels compelled to take on
responsibilities for family affairs and consistently assumes the role of protecting the family
during difficult times (Cho & Lee, 2018). The responsibility often falls on the child since
parents might not be able to fulfill it.

Parental incapacity. Parental incapacity, such as aging, illness or job loss, is another key
factor driving children to become “tagapagtaguyod na anak.” This perspective has been
substantiated by Dalmacio et al. (2022), who determined that parental deaths, incapacities,
illnesses and even a lack of social security are factors that influence children’s participation in
the workforce. It conveys how children end up supporting their families when parents are
unable to maintain their function as the primary providers of the family (Sacristan, 2014).
Consequently, the absence of a primary earner prompts the emergence of a new financial
support source within the family.

Parental expectations. The majority of the single adult Filipino children understand that a
“tagapagtaguyod na anak” is highly influenced by their parent’s expectations to help the
family. This is consistent with Udarbe’s (2001) claim that parental expectations and behavior
have a significant impact on a child’s ability to be a tagasalo. This indicates that Filipino
families engage in deliberate socialization, openly teaching norms, values, appropriate
behavior, social duties and family expectations (Medina, 2001).

Intrinsic factors.

Volition. Contrary to the perspective of single adult Filipino children,
middle-aged parents contend that being a “tagapagtaguyod na anak” of the family is a child’s
volition rather than an obligation. Children choose to help the family on their own, driven by
their initiative and willingness. It supports Frith’s (2013) idea that when people engage in
volitional behavior, they feel a high sense of agency. Because a voluntary act begins based on
the present demands and goals (Haggard, 2019), it suggests that children could initiate or
decide to assume the position of “tagapagtaguyod na anak.”

Sense of responsibility. Some Filipino parents believe that a child’s sense of responsibility
motivates them to assume the role of “tagapagtaguyod na anak” within the family. For them, a
child who lacks maturity and a desire to support the family would not genuinely take on this
role. This is supported by Escarieses and Jimenez (2018), who found that older single women
breadwinners generally had a positive attitude towards their roles, as they could focus on
improving their family’s quality of life. This indicates that similar to a “tagasalo” child, a
“tagapagtaguyod na anak” is often driven by a strong sense of responsibility to their family.
Financial independence. Financial independence enables a child to assume this role, which
is similar to one of the qualifications of a “tagapagtaguyod na anak.” Financial independence
positively correlates with an individual’s economic circumstances, including their earnings,
assets, employment position and level of education (Xiao, Chatterjee, & Kim, 2014).
Consequently, it is reasonable to agree with Filipino adults who argue that a child cannot
qualify as “tagapagtaguyod na anak” if they lack the financial capacity to sustain themselves
and their family. As illustrated in Figure 2, the factors influencing a child to become a

C. Perspectives and feelings of Filipino adults on the phenomena of “tagapagtaguyod
na anak”
Most single adult Filipino children view the phenomenon of “tagapagtaguyod na anak”
negatively, seeing it as unjust due to the excessive pressure and expectations placed on them.
Despite this, they understand its inevitability because of their family’s needs and economic
situation. As reflected by the findings of Dalmacio et al. (2022), children in today’s society
have to deal with the realities of life, some of which prevent them from choosing their
working age.

Consequently, single adult Filipino children believe that these expectations are troubling to
the mental health of the “tagapagtaguyod na anak.” One of the challenges faced by
breadwinners is emotional instability. Maghinay, Rio, and Pagaran (2014) found that feelings

“sandwich generation,” involves extended families with multiple generations where nuclear
families depend on each other (Miller, 1981, as cited in Tongson, 2018).

On the positive side, middle-aged parents perceived this phenomenon as a reflection of a
child’s sense of responsibility. For parents, having a child who is willing to help the family
gives them a sense of fulfillment. This illustrates how parents mold the idea of a “good child,”
where a child is obedient and demonstrates love and care for their family (Dela Cruz, Protacio,
Balanon, Yacat, & Francisco, 2001). However, middle-aged parents emphasized that children
should not lose their sense of self while fulfilling these responsibilities. Neglecting their own
identity can alter how they perceive themselves in the future, impacting their ability to balance
caring for themselves with caring for others (Fulcher & Coyle, 2011). Figure 3 illustrates the
contrasting perspectives on “tagapagtaguyod na anak.” Single adult children generally view
the phenomenon negatively, while middle-aged parents perceive it more positively. This
divergence may be influenced by generational differences, age, familial background and
upbringing.

Filipino value of family-orientedness. In Filipino culture, the closely-knit nature of families
creates an obligation for family members to support each other. The parents and the child are a
“kapwa” to one another and “hindi sila iba sa iba” (Enriquez, 1977). Children should prioritize
their families, as their relationship is not only connected biologically or by being

A child’s sense of gratitude (utang na loob) often motivates them to repay their parents’
sacrifices. Kaut (1961) translated utang na loob as “debt of gratitude”; however, Enriquez
(1977) argued that, within Filipino culture, it is more accurately understood as “gratitude/
solidarity.” This concept should not be seen as a burden or “debt,” but rather as an opportunity to
repay favors. Considering that the Filipino family is described as structurally nuclear,
functionally extended, and places a strong importance on reciprocity (Go, 1994). Once parents
have met their children’s basic needs, the responsibility shifts to the children to care for their
elderly parents (Tarroja, 2010). This aligns with Dancel (2005) that gratitude is a core value in
Filipino society and a key element of “kagandahang-loob” (an inner sense of desire to do good).
Because of this, demonstrating an unappreciative behavior may be referred to as “walang utang
na loob.” These might indicate that utang na loob toward their parents drives them to assist and
support the family, exhibiting their kagandahang-loob and pagtanaw ng utang na loob (sense of
gratitude). Though not explicitly stated or imposed by parents, “utang na loob” reflects a deep,
reciprocal relationship between parents and children. Figure 4 illustrates how the roots of
Filipino values associated with “tagapagtaguyod na anak” are interconnected.

Implications and recommendations
The lack of research on the phenomenon of “tagapagtaguyod na anak” prompted this study to
explore its meaning through the perspectives of Filipino single adult children and middle-aged
parents. Udarbe’s (2001) tagasalo personality framework was incorporated to identify
similarities and differences with the characteristics of “tagapagtaguyod na anak.” The findings
revealed that “tagapagtaguyod na anak” exists in Filipino families. It is significant to
emphasize that the distinction between the “tagapagtaguyod na anak” and the “tagasalo”
children is their duty to their families. Their primary role centers on being the family’s
financial provider and functioning as the family’s main support system. While tagasalo
focuses more on the emotional assistance that a child can provide to the family, the

of their responsibility is similar to how Filipino adults negatively perceive the existence of

tend to overlook their needs. As a result, these children may become depressed, worried,
apprehensive or lose their sense of self.

The “tagapagtaguyod na anak” and “tagasalo” share several similarities, including traits
like a strong sense of responsibility, conflict mediation and the ability to maintain emotional
distance. Both roles involve a desire for harmony and control, with parents seen as central
figures in their lives. Filipino values such as family orientation and “utang na loob” (debt of
gratitude) underpin the “tagapagtaguyod na anak,” while the “tagasalo” is more associated
with personality traits. The “tagapagtaguyod na anak” is viewed as a role shaped by personal
and societal factors, often with potential mental health impacts, whereas the “tagasalo” is seen
as a personality type driven by a need for control.

One of the study’s limitations is the predominance of female participants, which may have
influenced perceptions about the gender of a “tagapagtaguyod na anak.” Future research
should aim for a balanced gender representation to better understand these perceptions.
Despite this, the study found that participants did not view the role as specific to one gender.
Although male participants felt that females were more likely to assume this role, the study
acknowledges that it did not fully capture perspectives from both genders. Future research
should focus on the lived experiences of Filipino adults in the “tagapagtaguyod na anak” role,
including the psychological impact and role limitations. Comparative studies with similar
roles in other cultures could illuminate unique aspects of the Filipino experience. Researchers
should also explore how cultural norms and intergenerational responsibilities shape parent-
child relationships, financial obligations and constraints. Educating families on financial
literacy and redefining parental duties may improve understanding and balance within the
family. Additionally, mental health professionals should develop support services to address
the concerns of both “tagapagtaguyod na anak” and their parents.

All these observations suggest that the role of “tagapagtaguyod na anak” in Filipino

expectations and financial pressures, policymakers should allocate resources and support to
families who rely on a “tagapagtaguyod na anak.” Indeed, family relationships and obligations
hold considerable value in Filipino society.
