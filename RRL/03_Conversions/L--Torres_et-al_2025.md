Abstract
This study compares platform-based gig workers and traditional
employees across four key dimensions: financial literacy, debt-to-
income (DTI) ratio, social support networks, and job satisfaction.
Grounded in Dual Labor Market Theory, the research explores the
structural divide between the primary labor market—characterized
by stable, full-time employment with formal benefits—and the sec-
ondary labor market, where gig workers, particularly those en-
gaged in technology-mediated platform work (e.g., ride-hailing,
freelance digital services), experience income volatility and limited
protections. Using a quantitative approach and purposive sampling
across five provinces, the study examined individuals working ei-
ther through digital labor platforms or in conventional full-time
roles. Data analysis employed the Mann-Whitney U test to assess
differences in financial and social outcomes. Findings revealed no
statistically significant differences in financial literacy or social sup-
port networks, suggesting that both groups have comparable access
to financial knowledge and interpersonal resources. Although job
satisfaction levels were similar across groups, qualitative insights
highlighted that platform-based gig workers often value autonomy
and personal fulfillment, which contribute uniquely to their sense
of satisfaction. A notable distinction emerged in DTI ratios, with
traditional employees exhibiting higher median values, likely due
to more stable income streams and greater access to credit. These
results underscore the evolving nature of work in the digital age
and its implications for financial resilience and worker well-being.
By shedding light on the experiences of platform-based gig workers,
this study contributes to a deeper understanding of labor market dy-
namics and offers guidance for policies aimed at fostering economic
stability across diverse employment models.

1 INTRODUCTION
The global labor market is undergoing a profound transformation
with the rapid rise of the gig economy. Defined by short-term,
flexible, and often platform-mediated work arrangements, the gig
economy has redefined how individuals engage with employment.
This model offers greater autonomy and adaptability, allowing work-
ers to balance personal and professional responsibilities more freely.
According to Business Research Insights (2025), the global gig econ-
omy was valued at USD 556.7 billion in 2024 and is projected to
reach USD 1,847 billion by 2032, growing at a compound annual
growth rate (CAGR) of 16.18%. This growth is driven by increasing
demand for flexible work and the expansion of gig opportunities
across sectors such as transportation, asset-sharing, and profes-
sional services [1].

In the Philippines, the gig economy has gained significant mo-
mentum, particularly in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic. With
traditional employment disrupted, many Filipinos turned to online
freelancing and remote work platforms such as Upwork, Fiverr, and
Grab to sustain their livelihoods. Reports indicate that up to 1.5
million Filipinos are registered on international freelancing plat-
forms [2]. The Asian Development Bank [3] notes that gig work
is increasingly prevalent in sectors like ride-hailing, e-commerce,
and delivery services. While this shift has created new income
opportunities, it has also exposed workers to challenges such as
income instability, lack of benefits, and limited access to formal
financial services.

One of the most pressing financial challenges faced by gig work-
ers is maintaining a healthy debt-to-income (DTI) ratio. This metric,
which compares an individual’s monthly debt payments to their
gross monthly income, is a key indicator of financial health. Tra-
ditional employees often maintain lower DTI ratios due to stable

In this context, financial literacy becomes a critical tool for pro-

However, gig workers often lack access to employer-sponsored fi-
nancial education programs, leaving them to manage their finances
independently. This gap underscores the importance of understand-
ing how financial literacy influences the financial well-being of
independent workers.

Beyond financial concerns, job satisfaction is another area where
gig workers and traditional employees differ significantly. Accord-
ing to the Statista Research Department [8], 77% of gig workers
report satisfaction with the autonomy and flexibility of their roles.
However, this freedom often comes at the cost of job insecurity,
income instability, and limited career advancement opportunities.
Traditional employees, by contrast, benefit from structured ca-
reer paths, steady income, and employer-provided benefits, which
contribute to higher levels of financial security and overall job
satisfaction.

The absence of employer-sponsored benefits also contributes to
increased job-related stress among gig workers. Without stable in-
come and social protections, many turn to informal support systems
such as peer lending, family aid, and community-based financial
programs to manage financial uncertainty [9]. These social net-
works serve as critical safety nets, helping gig workers cope with
economic instability [10]. In contrast, traditional employees typi-
cally rely on formal benefits like health insurance and retirement
plans, reducing their dependence on informal financial support
[11].

This study aimed to provide a comparative analysis of gig work-
ers and traditional employees by examining their demographic
profiles and financial statuses. Key areas of focus included financial
literacy, debt-to-income ratios, social support networks, and job
satisfaction levels. By exploring these dimensions, the research
sought to determine which group was better positioned to achieve
sustainable financial security and long-term economic stability. The
findings contributed to ongoing discussions about the evolution of
the labor market and informed policies designed to support various
forms of employment.

2 RELATED LITERATURE AND STUDIES
The subsequent sections provide a comprehensive analysis of the
similarities and differences between traditional employees and gig
workers in relation to financial management practices.

2.1 Financial Literacy
Financial literacy, the ability to understand and utilize financial
skills, is increasingly vital in today’s economy, where effective
personal finance management is key to stability and growth. It
covers various areas such as budgeting, saving, investing, and debt
management [12]. Research shows that financial education signifi-
cantly improves both financial knowledge and behaviors, helping
individuals reduce vulnerability and enhance long-term financial
security. While traditional workers often exhibit higher financial
literacy due to stable incomes and employer resources, they may
lack advanced investment knowledge, primarily due to concerns
about fraud.

In contrast, gig workers face unique financial challenges, includ-
ing irregular income and limited access to benefits and financial
education. Many struggle with budgeting and financial planning,
exacerbated by the absence of organizational support and detrimen-
tal practices from gig platforms that may lead to the undervaluation
of their services. To bridge this gap, integrating financial literacy
programs into support systems for gig workers is essential [13].
These programs can help gig workers develop effective budgeting
strategies, manage income fluctuations, and prepare for taxes, ul-
timately empowering them to achieve greater financial resilience
and long-term economic stability.

2.2 Debt-to-Income Ratio
Research indicates a strong link between traditional workers’ debt
levels and factors such as income inequality and cultural values.
Davidsson [14] highlights that greater economic disparities lead
to increased borrowing and financial instability among these em-
ployees. Similarly, Shostya and Banai [15] identify how cultural
aspects, including individualism and uncertainty avoidance, signifi-
cantly influence borrowing decisions. The study underscores the
importance of addressing inequalities and understanding cultural
factors to develop effective financial education and policies that
encourage responsible borrowing.

Gig workers experience specific financial difficulties due to fluc-
tuating incomes and the lack of employment benefits, which con-
tribute to elevated debt-to-income ratios. Yumang [16] reveals
that many gig workers resort to predatory lending due to inconsis-
tent earnings, which increases their financial strain. Meanwhile,
Mitchell, Li, and Decker [17] note that these workers’ reliance on
high-interest loans exacerbates their financial precarity, affecting
their overall well-being. The findings call for targeted financial
interventions, comprehensive financial literacy programs, and pol-
icy reforms to better support the distinct financial needs of gig
workers.

work-life balance and reducing stress for traditional employees.
Oludayo and Omonijo [18] emphasize that strong workplace sup-
port networks enhance job satisfaction and lower stress levels, ulti-
mately improving employee motivation and productivity. Similarly,
the relationship between social support and work-family balance
among midlife women. Their findings indicate that strong support

mitment, and career advancement, enabling employees to manage
personal and professional responsibilities more effectively. This
underscores the need for workplaces to cultivate supportive envi-

Unlike traditional employees, gig workers function outside typi-

support system improves overall job satisfaction, productivity, and
well-being, emphasizing the importance of strengthening social
ties within the gig economy.

2.4 Job Satisfaction
Job satisfaction is essential as it significantly impacts motivation,
performance, and employee retention. Riyanto et al. [20] high-
lighted that job satisfaction serves a mediating role between motiva-
tion and performance by promoting greater employee engagement
rather than exerting a direct effect. High stress levels diminish
job satisfaction, while a positive work environment can enhance it.
These insights underscore the importance of managing workplace
conditions to improve job satisfaction and, ultimately, employee
performance. In traditional employment settings, job satisfaction
plays a critical role in organizational success. Organizational cul-
ture, leadership, and motivation significantly influence work per-
formance, with job satisfaction primarily shaped by leadership. Key
drivers of job satisfaction include career growth, supportive work
environments, and effective leadership. Studies reveal that career
development programs, such as mentoring and training, enhance
employee retention and job satisfaction, while satisfied employees
tend to perform better.

The satisfaction levels of gig workers are affected by specific
challenges unique to their roles. According to Naz and Raj [21],
factors that contribute to gig worker satisfaction include perceived
fairness, social support, and job control. They pointed out prob-
lems like irregular income and a lack of benefits, which are not as
common in traditional jobs.. Singha and Saikia [22] found that fac-
tors such as compensation, autonomy, flexibility, skill development,
social interaction, and work-life balance significantly contribute
to job satisfaction in the gig economy. These findings highlight
the critical need to understand the distinct factors that shape finan-
cial and job satisfaction experiences for both gig and traditional
workers. Collectively, these studies illustrate the importance of
career development, corporate culture, and leadership in enhanc-
ing job satisfaction and employee well-being across various work
environments.

3 METHODOLOGY
This study employed a quantitative, comparative analysis to ex-
plore how the gig economy contrasts with traditional employment
regarding financial literacy, debt-to-income ratios, social support
systems, and job satisfaction. By analyzing a diverse group of
participants from various sectors, the researchers aimed to un-
cover key differences and similarities between gig workers and
traditional employees, shedding light on their financial behaviors
and job satisfaction levels. The findings enhance understanding of
employment-related financial trends and may help organizations
and workers develop strategies for improving financial stability
and overall job satisfaction in a changing labor market. [23]

3.1 Sampling
This study utilized non-probability purposive sampling to compare
gig workers and full-time traditional employees in a region with five
provinces, focusing on financial literacy, debt-to-income ratio, job
satisfaction, and workforce resilience. Gig workers were defined as
those engaged in freelance or platform-based jobs like ride-hailing
and online freelancing, while traditional employees held full-time
positions with formal contracts and benefits, excluding those with
part-time or side jobs. A screening process ensured participants
met age and employment criteria, resulting in reliable data from
individuals with adequate work experience to provide insights into
their financial and employment conditions, ultimately highlighting
the changing employment landscape in the region.

3.2 Research Instrument
The researchers conducted a self-administered survey using Google
Forms to collect data on financial literacy, financial stability, and
workforce experiences from a diverse group of respondents, includ-
ing gig workers and traditional employees. The survey comprised
five sections: demographic information, assessment of financial
literacy through a multiple-choice questionnaire, evaluation of fi-
nancial stability via debt-to-income ratios, measurement of social
support networks using a Likert scale, and analysis of job satisfac-
tion using the Job Satisfaction Survey (JSS). Data were analyzed
using Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) and comparative re-
search analysis to identify patterns and relationships among key
variables, ultimately providing insights into the financial dynamics
and workplace experiences of different employment types.

3.3 Statistical Instrument
In this study, the Independent Sample t-Test was initially selected to
compare financial literacy, debt-to-income ratio, social support net-
works, and job satisfaction between gig workers and traditional em-
ployees. However, prior to conducting the t-test, the Shapiro-Wilk
test was applied to assess the normality of thse data distribution.
The results indicated that the data did not meet the assumption of
normality across all dependent variables. Consequently, the Mann-
Whitney U test was employed as a non-parametric alternative, as it
does not require a normal distribution and is suitable for comparing
two independent groups.

4 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Table 1 delineates the differences in financial status between Tradi-
tional Workers and Gig Workers. The results of the Mann-Whitney
U test indicate that there are no statistically significant differences
between the two groups regarding financial literacy, social support
networks, and job satisfaction.

With respect to financial literacy, both groups achieved a median
score of 9, signifying moderate levels of financial understanding.
The corresponding U-statistic was 40137, and the p-value was 0.267,
suggesting no significant difference in financial literacy between
Traditional and Gig Workers. This parity in financial literacy levels
implies that the type of employment may not substantially influence
financial knowledge. Factors contributing to this similarity may
include the widespread accessibility of financial education resources
and the necessity for individuals in both employment types to
effectively manage their personal finances.

In terms of social support networks, the median scores were
3.75 for Traditional Workers and 3.63 for Gig Workers, with a U-
statistic of 39340 and a p-value of 0.135. The absence of significant
differences between the two groups suggests that both Traditional
and Gig Workers have access to comparable levels of social interac-
tion and support, irrespective of their employment type. Notably,
Traditional Workers demonstrated a slightly higher median social
support score, indicating a marginal advantage stemming from
more structured workplace relationships and formal support sys-
tems. While the overall findings indicate no statistically significant
difference in social support networks, individual responses reveal
important disparities in workplace experiences. For instance, one
Gig Worker reported never receiving helpful information or ad-
vice from work peers or professional connections, highlighting
that some independent workers may face challenges in accessing
professional guidance and support. Thus, despite the marginally
higher median social support score among Traditional Workers, the
experience of workplace support may not be uniformly distributed
among all employees. In contrast, Gig Workers, who often oper-
ate within less structured environments, may encounter greater
difficulties in obtaining consistent professional guidance and peer
support.

Regarding job satisfaction, both groups exhibited similar medi-
ans, with Traditional Workers scoring 3.56 and Gig Workers scoring
3.67. The U-statistics was 41842, with a p-value of 0.797, further
corroborating the absence of a significant difference. Although

the difference in job satisfaction between the two groups is not
statistically significant, the marginally higher median score among
Gig Workers points to subtle distinctions in their experiences. For
example, one Gig Worker articulated strong agreement with the
statement, “My job is enjoyable,” reflecting how factors such as
autonomy, flexible scheduling, and personal control over work
positively influence job satisfaction. This observation underscores
the notion that such fulfilling aspects are not exclusive to one em-
ployment type but can manifest across both. Consequently, this
reinforces the argument that overall job satisfaction may hinge
more on the nature of one’s work and its alignment with individ-
ual values and preferences, rather than solely on whether one is
classified as a Gig or Traditional Employee. In essence, intrinsic
motivators, such as autonomy and enjoyment, play pivotal roles in
shaping job contentment across different employment structures.
Conversely, a significant difference was identified in the debt-
to-income ratio between the two groups. Traditional Workers
exhibited a higher median ratio of 40, compared to 31.41 for Gig
Workers. The Mann-Whitney U statistic was 34567, with a p-value
of less than 0.001, indicating a statistically significant difference at
the 0.05 level. This disparity suggests that Traditional Workers may
bear a greater debt burden relative to their income than their Gig
Worker counterparts, potentially reflecting differences in financial
responsibilities, income stability, or access to credit between the
two employment types. The elevated debt-to-income ratio among
Traditional Workers may signify greater access to credit facilities
and a propensity for long-term financial commitments, such as
mortgages or loans. In contrast, Gig Workers may exhibit more
cautious financial behaviors due to income variability and limited
access to traditional credit systems.

5 CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
This study explores the financial and social dimensions of platform-
based gig workers compared to traditional employees, focusing
on four key factors: financial literacy, debt-to-income (DTI) ratio,
social support networks, and job satisfaction. Framed within the
Dual Labor Market Theory, the research highlights the structural
divide between the primary labor market—marked by job stability
and formal benefits—and the secondary labor market, where gig
workers, particularly those engaged through digital platforms, face
income volatility and limited institutional protections.

By analyzing data from participants across five provinces, the
study reveals how technology-enabled gig work reshapes economic
behavior and personal well-being. A significant finding is that
platform-based gig workers report lower DTI ratios than traditional
employees, suggesting more cautious debt management despite
unstable earnings. In contrast, traditional employees often carry
higher DTI ratios due to greater access to credit and long-term
financial obligations. While job satisfaction scores were statistically
similar, qualitative insights show that gig workers derive fulfillment
from autonomy and flexibility, even amid financial uncertainty.

The study argues that conventional metrics like job satisfaction
may overlook the nuanced challenges faced by gig workers in the
digital economy. To bridge business and technology for a smarter,
sustainable economy, the research calls for targeted financial edu-
cation, adaptive credit assessment models, and inclusive support
systems. Recommendations include platform-driven financial tools
such as dynamic earnings forecasts and automated savings fea-
tures, as well as government reforms to enhance social protection
schemes with flexible contribution options.

Furthermore, the study advocates for a broader research agenda—
incorporating longitudinal and mixed-methods approaches—to bet-
ter understand the evolving financial resilience of gig workers.
Expanding participant demographics to include various types of
platform-based workers (e.g., freelancers, delivery riders) will im-
prove generalizability and inform policy interventions. Financial
institutions, employers, and educational bodies must collaborate to
equip all workers with the tools and knowledge needed for long-
term financial security in a rapidly digitizing labor landscape.
