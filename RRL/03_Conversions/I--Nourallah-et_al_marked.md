1. Introduction
Research findings regarding household financial capability are a valuable resource for policymakers, providing insights that can aid
in effectively planning a nation’s household financial landscape. Although studies in the United States and Canada show high levels of
achievement, such research appears to be rare in the European Union (EU). Major household financial surveys, such as the European
Household Finance and Consumption Survey, have not considered financial capability, resulting in a lack of knowledge about
household financial capability in Europe. Furthermore, exploring these issues poses several challenges because the research diverges in
how it measures financial capability (Birkenmaier, Rothwell, and Agar, 2022).
Sherraden et al. (2022) conclude that low financial ability prevents individuals from owning property, arguing that financially
capable people will experience less financial stress during economic downturns. Xiao and Bialowolski (2023) recently challenged
dominant research by emphasizing the need to explore the factors critical to fostering sustained financial capability while focusing on
the consequences of achieving such capability. Among the few attempts to examine critical factors, French et al. (2020) investigate the
influence of smartphone app use on financial behavior and suggested a positive impact of financial technology (FinTech) on financial
capability in the UK. In the United States, Lusardi et al. (2021) emphasize the benefits of using online technology to make financial
decisions. Meanwhile, FinTech has gained popularity in the EU, with a significant percentage of households using these applications on

This study is based on the capability approach, developed by Amartya Sen, the winner of the 1998 Nobel Memorial Prize in

This study, which measures five variables: skills, debt, saving, financial resilience, and financial well-being, identifies the top three
EU countries in terms of financial capability, namely Sweden, the Netherlands, and Austria, as well as the countries with the lowest
financial capability scores. Furthermore, the study finds that Hungary and Latvia have experienced the greatest percentage growth in
financial capability. Sweden, Finland, and Estonia top the FinTech rankings. Despite Romania and Bulgaria’s modest FinTech scores,
they appear to be on the right track, as they have the highest percentage growth in FinTech in the EU.
The findings show that FinTech has a significant positive effect on financial capability, which remains stable even after several
endogeneity and robustness checks. The study also found that the Human Development Index has a significant positive effect on the
treatment of financial capability. However, the study found no significant relationship between income and financial capability.
Financial freedom is also considered limited in an individual’s financial capability.
To ensure the robustness of the results, the study employs subsample techniques, dividing the countries under study into two
subsamples based on their level of implementation of the Europe 2020 strategy. The complementary results confirm FinTech’s sig-
nificant positive effect on financial capability, with countries adhering closely to the Europe 2020 strategy outperforming countries in
the second subsample. The latter may be related to the findings of Sa´nchez and Ruiz-Martos (2018), who found that households in
countries with high scores for the Europe 2020 strategy tend to have higher life satisfaction.
The study makes three significant contributions. First, to the best of the authors’ knowledge, this is the first study of household
financial capability in the EU following the development of measures of five financial capability variables, namely skills, debt, savings,
financial resilience, and financial well-being. Using these variables to capture various aspects of financial capability can help overcome
the relatively naive prior approach to measuring this capability and pave the way for future studies. Second, this study contributes to
increased knowledge of the overall financial capability situation in the EU by investigating the current financial capability of European
countries, concluding that countries in northern Europe have a significantly higher level. Third, by highlighting areas of deficient
FinTech in the EU, this study contributes to the Digital Finance Strategy, which aims to improve the use of FinTech solutions in the EU
while reducing digital service fragmentation between countries (European Banking Authority, 2024). The present study can be linked
to two projects managed by the European Central Bank (2024). The Digital Europe Project aims to provide EU households with secure
digital payment methods and will benefit from the FinTech assessment results. The study also suggests that the Household Finance and
Consumption Survey include a financial capability section to align with the US national financial capability study (FINRA Investor
Education Foundation, 2023) and the Canadian financial capability survey (Government of Canada, 2023).
The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 provides theoretical background, and Section 3 presents data and the
empirical model. Section 4 reports the results, and Section 5 concludes with a discussion and policy recommendations.
2. Theoretical background
2.1. Financial capability
Financial capability roots in Amartya Sen’s seminal work (Johnson & Sherraden, 2007) and the capability approach, which focuses
on individuals’ ability to address various “functionings” important to their lives (Sen, 2005). Functioning is a crucial notion in Sen’s
approach because it allows people to manage a variety of activities. In a primitive context, functionings may concern having enough
food, whereas in more sophisticated situations, it can mean complex actions (Sen, 1993). This approach is distinct in that it focuses on
actions rather than passivity. Furthermore, the ability to choose is critical in the Sen approach. In this regard, Nussbaum (2011)
compares two individuals: the first is “fasting” and the other is “starving.” She argues that, although both individuals lack the food they
require, they do not have the same opportunities because the fasting person chose not to eat, whereas the starving person did not have
food to eat. Sen (1993) defines a capability is “a set of such functioning n-tuples, representing the various alternative combinations of
beings and doings any one (combination) of which the person can choose.”
Regarding financial capability, the World Bank (2013) states that it “encompasses the knowledge, attitudes, skills, and behaviors of
consumers concerning managing their resources and understanding, selecting, and making use of financial services that fit their
needs.” The importance of an active rather than passive approach to financial capability has been highlighted in previous studies in this
field (French et al., 2020). The former is evident in Lusardi’s (2011, p.2) definition of financial capability: “how well people make ends
meet, plan ahead, choose and manage financial products, and possess the skills and knowledge to make financial decisions.” Xia and
Tao (2020, p. 15) emphasize the active approach, concluding that financial capability is the “consumer ability to apply appropriate
knowledge and perform desirable financial behavior to achieve financial well-being.”

2.2. Measuring financial capability
Birkenmaier, Rothwell, and Agar (2022) scrutinize how earlier studies measured financial capability and find that many studies
used various methods to assess this type of capability. Their results show that most previous studies took a naive approach to measuring
financial capability, relying primarily on financial access and knowledge. As a result, a more innovative approach is required to
consider the factors that are critical when investigating financial capability in developed countries (Birkenmaier, Maynard, and Kim,
2022; Xiao et al., 2022).
Reading and numerical skills are required for making sound financial decisions (Hastings et al., 2013). Following Sen’s approach,
households with financial skills are likelier to take advantage of opportunities such as purchasing property and managing portfolios. It
is important to note that skills in this context differ from knowledge, which is defined as “the ability to understand personal and
broader financial matters, [while] ‘skill’ is the ability to apply that knowledge in everyday life” (French et al., 2020, p. 305).
Debt is typically considered when studying household financial planning and management issues (Almenberg et al., 2021). Ac-
cording to Lusardi (2011), it is critical to consider both assets and debt when assessing financial capability. Debt can affect household
financial capability because high debt can lead to financial vulnerability and difficulty meeting financial obligations (Klapper &
Lusardi, 2020). Property debt, vehicle loans, and student loans are popular among households in developed countries (Zinman, 2015).
In 2021, the household debt level2 as a percentage of net disposable income was 255% in Denmark, 222% in the Netherlands, and
203% in Sweden. Thus, considering all types of debt when determining financial capability is likely to produce misleading results. To
address this issue, we rely on Xia and Tao (2020, p. 15), who argue that access to informal debt or exclusion from traditional financial
services is a reasonable indicator of low financial capability.
Saving is essential for households (Tufano, 2009), and households that save money can manage financial shocks without incurring
additional debt (Brounen et al., 2016). Such households can invest their savings and earn dividends, which can help them achieve
financial independence (Browning & Lusardi, 1996). Financial resilience, or dealing with unexpected financial expenses, is another
aspect of financial capability. Households that save money and manage their debt will be better able to handle unexpected financial
expenses (Tahir et al., 2022).
Not only money-related issues can help households become financially capable; psychological factors are also crucial (Financial
Consumer Agency of Canada, 2019). According to prior studies (Taylor et al., 2011), households with strong financial capabilities are
less likely to experience money-related physiological issues. Due to its role in mitigating financial anxiety and increasing financial
security (Barrafrem et al., 2020), financial well-being seems an irreplaceable aspect when financial capability is considered.
Taken together, skills, debt, savings, financial resilience, and financial well-being all appear to be important factors in determining
financial capability.
2.3. Financial technology and financial capability
Although its origins can be traced back to early attempts to use analog technology in the financial industry (Nguyen et al., 2023),
embryonic FinTech can be found in the 15th century, when people conducted financial transactions with physical media such as papers
and coins (Alt et al., 2018). However, advanced technology, such as blockchain, has enabled the development of customized, fast, and
secure FinTech solutions (Gomber et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2023). In 2019, global investments in FinTech solutions exceeded USD 135
billion, and as a result of the widespread use of these solutions by households, several central banks around the world are considering
issuing novel types of money, such as central bank digital currencies (Keister & Sanches, 2023).
The reasons for widespread adoption of FinTech solutions extend beyond their ease of use and reasonable prices. FinTech solutions,
such as mobile banking apps, can assist people in making sound financial decisions (Nourallah & O ¨ hman, 2021). Furthermore,
financial robo-advisors enable investors to easily build portfolios and track their investments (Kuvvet, 2022). These robo-advisors can
reduce behavioral biases and help investors gain financial knowledge and learn about financial planning (Nourallah, 2003). Various
online financial information sources have become popular among households, with personal finance blogs regarded as critical to
household financial decisions (Hoffmann & Otteby, 2018).
Taken together, households that use FinTech solutions can compare products, read reviews written by others, and may be able to
use artificial intelligence solutions such as ChatGPT. They can also use various online financial resources to learn more about financial
issues, which will likely improve their financial situation (Panos & Wilson, 2020).

3.2. Variables
3.2.1. Financial capability
Financial capability is measured by using the arithmetic means of the five constructs, as shown in Eq. (1).
Financialcapability=skills+debt+saving+financialresilience+financialwellbeing/5 (1)
The measurements are as follows (variables, definitions and their sources are provided in Appendix 1):
• “Skills” is measured by the “participation rate in education and training” (Eurostat, 2024).
• The arithmetic means of the percentage of respondents who stated that they borrowed (i) for health or medical purposes (% age
15+) and (ii) from family or friends (% age 15+) are calculated (Demirgüç-Kunt et al., 2022). Higher percentages indicate lower
financial capability. Hence, we use 1 minus the arithmetic means of the previous percentages to find the debt variable.
• In line with Demirgüç-Kunt et al. (2022), saving is measured as the arithmetic mean of (i) saving any money (% age 15+), (ii)
saving for old age (% age 15+), and (iii) saving at a financial institution (% age 15+).
• Financial resilience is measured by the ability to face unexpected financial expenses, which is calculated as 1 minus the inability to
face unexpected financial expenses (Eurostat, 2024).
• Financial well-being is measured using (i) the average satisfaction rating and (ii) the distribution of the population aged 18+with a
health status considered very good (Eurostat, 2024).
3.2.2. Financial technology
Previous studies apply “using mobile payment for paying bills” as a proxy for FinTech use (Demir et al., 2022). To properly measure
FinTech, this study takes into account variables that target various aspects of FinTech, specifically the arithmetic means of the
following four variables: (i) received digital payments (% age 15+), (ii) made a digital payment (% age 15+), (iii) used a mobile phone
to pay utility bills (% age 15+), and (iv) individual frequency of Internet use (Appendix 2).
3.2.3. Control variables
According to the financial competence framework for adults in the EU, the European Union-Organization for Economic Co-
operation and Development (2022) state that “income-related competencies are essential for financial resilience and relevant for
many other competencies such as budgeting, saving and retirement.” Households with a high income have a higher level of financial
literacy (Lusardi et al., 2017), whereas households with a low income have a lower level of savings (Almenberg et al., 2021).
Furthermore, “financial freedom is an indicator of banking efficiency as well as a measure of independence from government control
and interference in the financial sector” (Heritage Foundation, 2022a). Hence, financial freedom can help determine financial
capability and thus improve the efficiency of the EU’s financial sector (Chortareas et al., 2013). Recent studies (Ofosu-Mensah Ababio
et al., 2021; Zeqiraj et al., 2022) have also taken the Human Development Index into account when studying issues related to
household financial capability, and Collins and Urban (2020) report an interrelation between financial freedom and financial well-
being. Thus, three control variables are included: received wages, financial freedom, and the Human Development Index. In addi-
tion, we replace received wages with the annual percentage of gross domestic product (GDP) per capita growth, i.e., GDP per capita
growth (annual %) (Appendix 2).
We include additional control variables to ensure that the effects of the key explanatory variable do not overlap with those of other
variables. By improving the business environment, these additional variables (Appendix 2) may indirectly affect financial capability.
Given the rule of law in promoting growth in wealthy countries (Aixala´ & Fabro, 2008), we use data from the World Justice Project
(2021) to account for any such effects. Trade openness (i.e., international openness) may also affect household financial position
because more foreign products in a country may encourage households to spend more money on consumption (The World Bank, 2022).
When focusing on the impact of FinTech on income inequality, Demir et al. (2022) find that trade openness has a negative effect.
Furthermore, we take into account the use of technology in the business sector, specifically companies that use software solutions,
because extensive digitalization may affect household financial capability (Iddrisu & Chen, 2022; Van Bon, 2021). Increased stock
market returns are also likely to increase financial capability (Van Rooij et al., 2011).

3.5. Estimation strategy
The fixed-effects approach considers country-specific effects while eliminating unobserved heterogeneity between countries
(Baltagi & Baltagi, 2008). This estimation only considers within-individual differences, effectively discarding any information about
differences between individuals.
Although the fixed-effects estimator accounts for the effects of time-invariant variables, it cannot estimate them. The within-
country transformation removes the time-invariant variables, so we cannot estimate their coefficients. Thus, employing a random-
effects strategy may produce better results. The estimator uses both within- and between-country variances to estimate the effect of
time-invariant variables, resulting in a more efficient estimator. However, using this strategy necessitates strict exogeneity and
orthogonality between regressors and specific fixed effects. Hence, we used the Hausman (1978) test. The null hypothesis implies the
advantage of the random-effects model, whereas the alternative is the fixed-effects model (Greene, 2008). The Chi-square of the
Hausman test is χ2 =20.03 with a significant p-value (i.e., p <0.01). We therefore reject the null hypothesis, indicating a correlation
between the unique errors and the regressors. This also confirms the fixed-effects strategy’s advantage.
3.6. Serial correlation and heteroskedasticity
A panel data approach necessitates some specification tests for serial correlation (common in panel data) and heteroscedasticity.
Serial correlation biases standard errors, reducing the efficiency of estimates. The classical linear panel data model assumes that
disturbances are serially uncorrelated. This assumption may be violated because the dynamic effect of shocks on the dependent
variable is frequently spread across multiple periods (Baltagi & Baltagi, 2008). Most serial correlation tests rely on specific assumptions
about the nature of individual effects. Wooldridge (2022) proposes a test with few assumptions. Using this test, we obtained an F
statistic of 8.131 and rejected the null hypothesis at a 1% significance level. This shows that we do not rule out the existence of serial
correlation. Therefore, we cluster the standard errors by country.
Because the model may not capture all differences between the countries under study, we account for the heteroskedasticity that is
likely to appear in the data. A modified Wald statistic, which is viable when the assumption of normality is violated at least in
asymptotic terms (Greene, 2008), shows that χ2 =31,227.37 with a significant p-value. This implies that error terms have hetero-
skedastic variances across groups. Accordingly, we use robust standard errors in our regression analyses.

5. Conclusion
5.1. Policy recommendations
This study uses three waves of Global Findex Panel Data (2014, 2017, and 2021) and the Eurostat database to assess financial
capability in 24 of the EU’s 27 countries and investigate the role of FinTech in addressing financial capability. The results show a strong
correlation between FinTech and financial capability. The Human Development Index positively and significantly affects financial
capability, while received wages and financial freedom have no effect.
The positive and significant impact of FinTech on financial capability highlights the role of financial service providers in enhancing
individual financial capability. Those who use FinTech solutions receive newsletters and other forms of communication containing
useful financial information via their devices. These findings echo the results of Karlan et al. (2016), who argue that reminder messages
sent by banks improve customer commitment to saving goals. Furthermore, FinTech solutions’ opportunities for individuals to control
and trace their financial transactions may allow them to conduct sound financial planning (Nourallah & O ¨ hman, 2021).
The study also suggests that the higher the achievements of European countries in terms of human development, the greater
households’ potential financial capability. In terms of income, an increase in the proportion of people receiving wages appears to have
no effect on financial ability. This contradicts the findings of Almenberg et al. (2021) and Lusardi et al. (2017), but it supports the
argument that higher income does not guarantee a stable financial position. For example, Atkinson et al. (2007) report that low-income
households with good money management may be better financially than higher-wage households with poor money management
skills. This study also found that financial freedom has a limited impact on people’s financial abilities. This contradicts the suggested
role of financial freedom in banks (Chortareas et al., 2013), implying that financial freedom can serve an important function in the
banking sector but may have a limited role in individual financial capability.

which focuses on the psychological and economic aspects of financial behavior (Bruijne & Streefkerk, 2020).

the conflict in Ukraine. However, we must remember that financial literacy is a passive approach. In addition to efforts to improve
financial literacy, a more proactive approach should be considered. In this sense, financial capability appears appropriate.

5.2. Limitations and suggestions for further research
Even though income and financial freedom can help improve financial issues (Collins & Urban, 2020; Lusardi et al., 2017), other
factors appear to be at least as important. To address these issues, alternative methodologies such as surveys and qualitative studies can
be used. This study does not explore the role of socioeconomic variables because of a lack of data. Therefore, we recommend future
studies should address these factors. The three waves of data used here have some limitations, and future studies could try to access
other datasets covering longer periods.
According to the present results, the proliferation of foreign products and services and the use of big data analytics to design
marketing campaigns appear to mitigate household financial capability. Future studies could explore the potential impact of marketing
campaign exposure on financial capability.
FinTech is possibly associated with some unethical financial behavior, such as overconsumption and fraud (Gomber et al., 2018).
However, proper use of FinTech can have positive consequences, not least in terms of increasing financial capability. This study can be
expanded upon to explore what factors can improve the proper use of FinTech solutions. Future studies may also focus on financial
capability in particular groups of people, such as the young generation. This generation lacks financial experience but possesses strong
technological skills (Nourallah & O ¨ hman, 2021), so it may be of interest to investigate how to use FinTech solutions to improve
financial capability among this group of individuals, which would have a significant impact on how the society of the future will be
shaped.
