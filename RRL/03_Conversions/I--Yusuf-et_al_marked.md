A B S T R A C T
Financial stress among postgraduate students has become an increasing concern in the digital era, particularly with the rapid
adoption of digital financial tools that may both alleviate and intensify psychological distress. This study aims to examine
whether technology reduces or amplifies financial stress among Nigerian postgraduate students through a cognitive-behavioral
perspective.  The  study  employed  a  conceptual  and  analytical  research  design  using  a  qualitative,  theory-driven  approach
grounded in Cognitive-Behavioral Theory (CBT). Data were collected from secondary sources, including approximately 70
peer-reviewed  journal  articles,  policy  documents,  and  empirical  studies  published  between  2015  and  2024,  retrieved  from
Scopus, Web of Science, Google Scholar, and JSTOR. Data analysis was conducted using thematic and comparative analysis,
followed by theory-driven interpretation based on CBT principles. The findings indicate that digital financial technologies play
a dual role. On one hand, tools such as budgeting applications, mobile banking, and online savings platforms enhance financial
awareness, self-efficacy, and emotional regulation. On the other hand, misuse of fintech services, impulsive digital borrowing,
exposure to fraud, and information overload trigger cognitive distortions that exacerbate anxiety and maladaptive financial
behaviors. The results highlight that financial stress is shaped more by students’ cognitive interpretations and coping strategies
than by technology itself. It is concluded  that digital financial tools can reduce financial stress only when accompanied by
adequate cognitive-behavioral skills and financial literacy.

Economic  conditions  in  Nigeria  further  complicate  the  financial  experiences  of  university  students,
particularly  those  pursuing  postgraduate  education.  Rising  inflation,  unstable  employment  opportunities,  and
broader  economic  uncertainty  have  contributed  to  increased  financial  pressure  on  students  and  their  families

additional financial obligations such as tuition payments, research expenses, and family responsibilities while often
lacking stable income sources (Igbinedion & Adeyemi, 2023; Yusuf & Olayiwola, 2022). Limited financial aid
and  scholarship  opportunities  intensify  these  pressures,  leading  many  students  to  rely  on  informal  financial
support, part-time employment, or digital borrowing platforms  (Edem et al., 2023; Oladeji & Bamidele, 2022).
Financial constraints have also been associated with delayed graduation and reduced academic productivity among
postgraduate students in Nigerian universities (Adebayo & Ibrahim, 2023; Ajayi & Okonkwo, 2023).

Financial  stress  represents  a  multidimensional  challenge  affecting  both  economic  stability  and
psychological  well-being.  Research  in  Nigerian  higher  education  contexts  indicates  that  financial  difficulties
frequently produce anxiety, emotional distress, and reduced academic persistence among postgraduate students
(Chukwuemeka & Omotosho, 2021; Ogunyemi & Balogun, 2021; Oluwasegun & Uche, 2022). Students often
develop  coping  strategies  such  as  entrepreneurship,  part-time  employment,  and  reliance  on  family  support  to
manage financial pressure (Akanbi & Aremu, 2022; Edem et al., 2023; Ugwuanyi et al., 2021). Digital financial
platforms  may provide additional tools for managing financial  resources, including budgeting applications and
investment platforms that support financial planning  (Ojo & Afolabi, 2024; Oyediran & Udo, 2023). However,
simplified access to digital credit and online lending systems may also encourage excessive borrowing behaviors
and financial dependency, thereby increasing financial stress rather than reducing it (Adediran & Okonkwo, 2023;
Adeyemo, 2023).

Existing literature on student financial well-being often emphasizes structural economic factors such as
tuition costs, inflation, and limited financial assistance, while psychological processes underlying financial stress
receive comparatively less attention. Financial literacy research highlights the importance of financial knowledge
and budgeting skills in shaping responsible financial behavior (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014; Onyishi, et al., 2021).
Nevertheless, financial stress is not solely determined by economic constraints but is also influenced by cognitive
interpretations,  emotional  responses,  and  behavioral  decision-making  processes.  Cognitive-Behavioral  Theory
(CBT) provides a valuable theoretical framework for understanding how individuals interpret stressful situations
and develop behavioral responses to those challenges (Beck, 1976; Dobson & Dozois, 2019; Onyishi, et al., 2021).
Empirical  studies  have demonstrated  that  cognitive-behavioral  interventions  can  significantly  reduce  academic
stress and improve psychological resilience among students (Eneogu et al., 2024; Eze et al., 2023; Hofmann et al.,
2012; Nwadi et al., 2025). Current research addressing fintech adoption among students has largely focused on
financial inclusion and financial behavior outcomes. Limited attention has been given to the complex interaction
between  digital  financial  technology,  psychological  stress  responses,  and  postgraduate  students’  financial
experiences  in  developing  countries.  Evidence  from  Nigerian  contexts  suggests  that  fintech  adoption  may
simultaneously  enhance  financial  accessibility  while  also  creating  psychological  pressure  associated  with  debt
obligations  and  financial  uncertainty  (Adediran  &  Okonkwo,  2023;  Ibrahim  &  Eze,  2023).  Such  conditions
highlight  the  importance  of  integrating  technological,  economic,  and  psychological  perspectives  in  order  to
develop a more comprehensive understanding of financial stress among postgraduate students.

Three  primary  objectives  guide  this  study.  First,  the  research  examines  the  level  of  financial  stress
experienced  by  Nigerian  postgraduate  students  and  identifies  major  contributing  factors.  Second,  the  study
analyses  the  dual  role  of  digital  financial  technologies  by  investigating  whether  these  technologies  alleviate
financial challenges or amplify financial stress among students. Third, the research applies a Cognitive-Behavioral
Theory  framework  to  explain  how  students’  perceptions,  attitudes,  and  behavioral  responses  mediate  the
relationship between digital financial technology usage and financial stress. Expected contributions include both
theoretical and practical implications. Theoretical contributions emerge through integration of fintech adoption
literature  with  cognitive-behavioral  perspectives  in  explaining  financial  stress  within  postgraduate  education
contexts.  Practical  implications  involve  recommendations  for  universities,  policymakers,  and  financial  service
providers to develop supportive interventions that promote responsible financial technology usage and strengthen
financial  resilience  among  students.  Comprehensive  understanding  of  the  interaction  between  economic

2.  METHOD

This study employed a conceptual and analytical research design, which is commonly used in position
papers aimed at synthesising and critically evaluating existing knowledge rather than generating new empirical
data. Conceptual research allows scholars to integrate theoretical perspectives and empirical findings from prior
studies in order to construct a coherent analytical argument. Through this approach, the present study develops a
reasoned position on whether digital technology functions primarily as a mechanism that reduces financial stress
or as a factor that amplifies financial vulnerability among Nigerian postgraduate students. The research adopted a
qualitative and theory-driven analytical approach guided by Cognitive-Behavioral Theory (CBT). This theoretical
perspective views stress not only as a response to external conditions but also as a result of cognitive appraisal,
beliefs, and behavioural responses (Beck, 1976; Dobson & Dozois, 2019). Applying CBT enables the study to
interpret financial stress as a multidimensional phenomenon shaped by interactions between economic conditions,
technological environments, and students’ psychological interpretations of financial challenges.

Data for this study were obtained from secondary sources, primarily peer-reviewed journal articles, policy
reports,  and  empirical  studies  relevant  to  financial  stress,  digital  financial  technologies,  and  student  financial
behaviour. A total of approximately 70 academic sources published between 2015 and 2024 were reviewed and
analysed. The selected time frame was intended to capture the rapid development of digital financial technologies
and the increasing attention to financial well-being among university students during the past decade. Literature
sources were retrieved through systematic searches of several electronic academic databases, including Scopus,
Web of Science, Google Scholar, and JSTOR. Searches were conducted using combinations of relevant keywords
such as financial stress, postgraduate students, digital financial literacy, financial technology, fintech adoption,
Cognitive-Behavioral  Theory,  and  Nigeria.  Additional  sources  were  identified  through  backward  and  forward
citation tracking in order to ensure comprehensive coverage of relevant literature. Selection of literature followed
purposive criteria to ensure that included studies directly addressed themes relevant to the research objectives.

Discussion

The  present  study  examined  the  relationship  between digital  financial  technology usage  and financial
stress  among  Nigerian  postgraduate  students  using  Cognitive-Behavioral  Theory  (CBT)  as  the  analytical
framework.  Findings  from  the  literature  synthesis  indicate  that  financial  stress  among  postgraduate  students
emerges  from  the  interaction  of  structural  economic  conditions,  technological  financial  environments,  and
cognitive-behavioral processes. Economic pressures such as rising tuition costs, limited financial aid opportunities,
and  unstable  income  sources create  the  structural  foundation  of  financial  stress,  while  students’  psychological
interpretations and behavioral responses shape how these financial challenges are experienced (Abiogu et al., 2020;
Agah et al., 2021). Cognitive-Behavioral Theory emphasizes that emotional and behavioral responses are strongly
influenced by individuals’ cognitive interpretations of stressful situations (Beck, 1976; Dobson & Dozois, 2019;
Okide  et  al.,  2020).  Evidence  from  the  reviewed  literature  indicates  that  postgraduate  students  frequently
experience  maladaptive  cognitive  patterns  when  confronting  financial  difficulties.  Catastrophizing  financial
problems,  negative  self-evaluation  related  to  financial  status,  and  pessimistic  expectations  regarding  academic
completion often intensify financial anxiety (Barrett & Stewart, 2020; Igwe et al., 2022). Such cognitive distortions
may also lead to avoidance behaviors, delayed financial planning, and reduced academic engagement,  thereby
reinforcing the cycle of financial stress.

Findings also reveal the dual role of digital financial technologies in shaping financial experiences among
postgraduate  students.  Digital  financial  platforms  provide  various  tools  that  support  financial  awareness  and
financial  planning.  Budgeting  applications,  mobile  banking  services,  and  automated  savings  platforms  enable
students to monitor expenditures, organise financial resources, and improve financial decision-making (Adeyemo,
2023;  Ojo  &  Afolabi,  2024;  Oyediran  &  Udo,  2023).  Greater  financial  awareness  and  structured  financial
management practices are associated with higher levels of financial confidence and reduced financial uncertainty
among students. Evidence from the reviewed studies simultaneously indicates that digital financial technologies
may  also  intensify  financial  stress  when  used  without  adequate  financial  knowledge  or  behavioral  regulation.
Fintech-based lending applications allow rapid access to credit, yet such accessibility may encourage impulsive
borrowing  and  unsustainable  debt  accumulation  (Adediran  &  Okonkwo,  2023;  Ibrahim  &  Eze,  2023).  Digital
financial  environments  may  also  expose  students  to  financial  misinformation,  online  fraud,  and  continuous
financial decision-making pressures, all of which can increase financial worry and emotional distress (Adeyemo,

Financial  literacy  research  has  consistently  emphasized  the  importance  of  financial  knowledge  and

roles in shaping financial behavior. Financial literacy interventions may therefore become more effective when
combined  with  psychological  strategies  that  address  maladaptive  cognitive  patterns  and  promote  constructive
financial decision-making.

Practical implications emerge for universities, policymakers, and financial service providers. Financial
stress among postgraduate students requires institutional responses that address both financial and psychological
dimensions of student well-being. Financial counseling programs grounded in Cognitive-Behavioral Theory may
help students recognize and modify maladaptive financial beliefs while developing healthier financial behaviors.
Digital  financial  literacy  initiatives  may  also  strengthen  students’  capacity  to  evaluate  financial  risks,  manage
digital financial tools responsibly, and make informed financial decisions. Educational institutions may further
support student financial resilience by integrating digital financial education into university curricula. Financial
literacy  modules  focusing  on budgeting practices,  savings automation,  debt  management,  and  fraud  awareness
could  strengthen  students’  ability  to  navigate  increasingly  complex  digital  financial  environments  Several
limitations should be acknowledged. Conceptual and literature-based methodology limits the ability to directly
measure  the  lived  experiences  of  Nigerian  postgraduate  students.  Evidence  synthesized  in  the  study  reflects
patterns observed in existing literature rather than primary empirical data. Rapid technological changes within the
fintech sector may also introduce emerging financial behaviors that remain insufficiently documented in current
research. Future research should employ empirical methods such as surveys, experiments, or longitudinal studies
to examine the relationship between digital financial technology usage and financial stress among students.

4.  CONCLUSION

The study concludes that financial stress among Nigerian postgraduate students emerges from a complex
interaction between structural economic constraints, digital financial technology usage, and cognitive-behavioral
processes. Digital financial technologies function neither purely as solutions nor solely as sources of stress; rather,
their impact depends on how students cognitively interpret and behaviorally engage with these tools. Cognitive-
Behavioral  Theory  highlights  that  maladaptive  thought  patterns  (catastrophizing  financial  difficulties  or
associating financial hardship with personal failure) can intensify financial anxiety and lead to ineffective financial
behaviors, even when technological resources for financial management are available. Digital financial platforms
therefore hold the potential to reduce financial stress by improving financial awareness, planning, and control, yet
the  same  technologies  may  also  amplify  financial  vulnerability  when  accompanied  by  impulsive  borrowing,
financial  misinformation,  or  limited  financial  literacy.  A  conceptual  understanding  emerging  from  this  study
suggests that effective mitigation of financial stress requires an integrated approach that combines digital financial
literacy,  responsible  fintech  engagement,  and  cognitive-behavioral  coping  strategies,  thereby  enabling
postgraduate  students  to  transform  digital  financial  technologies  into  tools  for  financial  resilience  rather  than
