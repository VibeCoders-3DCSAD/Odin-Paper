## TL;DR

Attitude toward investment and monthly salary predict investment decisions among Filipino young professionals in Laguna, explaining 16% of variance, with male and higher-income participants showing stronger decisions.

## Problem and Motivation

Investment decisions in the Philippines are shaped by more than financial capacity, yet behavioral and perceptual factors such as risk appraisal, institutional trust, and market attitudes remain underexamined in localized urban settings. Understanding these predictors matters because limited capital-market participation constrains household access to wealth-building opportunities. Prior work lacked an integrated, extended Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) analysis focused on mid-sized Philippine cities like Calamba, Santa Rosa, and Biñan.

## Approach

- Cross-sectional online survey of 483 respondents from three key cities in Laguna, Philippines.
- 6-point rating scales measured risk tolerance, attitude toward capital markets, capital market knowledge (financial literacy), government trust, attitude toward investment, and self-reported investment decision.
- Descriptive statistics, group comparisons (sex, monthly salary), Pearson correlations, and stepwise multiple regression.
- Alpha level of 0.05 for significance testing; composite scores computed as means of item responses per construct.

## Findings

1. **Stepwise regression identified attitude toward investment (coefficient = 0.345) and monthly salary (coefficient = 0.368) as significant predictors, explaining 16.2% of the variance (R² = 0.162, p < .001).**
2. Investment decision differed significantly by sex (p = .002), with male respondents scoring higher than female respondents.
3. Investment decision differed significantly across monthly salary brackets (p < .001); the lowest salary group (15,000–45,000) reported the lowest scores.
4. Capital market knowledge showed the strongest correlation with investment decision (r = 0.210, p = .001), followed by attitude toward investment (r = 0.179, p = .003).

- Risk tolerance had the highest mean rating (M = 4.81), government trust the lowest (M = 3.85) on a 6-point scale.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| TPB | Theory of Planned Behavior — a framework linking attitudes, norms, and perceived control to behavioral intention. |
| Stepwise regression | A variable selection method that adds or removes predictors iteratively based on statistical significance. |
| Capital market knowledge | Familiarity with stock market concepts, instruments, and risk–return trade-offs (synonym for financial literacy in this paper). |
| Risk tolerance | Willingness to accept financial uncertainty or potential loss when investing. |
| Government trust | Perceived credibility and fairness of political institutions and their influence on market conditions. |

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference; associations may not reflect true predictive direction.
- Purposive sampling and online-only collection limit representativeness (digital access bias). [unacknowledged]
- Self-reported measures may be influenced by social desirability and response style.
- Modest R² (0.162) indicates substantial variance unexplained; important predictors (e.g., perceived behavioral control, social norms) omitted.
- Stepwise regression may be sample-specific and not generalize across settings.
