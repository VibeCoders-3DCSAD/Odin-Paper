## TL;DR

Aging Filipino domestic workers in Singapore and Hong Kong turn to financial education courses to secure their futures, shifting from breadwinner to self-responsible investor amid limited state and kinship care.

## Problem and Motivation

Aging migrant domestic workers face precarious futures because state-based social protection is minimal and kinship care is uncertain after decades of sending remittances. This matters because millions of women who have worked abroad for 15–35 years risk returning home without savings for old age. Prior research documented remittance flows and family separation but not how financial literacy programmes reshape aspirations and social protection strategies.

## Approach

- Ethnographic fieldwork (2018–2022) with 50+ migrant domestic workers aged 45–65 in Singapore and Hong Kong.
- Observations of financial education courses run by NGOs, corporations, and co-operatives on Sundays.
- In-depth interviews with course participants and non-participants, plus policy officers and NGO representatives.
- Analysis of narratives on saving, investing, saying “no” to family demands, and alternative strategies (activism, faith, land).

## Findings

1. **Only 5%** of overseas Filipinos in Singapore contribute to the state social security system (SSS), and pensions (e.g., 1500 pesos after 30 years) are insufficient.
2. Many women work 15–25 years abroad but return home without savings because they “give in” to never-ending family requests for money.
3. Financial education courses cultivate a transformation narrative: from “breadwinner” (giving everything to family) to “financially smart” (self-responsible investor).

- Migrants enrol to gain a sense of purpose, build peer communities, and avoid being a burden on children who may not care for them.
- Alternatives exist: political activism (demanding state pensions), faith-based resignation, and traditional land purchases.
- Financialisation depoliticises social protection by individualising responsibility, yet participants find genuine agency and new self-understandings.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| OFW | Overseas Filipino Worker – a citizen working abroad under state-labour-export policy. |
| SSS | Social Security System – Philippine state contributory pension scheme. |
| Breadwinner mentality | The belief that a migrant must give all earnings to family back home. |
| Paluwagan | Informal Filipino rotating savings and lending group. |
| Financialisation | The process of turning future security into individual investment products rather than collective state or kinship support. |
| Social protection | Formal (state pensions, insurance) and informal (family, faith) mechanisms that secure wellbeing. |

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper directly informs Odin’s understanding of Filipino financial behavior, specifically the tension between family obligations and personal savings. It justifies the need for budget recommendation and anomaly detection that account for cultural expectations such as remittances, informal lending circles (paluwagan), and emotional pressure from relatives. Although the study focuses on aging migrants abroad, the underlying dynamics of family-based financial demands and the desire for self-reliance are transferable to young professionals in Metro Manila who also face similar cultural pressures.

**Directly justifies:**

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is aging migrant domestic workers (45–65), not young professionals (20–40) in Metro Manila.
- Context of temporary migration abroad (Singapore/Hong Kong) differs from local employment in the Philippines.
- No evaluation of whether financial education actually improves long-term savings outcomes.
- Gender-specific findings (all female participants) may not fully apply to male young professionals.
- Focus on manual transaction behaviour is not addressed; the paper discusses financial mindset, not app-based tracking.

## Limitations

- Sample is limited to Filipino domestic workers in Singapore and Hong Kong; findings may not generalise to other nationalities or host countries.
- Participants in financial education courses are self-selected and likely more motivated than the broader migrant population.
- Paper relies on self-reported narratives and retrospective accounts, not longitudinal tracking of savings outcomes.
- No assessment of whether financial investments (e.g., cacao farm shares) actually yield returns or increase security. [unacknowledged]
- The role of employers in funding courses creates new dependencies that the paper acknowledges but does not fully explore.
