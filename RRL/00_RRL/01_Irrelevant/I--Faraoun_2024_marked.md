ABSTRACT
The rise of the platform economy is one of the most widely debated issues in contemporary studies of work and labor relations.
Scholarly work on platform labor has been permeated by claims of radical restructuring, either caused or exacerbated by the
disruptive properties of algorithms, networks and data‐driven systems of control. However, a growing body of literature has
drawn attention to the weaknesses of such claims, highlighting several continuities in the organization of work between the pre‐
platform and platform economy, including in labor processes and in the regulation of labor‐capital relations. This article reviews
key theoretical interventions within these areas of the literature, arguing that they eventually point toward the lack of historical
distinctiveness of platform labor. It then suggests that a more nuanced and critical understanding of labor restructuring and
platformization as part of a process of systemic social change would benefit from engagement with the theory of Uneven and
Combined Development (U&CD). This engagement would build on two lines of inquiry: workforce diversity and spatial dif-
ferentiation in the platform economy. The article concludes that U&CD is a useful meta‐theoretical framework to understand
change within continuity, accounting for the variegated experiences of platform workers in interconnected spaces of capitalist
accumulation.

2 | Platform Labor Process: Control, Resistance,
and Consent

The role of digital infrastructures and software applications in
the quantification, measurement, supervision, and evaluation of
the labor process is considered as the core feature of platform‐
driven restructuring of work (Kenney and Zysman 2020;
Veen, Barratt, and Goods 2020). In particular, platform com-
panies use an algorithm to automate the allocation of tasks,
implement dynamic pricing systems based on real‐time infor-
mation, and/or evaluate task performance through online rating
systems (Lee et al. 2015). This automation has been termed
“algorithmic management,” which can be defined as “a system
of control where self‐learning algorithms are given the re-
sponsibility for making and executing decisions affecting labour,
thereby limiting human involvement and oversight of the labour
process” (Duggan et al. 2020, 119). In this sense, it has been
viewed as a new form of scientific management methods
advanced by Frederick W. Taylor in the early 20th century,
which sought to enhance industrial productivity and efficiency
through time and resource optimization, routinization of tasks
and the close supervision of worker performance by a foreman
(Edwards 1979). Accordingly, algorithmic management has also
been described by Shibata (2021) as “neo‐Taylorism” or “Digital
Taylorism”: a managerial system where digital technology re-
places the foreman in the role of worker supervision. In this
sense, the role of digital technology is seen as a new way of
intensifying exploitation and preventing
subduing workers,
resistance.

emphasizing the historical continuity of unfair managerial prac-

technological layer. In a similar vein, Joyce (2020) argues that
labor control in the platform economy is fundamentally reliant on
the dull economic compulsion of the “cash nexus” whereby
workers enter into unequal relations of production due to their
dependency on market relations for subsistence. He proposes that
the degree of this compulsion can be understood through
Marx's (1996) concept of subsumption, understood as the process
of bringing formerly independent labor processes under the
control of capital. Subsumption is said to be “formal” when labor
processes are only minimally changed and “real” when they are
effectively transformed by capital. In practice, this translates into
a continuum from independent to dependent labor, with “tran-
sitional” forms of employment characterized by temporal flexi-
bility, piece‐rate systems and the involvement of third parties
such as clients or middlemen, which are common features of
platform work (Calo and Rosenblat 2017; Rosenblat 2018; Hardy
and Camille 2021). It has been pointed out that such transitional
forms are far from new, and can be traced to the pre‐industrial
“putting‐out” system in Europe and the colonies (Stanford 2017;
Joyce 2020; Tucker 2020).

concept should disappear. […] Platform workers do
not benefit from being called “platform labour”. In
fact, the process is just the opposite. The fact [sic] that
platform labour is no different from ordinary labour,

3 | Platform Labor Regulation: Radical Shift or
Continuity?

The regulatory dimension of platform labor has attracted a great
deal of interest and discussion, mostly revolving around the
issue of the legal classification of platform workers. The main
problem stems from the reliance of online labor platforms on an
“on‐demand” labor outsourcing strategy which saves costs and
exempts them from employer obligations (De Stefano 2016;
Healy, Nicholson, and Pekarek 2017). As such, they typically
classify workers as self‐employed or independent contractors
and use terms that avoid association with the legal terminology
of employment (Scholz 2017; Woodcock and Graham 2020).
Since most platform work deviates from direct employer‐
employee relationships, it is seen to function through a “trian-

Although the regulation school offers a useful alternative to the
narrative of radical discontinuity, it has at least two weaknesses
that need to be highlighted. First, making claims of disconti-
nuity based on the novel technological architectures of plat-
forms (virtual networks and smart technologies) is problematic.
While these architectures are technically novel, their socio‐
political logics are far from being so. As pointed out by Peck
and Phillips (2020, 86) in their longue durée analysis inspired by
Fernand Braudel, platform strategies “have hardly broken” with
long‐term patterns of capitalist wealth appropriation through
monopolization, predatory strategies of market control, the use
of obfuscation in pricing and contracts, and symbiotic relations
with political elites that protect
them from working class
contestation. Second, the historical specificity attributed to the
institutions of the platform accumulation regime takes the re-
gimes of Fordism and post‐Fordism, and the transition from the
former to the latter, as unproblematic. However, there are
extensive critiques of Fordism as a mythical ideal‐type that
disregards the racial, class and gender inequalities at its heart,
and the narrow coverage of its employment protection and
welfare institutions (Clarke 1988; Esch 2018; Betti 2016). It has
also been argued that social democratic institutions served to
protect the interests of capital by co‐opting a particular segment
of privileged white, male, middle class, Western workers, while
providing regulatory means to control the poor and black and
female workers (Baca 2004; Martin 2017). In this sense, it could
be argued that the forms of flexibilization and disciplinary
tendencies associated with post‐Fordist and platform regimes
signal not an erosion of the protections afforded under a stable
social contract between labor and capital, but rather the partial
displacement of some previous forms of privilege experienced by
some workers (see also the related discussion on precarity in
Shin, Kalleberg, and Hewison (2023) in this journal). Moreover,
as Berg (2022) suggests, the narratives of structural change and
crisis which are attached to concepts such as post‐Fordism and
neoliberalism are told from the perspective of capital, and tend
to dismiss the subjectivities of many platform and gig workers
who willingly depart from “Fordist” work arrangements, in full
awareness of the disadvantages involved in doing so.

Lastly, the concept of post‐Fordism is recurrent within discus-
sions on the role of new ICTs in the re‐organization of capitalist
production and circulation and its de‐territorialization, epito-
mized by Castells' ([1996] 2010) concept of the network society.
The latter suggests that ICTs introduced radically new logics of
space and time which dissociated work from a physical site of
production. This has been linked to the rise of remote work such
as call centers, which are now seen as an early “laboratory for
flexible work practices” or “precursors of platforms” (Valen-
duc 2021, 87). Post‐Fordism also has a related meaning in the
autonomist Marxist tradition (Hardt and Negri 2000; Lazzar-
ato 1996), associated in broad terms with the increasing

importance of “immaterial” (informational, communicative,
emotional) tasks as sources of surplus value and means of control.
The latter informs Wang and Tomassetti's (2024) recent argu-
ment that platforms mobilize network logics to bring together
material and immaterial labor into the “social factory,” creating a
“neoliberal, datafied, and somewhat post‐organisational society”
(11). Yet this argument replicates long‐standing conceptual
flaws, notably the re‐invention as immaterial labor what has long
existed as unpaid, invisible reproductive labor performed mostly
by women outside of the typical Fordist workplace (Jarrett 2014).
The idea that platforms reconstruct labor‐capital relations by
integrating material and immaterial labor reifies the problematic
separation between the industrial factory and the household, as
sites of capital accumulation, social regulation and struggle.
Forms of labor that were traditionally seen as “women's work”
suddenly gain new importance when extensively sourced out of
the domestic sphere. The transformative effect attributed to
networks in this respect seems to misconstrue the role of tech-
nical innovation in such labor's increased visibility as qualitative
changes in social relations.

Combined Development of Capitalism

it is evident that academic

respect to the relations of the labor process as well as the meso‐
and macro‐level structures of labor regulation and their medi-

this impasse through engagement with the theory of Uneven
and Combined Development (U&CD) in future studies of plat-

two emerging empirical research directions that can be fruitfully
integrated into this research agenda, although they do not
currently engage explicitly with this theory.

Based on the work of Leon Trotsky (2008), U&CD is a theory
that explains diversity within capitalism with reference to a
“universal
(van der

Linden 2007). Its basic premise is that the process of capitalist
expansion happens under conditions of uneven societal and
institutional development, such that the capitalist mode of
production co‐exists and combines with forms of social organi-

stages of capitalism. Trotsky formulated U&CD to explain how a
country such as Russia, seen to be in a “backward” stage of

widely held belief inherited from Enlightenment thought, and
adopted in some Marxist streams, that so‐called backward so-
cieties would undergo a predetermined succession of develop-
mental stages, arguing instead that their interaction with core
capitalist countries would compel them to catch up and leap
over intermediate stages, resulting in a fusion of archaic and
modern technical and social formations (Davidson 2006). As
such, it has been particularly useful in explaining the variegated
trajectories of late developing countries, especially the post‐
colonial developing world, without falling into the stageist

unevenness and combination.

In this respect, U&CD offers a major strength when theorizing
the temporality of capitalist development and changes in its
social relations. Namely, it avoids the problematic idea that the
latter moves forward through a series of successive phases that
result from a steady improvement in technological apparatuses,
the dissolution of previous social orders and the ushering in of
new ones. As explained in previous sections, this idea is com-
mon in accounts of platformization,
including those more
balanced and sociologically informed. For instance, the regula-
tion school's epochal lens to historical change identifies non‐
linear patterns of development in the movement from one
regime of accumulation and mode of regulation to another, but
does not sufficiently take account of heterogeneous patterns that
defy classification into discrete socio‐historical orders. As
Makki (2015, 489) points out,

this formulation […] presumes a degree of uniformity
that subsumes social and historical unevenness in a
diachronic succession of temporal phases. But if we
proceed from a more differentiated conception of
historical temporality in which time is not simply a
uniform medium, it becomes possible to appreciate
plural, uneven, and interwoven processes of social
change. Capitalist development so conceived does not
move according to a single uniform beat, but operates
through the complex contemporaneity of its unevenly
combined social forms.

Acknowledging this differentiated conception allows us to see
how capitalism reconstitutes itself along pre‐existing social
forms which remain important to the subsumption of labor into
its productive rhythms. As such, this explains how organiza-
tional properties observed in platform work arrangements
closely correspond to archaic forms that have existed in previous
stages of capitalist development, such as the putting out system,
or even bonded labor (Li 2024). This also means that the pres-
ence, in the platform economy and beyond, of “neoliberal”
mechanisms of discipline, does not signal a new mode of
toward
regulation

transformative movement

nor

a

disembeddedness. Capital simultaneously holds a differentiated
set of relationships with labour that are partly path‐dependent
on pre‐existing unevenness in the workforce. As such, capital
elicits productivity through consent or coercion depending on
workers' structural advantages and vulnerabilities tied to
gender, race, citizenship, skills, etc. Capital may also conform to
institutional,
ideological or occupational norms that were
gained through stronger labour activism (see for instance
“above‐the‐line” Hollywood workers' responses to the rise of
Internet media markets: Kleingartner 2001) or grant privileges
to workers in a particularly high value‐added segment of the
production process.

It can thus be argued that the universal feature of capitalism in
the platform as in the pre‐platform age is that its accumulation
process involves the extraction of surplus value through the very
multiplicity of forms of labor and labor market institutions. This
offers a corrective to the view of platform‐driven capitalist
transformation as a historical rupture, while also adding nuance
by connecting the emergence of platform labor outsourcing to
the differential exploitation of labor, which results not only from
capital's differential
investment in the forces of production
(Barker 2006), but also on how labor's agency takes shape across
sectors and spaces of accumulation (Herod 2006). Converting
this approach into a research agenda on platform labor involves
“a framework where structural, systemic change is connected to
the experiences, political subjectivities and relations of power
associated with particular types of labour” while also acknowl-
edging how workers on a global scale are “bound together by an
ever‐proliferating international division of labour” (Taylor 2014,
135). Accordingly, theorizing change and continuity in the
context of the platform economy requires further attention to
both workforce diversity and its spatial differentiation in mul-
tiple scales of the capitalist world economy. Below I summarize
how these two dimensions of analysis correspond to emerging
research directions in the platform labor literature, as well as
the remaining tasks for future research.

The first dimension of analysis is workforce diversity and its
link with varying constraints on workers' agency that do not
stem from platformization. While a number of scholars have
found that many platform workers are vulnerable due to their
race, gender, citizenship and overlapping minority group status
(van Doorn 2017; van Doorn, Ferrari, and Graham 2023),
theoretically driven studies on the subject are quite few.
Notably, Gebrial (2022) introduces the concept of “racial plat-
form capitalism” in her study of migrant Uber drivers in Lon-
don, arguing that uberization was not simply concomitant with,
but grounded in existing processes of racialization that actively
produce a disposable surplus population, constituted as a racial
“other.” Similarly, Alimahomed‐Wilson and Reese (2021) and
Animento (2024) draw from the Black Radical tradition (Rob-
inson 1983) to analyze how the devaluation and exploitation of
platform workers in the United States and across Europe,
respectively, are rooted in colonial history and the cumulative
production of racial differences and hierarchies, reproduced in
technological
infrastructures. Complementing this critique,
Kampouri (2022) calls for the gendering of platform labor
studies through the lens of Feminist social reproduction theory,
noting how the commodification of affective labor in the plat-
form economy reinforces gender inequalities through the

Taking such diversity into account is important not only to
understand the extent to which platforms alter relations of
coercion and consent (if at all), but also workers' political sub-
jectivities and how they choose to exercise their agency,
including what regulatory interventions they support or reject.
A case in point is the reception of legislation to protect platform
workers, such as California's AB5 and the EU Platform Work
Directive, both of which attracted opposition from workers
themselves. Organizations such as California's Freelancers
Against AB5 in the U.S. and Freelance Movement in Europe have
collectively lobbied against such laws by filing petitions and
lawsuits and lobbying against their adoption (The New York
Times 2021; Telegram 2023; Hodgson 2024). They emphasize
that such laws focus on the misclassification of certain low‐
skilled workers and do not reflect the situation of genuinely
independent freelancers, thereby posing a threat to their au-
tonomy and flexibility in this type of arrangement
(Ker-

workers; for instance, delivery workers organized under the
Autonomous Association of Riders in Spain protested vehe-
mently against the introduction of the Riders' Law, which aimed
to recognize them as de facto employees of platform companies
(Cater 2021). It appears thus that a significant number of
workers do not experience algorithmic management, fragmen-
tation of work, remoteness and other characteristics of platform
work as more precarious, degrading or coercive than non‐
platform‐based work arrangements, and to dismiss their posi-
tion as cooptation or an inability to conceive of a different
scenario (Vieira 2023) is counter‐productive.

Indeed, the main legislative device used in the regulation of
platform work is the “presumption of employment,” which ex-
tends coverage to platform workers provided that they meet the
criteria of control and subordination tied to the traditional
concept of employment. However, as discussed by Aloisi
et al. (2023) the narrow definition of employment not only
leaves room for platform companies to redefine their contrac-
tual terms with workers to circumvent the rules, but it also fails
to reflect concrete labor processes in many occupations and
work arrangements which inherently defy binary classification
into employment or self‐employment. The idea that classifying

platform workers as employees would solve the problem reflects
the reductionist assumption that platforms have disrupted pre-
viously stable, continuous and more desirable working ar-
rangements, and the discourse of platformization as an
extension of neoliberal crisis and market discipline which has
shaped much discussion on the subject. This framing of plat-
form work has so far informed a blanket solution which elides
complex issues that lead to differentiated labor market out-
comes for workers.

In this sense, thinking from the perspective of U&CD advances
the theoretical debate as well as the practicalities of regulation.
One implication is that platform worker legislation would be
inadequate, and in some cases merely cosmetic, without
comprehensive reforms of immigration laws, sector‐specific as
well as racial, gender equality and anti‐discrimination laws;
areas which have long provided mechanisms for capital to apply
different rules to various groups of workers. Hence, much of
what has been conceptualized as specific features of algorithmic
control and neoliberal discipline in platform labor might be
better explained by the more deeply ingrained and overlapping
racialized, gendered and migrant labor regimes, as well as issues
of poverty and exclusion which are often the underlying causes
of precarity and lack of choice. For example, many migrant
workers resort
to platform work due to the difficulty of
accessing standard paid employment or to avoid the oversight of
immigration authorities, and thus are not automatically pro-
tected and might even evade reclassification for fear of retalia-
tion from platforms, or deportation in the case of undocumented
migrants (Leo and Grossi 2023). Regulating platforms is likely to
fail without addressing the complex socio‐political mechanisms
of differentiation through which capital overexploits those
construed as the dispensable “other.”

Furthermore, risk externalization and fragmentation in the
platform economy depends on spatial configurations and re-
lationships that predate and fuse with digital outsourcing. This
leads to the second dimension of unevenness and combination,
which is the differentiation of platform workers across global
labor markets, and how it maps into, or deviates from, long‐
term geographical divisions and power asymmetries between
the Global North and Global South. Some scholars highlight the
colonial and imperialist foundations of the platform economy.
Notably, Fuchs's (2021) concept of “international division of
digital labor” captures the spatial organization of labor that is
functionally necessary to the platform economy, including slave
and forced mine labor in Congo where the core materials of
digital infrastructures are extracted, “Fordist” electronics factory
workers in China, high‐paid software engineers in Silicon Valley
and their low‐paid counterparts in India. At the heart of this
division is transnational monopoly capital's realization of profit
through the differential integration of workers into a global
competitive system. The question then is whether the same di-
visions and interdependencies are at play in the platform
economy's service sector.

In this respect, Graham, Hjorth, and Lehdonvirta's (2017) study
of crowdwork reveals an overarching pattern whereby platform
operators are concentrated in the Global North, while most
crowdworkers are outsourced from the Global South, which
likely further constrains their bargaining power. Others directly

On the other hand, mapping spatial differentiation also entails
critical reflection on the applicability of classical concepts such
as colonialism and imperialism, which may oversimplify
contemporary socio‐spatial formations (Casilli 2017). Scholars
should be cautious about treating the Global North and South as
static or internally homogeneous entities, as the combined
development of capitalism has generated significant shifts that
shape the growth of platform markets. Notably, the rise of state‐
backed platform capital in late developers such as China and
Brazil has created new configurations of regional subordination
and hierarchies of labor exploitation that align with nationalist
authoritarian projects (Grohmann and Qiu 2020) or what
Seto (2024) calls “sub‐imperialist” strategies, which further
complicate platform workers' struggles. Furthermore, the neo‐
colonial parallel in platforms' labor outsourcing in the Global
South is not reducible to erstwhile patterns of political and
economic domination, nor should it undermine articulations of
class relations and workers' agency in the Global South. This is
duly illustrated by Roberts' (2019) research on commercial
content moderation in the Philippines. Overlapping with pre‐

firms are utilized by platforms to manage a fragmented work-
force through various engagement mechanisms, which include
putting a “positive spin on the Philippines' long history of
colonization and cultural domination from the West” (65) to
attract skilled urban youth. Such work is part of urban devel-

and often aligns with white‐collar occupational status by local
standards. Far from being forced laborers or passive victims of
neoliberal deception, Filipino content moderators clearly un-
derstand their status as on‐demand labor doing the “dirty work”
of moderation out of sight and mind, and construe it in their
terms of an imaginary of success, transcendence of traditional
labor control and the achievement of a middle class status
(Soriano and Cabañes 2019).

and discontinuities or hybridizations of pre‐existing structures,

multiple scales of the global economy. This entails turning
attention toward the variegated experiences, differential
embeddedness and exploitation of platform workers, as well as
how they are interlinked with each other across differentiated
spaces of capitalist accumulation. To this end, this article sug-
gests that Uneven and Combined Development (U&CD) is a
useful meta‐theoretical framework for reaching a historically
grounded interpretation of labor restructuring in the platform
economy, by building on studies of workforce diversity and in-
equalities based on race, gender, citizenship, skills, etc. as well
as studies that map the relationships between workers within
and beyond platform economy sectors across multi‐level,
mutually constitutive socio‐spatial and functional divisions.
Future research that integrates empirics with theoretical work
along these lines of inquiry can better situate platform labor in
the unevenness and combination of capitalist development.
