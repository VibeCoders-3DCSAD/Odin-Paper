## TL;DR

Fast Internet access increases employment by 3–13 percentage points in Sub-Saharan Africa, driven by skilled jobs and firm entry, but platform work lacks social protection.

## Problem and Motivation

Digitalisation affects labour markets, but evidence from developing countries is patchy and “context matters” because access and adoption vary widely. Understanding these effects is crucial for policies that help workers benefit from digital progress rather than being harmed by it. Prior work lacked causal estimates of infrastructure expansion and systematic data on digital platform work in low-income settings.

## Approach

- Data: Multiple micro-datasets (Demographic and Health Surveys, Afrobarometer) from 12 Sub-Saharan countries, plus World Bank Enterprise Surveys.
- Methods: Differences-in-differences comparing locations that gained fast Internet (submarine cable arrival) to those that did not; instrumental variables for mobile phone coverage using terrain curvature.
- Outcome measures: Employment probability, labour force participation, wage employment, firm entry/exit, productivity.
- Platform work analysis: ILO survey of 5,000 location-based and 2,900 online platform workers across developing regions.

## Findings

1. Fast Internet access raises employment probability by **3.1 to 13.2 percentage points** depending on country set – driven by skilled jobs, not unskilled.
2. Mobile broadband in Nigeria increases labour force participation by 3.3% and wage employment by 1.4%.
3. Mobile phone coverage in rural South Africa increases employment by 15%, entirely from wage jobs, mainly for women.
4. Online labour supply comes overwhelmingly from low/middle-income countries (India 28.6%, Bangladesh 14.3%, Pakistan 11.9%), while demand is almost entirely from high-income countries.
- Location-based platform workers earn more than traditional counterparts in most countries (e.g., +86% for Ghana taxi drivers), but social protection coverage is low (51–53% have health insurance).
- Only two rigorous impact evaluations of digital skills training exist; both find that job referrals or networking platform training (not just skills) drive employment gains.

## Limitations

- Employment estimates rely on identification assumptions that may not fully hold (e.g., submarine cable placement not fully random).
- Platform worker surveys have low response rates and non-representative samples (ILO survey removed 30% due to partial completion).
- Digital skills data are scarce and outdated, especially for low-income countries (most SDG Indicator 4.4.1 data missing for Africa).
- Only two rigorous evaluations of digital skills training exist; both are context-specific (Kenya, South Africa) and not generalizable.
- Paper does not analyze effects on informal employment separately, despite informality being dominant in developing country labour markets [unacknowledged].
