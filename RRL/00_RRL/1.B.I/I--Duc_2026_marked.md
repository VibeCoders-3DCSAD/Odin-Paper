Policy pathways towards inclusive protection

Closing  the  protection  gap  requires moving  beyond
the rigid employee/contractor binary. Policy innovation
should  focus  on  creating  portable,  prorated,  and
universal benefits.

Establish  earning-based  contribution  systems  where
obligations  are  tied  to  work  activity,  not  contractual
form. A small levy on each transaction or a percentage of
platform  fee  could  be  directed  into  dedicated  social
security  funds,  with  contributions  split  between  the
platform, the user (as client), and the worker.

Workers  could  accumulate  rights  in  individual
portable benefit accounts linked to their digital identity.
Benefits  would  follow  them  across  multiple  platforms
and gigs, covering health, injury, and pension savings.

Governments must explicitly extend core protections,
starting  with  work  injury  insurance,  to  all  workers,
irrespective  of  classification.  This  can  be  financed
through a platform levy or a broader digital services tax.
Creating  formal  tripartite-plus  forums  (government,
platforms, worker associations, civil society) is essential
to  design  feasible  and  fair  solutions,  including  setting
minimum earning standards and transparent deactivation
procedures.

Conclusion

The  rise  of platform  work  in  ASEAN has laid  bare
the inadequacy of 20th-century social protection models
in  a  21st-century  digital  labour  market.  The  current
system, which permits platforms to externalize the social
costs  of  work,  is  creating  a  vast,  vulnerable  workforce
without safety nets. While the gig economy offers valued
flexibility, it must not become a synonym for heightened
precarity and abandoned social responsibility.

The  diverse  experiments  within  ASEAN,  from
Thailand's  judicial  interventions  to  Malaysia's  portable
scheme,  provide  a  foundation  for
learning.  The
imperative  now  is  for  bolder,  systemic  innovation  that
recognizes work wherever it occurs and ensures that all
workers, regardless of how they are engaged, contribute
to and benefit from collective risk-pooling mechanisms.

The future of social cohesion and equitable growth in
ASEAN depends on building an inclusive digital social
contract  that  leaves  no  worker  behind.  Achieving  this
requires political will to recalibrate the balance between
platform  innovation  and  worker  security,  ensuring  the
region's  digital  transformation  is  both  economically
dynamic and socially sustainable.
