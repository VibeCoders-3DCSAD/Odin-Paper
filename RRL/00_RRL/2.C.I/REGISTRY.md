## I--Askhiyah_2026

### Description

Examines the causal and correlational relationships between the intensity of digital finance usage and multiple dimensions of consumer economic behavior, which provides a foundation for understanding the behavioral rationale behind protected expense categories.
- Notes Improved Financial Literacy: Identifies that digital finance adoption improves financial literacy, planning behavior, and management practices, suggesting users possess the behavioral framework necessary to categorize expenses based on their perceived importance (protected vs. negotiable).
- Details Consumption-Savings Trade-offs: Finds that digital finance adoption increases household consumption expenditure while reducing savings balances, which highlights the trade-offs in financial behavior that could lead users to designate certain consumption categories as non-negotiable despite optimization pressure.

## I--Levi_2025

### Description

Demonstrates that consumers, when exposed to a consumption-oriented financial sustainability index and a comparison with past spending, reduced their **discretionary spending**, suggesting that non-discretionary categories were not reduced.
- Identified Discretionary Categories: Uses **discretionary spending**, defined as spending on items over which consumers have relatively more control and can adjust over a short period (e.g., entertainment, restaurants), as the main variable of interest in the experiment.
- Categories Difficult to Adjust: Confirms that there were **no significant differences in spending** on categories relatively difficult to adjust in the short run, such as **gas, groceries, telephone, or utilities**, supporting the idea of categories that are effectively non-negotiable or protected.

## I--Nokhiz_&_Ruwanpathirana_2025

### Description

Examines how systemic barriers, such as fixed obligatory payments and algorithmic manipulation, constrain consumer agency, leading to a loss of financial autonomy, which suggests a need for recognizing non-negotiable expenses.
- Fixed Payments Cause Financial Risk: Constructs formal analytical scenarios demonstrating that even rational agents experience early financial ruin when their agency is limited across structural, behavioral, or temporal dimensions by factors like fixed obligatory payments.
- Non-Negotiable Essential Expenses: Assumes that individuals initially allocate resources to non-negotiable essential expenses, such as food, shelter, taxes, and transportation, before budgeting remaining funds for discretionary purchases, providing evidence for category protection logic based on user behavior.

## L--Dimaranan_&_Dy_2025

### Description

Identifies remittances to family of origin as a non-negotiable expense category for Filipina wives, which is a "personal choice" that reflects the deep cultural value of family-centeredness among Filipinos.
- Budget Categories: Notes that a working monthly budget includes fixed expenses, variable/flexible expenses, loans and liabilities, and subscriptions/memberships, suggesting these are the core categories that must be managed and prioritized.
- Risk of Unplanned Remittances: Explains that emergency requests for additional, unplanned remittances place the monthly family budget and savings at risk, which underscores the need for setting clear boundaries on this non-negotiable expense to ensure financial stability.

## L--Mansueto_et-al_2025

### Description

Examines the financial resource allocation and key spending patterns of Filipino retirees in Siquijor, providing a basis for understanding which expenses are treated as non-negotiable necessities.
Priority Spending Categories: Identifies essential living expenses, such as utilities (3.94) and groceries (3.80), as taking priority in retirees' budgets, suggesting these categories are implicitly treated as protected or non-negotiable.
Healthcare Spending Priority: Notes that healthcare expenditures are primarily allocated to prescription medications (3.60), reflecting the prioritization of curative treatments over limited spending on preventive care (3.36) due to financial constraints.
