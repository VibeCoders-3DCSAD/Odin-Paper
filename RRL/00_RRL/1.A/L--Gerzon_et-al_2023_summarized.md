## TL;DR

Philippine public health nurses have high financial literacy and great financial well-being; monthly income predicts literacy, and literacy strongly predicts well-being (rs=0.660).

## Problem and Motivation

Nurses globally and in the Philippines reportedly have low financial literacy, yet no study had assessed financial literacy and well‑being among public health nurses in a first‑class Philippine province. Financial literacy directly affects personal financial decisions, and financially stressed employees suffer from burnout, turnover, and reduced productivity. Prior work lacked a focused assessment of Filipino nurses, leaving a gap in understanding their financial capability and its link to well‑being.

## Approach

- Sample: 178 permanent public health nurses, stratified random sampling, from a first‑class province in the Philippines (2022‑2023).
- Instrument: Researcher‑made 52‑item 4‑point Likert questionnaire, with domains: financial knowledge, financial behavior, financial discipline, financial security, financial resiliency.
- Validity/reliability: Lawshe CVR = 0.91; Cronbach’s α = 0.945 (financial literacy) and 0.904 (financial well‑being).
- Analysis: Descriptive (mean, SD, frequency) and correlational (Pearson, Point Biserial, Rank Biserial, Spearman).
- Framework: Anchored on Joo’s (1998) Conceptual Model of Personal Financial Wellness.

## Findings

1. Overall financial literacy level was **“High” (M=3.22, SD=0.39)** on a 4‑point scale.
2. Overall financial well‑being extent was **“Great” (M=3.03, SD=0.46)**.
3. Only monthly income correlated with financial literacy [r(176)=0.223, p=0.003]; age, sex, civil status, and dependents showed no correlation.
4. No demographic factor correlated with financial well‑being.

- **Financial literacy and financial well‑being showed a strong positive correlation [rs(176)=0.660, p=0.000]**.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Financial literacy | Combination of financial knowledge and financial behavior. |
| Financial well‑being | State of financial affairs including discipline, security, and resiliency. |
| Financial behavior | Actions related to saving, investing, budgeting, spending, and borrowing. |
| Financial discipline | Ability to control spending, manage debt, and live within means. |
| Financial security | Feeling of being financially safe in the present and future. |
| Financial resiliency | Ability to absorb and recover from financial setbacks. |

**Limits of relevance:**

- Study population is nurses, not specifically young professionals aged 20–40; income levels and spending patterns may differ.
- Cross‑sectional design cannot establish causality between literacy and well‑being (only correlation).
- Sample limited to one province in the Philippines; findings may not generalize to all Filipino young professionals.
- No algorithm, ML model, or system evaluation is discussed — relevance is at the behavioral and demographic background level.

## Limitations

- Sample restricted to one first‑class province and only public health nurses, limiting generalizability to other Filipino workers.
- Self‑reported survey data may be subject to social desirability and recall bias.
- Cross‑sectional design prevents causal inference between financial literacy and well‑being [unacknowledged].
- Researcher‑made instrument, though validated, may not align with international standardized surveys like S&P or OECD [unacknowledged].
- No qualitative component to explain *why* nurses have high literacy or how they practice financial behaviors.
