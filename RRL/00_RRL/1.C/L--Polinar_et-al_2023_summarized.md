## TL;DR

Non-teaching staff have moderate personal finance knowledge and practice, but emergency fund and investment behaviors are weak, and knowledge does not correlate with practice for emergency funds or debt management.

## Problem and Motivation

Little literature exists on the personal finance of non-teaching staff in Philippine universities, despite evidence that only 2 out of 10 Filipinos answer basic financial questions correctly. The COVID-19 pandemic’s economic shock highlighted widespread financial vulnerability, making understanding knowledge-practice gaps critical for recovery. Prior studies focused on teachers or business owners, leaving non-teaching staff unexamined.

## Approach

- Sample: 50 non-teaching staff from a private university in Cebu City, selected via simple random sampling (permanent position, ≥1 year tenure).
- Design: Descriptive-correlational, using an adopted 4-point Likert survey questionnaire from Polinar (2022).
- Variables: Budgeting, saving and spending, emergency fund, debt management, insurance, investment.
- Analysis: Weighted mean for degree of knowledge and practice; Pearson correlation for relationships between knowledge and practice.
- Data collection: Printed questionnaires and Google Forms during the pandemic, with health protocols followed.

## Findings

1. Grand mean knowledge = 3.10 (moderately knowledgeable); budgeting (3.29) and saving/spending (3.32) were “highly knowledgeable.”
2. **Grand mean practice = 2.71 (moderately practiced)**; emergency fund (2.42) and investment (2.20) were “less practiced.”
3. Significant positive correlations (p < 0.05): budgeting (r=0.939), saving/spending (r=0.839), insurance (r=0.969), investment (r=0.973).
4. No significant correlation for emergency fund (p=0.052) or debt management (p=0.053) – knowledge does not predict practice.
5. Investment knowledge (2.56) is higher than practice (2.20), indicating a notable gap.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| Personal finance | Management of individual financial activities including budgeting, saving, spending, debt, insurance, and investment. |
| Budgeting | Planning how to allocate income across expenses and savings. |
| Saving and spending | Setting aside money for future use versus using money for current consumption. |
| Emergency fund | Money set aside specifically for unexpected expenses (e.g., medical bills, job loss). |
| Debt management | Strategies to handle and repay borrowed money. |
| Insurance | Financial product that provides protection against specific risks (e.g., health, life). |
| Investment | Allocating money into assets (stocks, bonds, etc.) to generate returns. |
| Descriptive-correlational | Research design that measures variables and tests relationships without manipulation. |
| Weighted mean | Average where each value is multiplied by a weight before summing. |
| Pearson correlation coefficient | Statistical measure (r) of linear relationship between two variables, ranging from -1 to +1. |

**Limits of relevance:**

## Limitations

- Small sample size (n=50) from one university – limits generalizability to other institutions or regions. [unacknowledged]
- Self-reported knowledge and practice may suffer from social desirability bias – respondents may overstate good behaviors.
- No longitudinal data – causal direction between knowledge and practice cannot be established.
- Survey was adapted from a study on teachers – may not be fully validated for non-teaching staff roles.
- Does not measure actual financial outcomes (e.g., savings amounts, debt levels) – only perceived practice.
