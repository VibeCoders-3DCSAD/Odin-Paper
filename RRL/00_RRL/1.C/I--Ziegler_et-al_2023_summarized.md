## TL;DR

Millennials and female-led micro-enterprises in ASEAN primarily use digital lending for daily expenses and working capital, with fintech complementing rather than replacing traditional banking.

## Problem and Motivation

Traditional banking systems in ASEAN under-serve many individuals and micro, small and medium enterprises (MSMEs), leaving a credit gap. Digital finance platforms such as P2P lending and buy now, pay later (BNPL) have grown rapidly, but little empirical data exists on who uses them, why, and with what outcomes. Prior studies measured transaction volumes but not the borrower profiles, decision factors, or post-financing impacts at the customer level.

## Approach

- Survey of 600 cleaned responses from individual consumers and MSMEs across Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, Singapore, and Thailand.
- Respondents had used a digital alternative finance platform between 2020 and 2021.
- Models analyzed: P2P/marketplace consumer lending (n=272), BNPL (n=138), P2P/marketplace business lending (n=150), invoice trading (n=25), equity crowdfunding (n=15).
- Four research themes: respondent demographics, relationship with traditional finance, financing experience, post-financing outcomes and Covid-19 impact.
- Descriptive analysis applied to quantitative data; content analysis to qualitative responses.

## Findings

1. **44%** of P2P consumer borrowers and **54%** of BNPL users were aged 25–34 (Millennials).
2. **57%** of P2P business borrowers were female; **54%** of MSME respondents were female-led.
3. Primary borrowing purpose: **49%** of consumer P2P borrowers used funds for day-to-day expenses; **39%** of BNPL users for fashion/apparel.
4. **65%** of consumer P2P borrowers and **60%** of BNPL users rated "speed of receiving funds" or "zero/low interest" as very important decision factors.
5. **89%** of P2P business borrowers never missed a payment; default rate was **1%** — below the World Bank’s 3%+ NPL average.
6. More than **50%** of P2P business borrowers reported increased revenue, profit, and employment after financing.

- Fintech use led to increased use of banking products (e.g., savings accounts, personal loans), showing complementarity.

## Relevance to Odin

**Topics:**

1.C — Financial Behavior of Filipino Young Professionals

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper provides empirical evidence on the financial behavior of digital finance users in ASEAN, including the Philippines. It shows that Millennials (ages 25–34) are the primary borrowers, using funds mainly for day-to-day expenses — directly relevant to Odin’s target demographic. The finding that fintech complements rather than replaces banking (users increase savings account usage) supports Odin’s assumption that a budgeting tool can coexist with traditional financial products. The paper’s identification of speed and low interest as key decision factors informs Odin’s value proposition: users prioritize immediacy and low friction, which Odin’s manual-input model must address through ease of use rather than gamification.

**Directly justifies:**

- "In ASEAN, 49% of P2P consumer borrowers use digital loans for day-to-day expenses, indicating that daily spending management is a primary financial pain point for young adults."
- "Millennials (ages 25–34) constitute the largest user group (44–54%) of digital finance platforms in Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, Singapore, and Thailand."
- "After using digital finance, 52% of borrowers began using or increased use of personal savings accounts, showing that fintech can drive banking adoption rather than cannibalize it."
- "Default rates on digital lending (1%) are below the regional bank NPL average (3%+), suggesting responsible borrowing behavior among this demographic."

**Limits of relevance:**

- The study covers all ASEAN countries; specific Philippines-only data (n=200 after sampling) may not fully represent Metro Manila young professionals.
- The study focuses on credit access (borrowing), not spending management or budgeting, which is Odin’s core function.
- Behavioral findings are derived from borrowers of fintech lending platforms, not from users of personal finance management apps; motivations may differ.

## Limitations

- Sample not evenly distributed across countries and verticals; no cross-country or cross-model statistical comparison performed.
- Quantitative figures (income, borrowed amounts) are approximate; outliers were removed and stratified sampling applied.
- The paper does not measure loan quality, price, or diversity of offers; only borrower-reported data.
- Causality not established: observed banking relationship changes may be correlated with but not caused by fintech use. [unacknowledged]
- Covid-19 impact data relies on self-report during the pandemic period; no pre-pandemic baseline for comparison.
