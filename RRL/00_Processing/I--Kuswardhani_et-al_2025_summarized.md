## TL;DR

Frequent e-wallet use increases impulsive spending among Generation Z by reducing the perceived pain of paying.

## Problem and Motivation

E-wallets’ convenience and speed may foster impulsive consumption, but systematic evidence linking specific features to consumptive behavior in Gen Z was missing. This demographic is financially formative, making the question urgent for financial well‑being. No prior synthesis had consolidated empirical findings on digital payment psychology and youth spending.

## Approach

- Systematic literature review following PRISMA guidelines.
- Searched Scopus, Web of Science, ScienceDirect, ProQuest, and Google Scholar (2020–2025).
- Keywords: “e‑wallet AND Generation Z,” “digital payments AND consumer behavior,” “mobile wallet AND impulsive buying.”
- Initial 186 articles → 42 after deduplication → final 28 empirical studies.
- Thematic analysis of purchase frequency, impulse buying, promotional effects, and behavioral triggers.

## Findings

1. E‑wallet usage shows a **significant positive correlation** with spending frequency among Gen Z (p < 0.005, from Lestari et al., 2003).
2. Cashback, discounts, and flash sales create perceived “value‑saving,” driving purchases even when unneeded.
3. Push notifications and gamification (loyalty tiers, spending streaks) condition automatic, habitual spending.
4. Most Gen Z users lack budgeting tools in e‑wallets, leading to post‑purchase regret and difficulty saving.
5. Mental decoupling (disconnecting payment from money awareness) reduces the psychological barrier to spending.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| E‑wallet | Digital payment app that stores money and enables cashless transactions (think: phone as wallet). |
| Generation Z | Individuals born 1997–2012, the first fully digital native cohort. |
| Mental decoupling | Psychological separation of spending action from awareness of losing money [pain‑free paying]. |
| Consumptive behavior | Spending driven more by impulse and emotion than by rational need. |
| Digital nudge | Interface cue (e.g., limited‑time offer) that subtly guides user decisions. |

## Critical Citations

- [Thaler, 1985] — Mental accounting theory explains why digital money feels less “real” than cash.
- [Chatterjee & Kumar, 2020] — Foundational evidence that e‑wallets weaken financial restraint.
- [Liu et al., 2021] — Established promotional features as direct drivers of consumer behavior.

## Relevance to Odin

**Topics:**

A.1 Spending and Budgeting Behavior of Filipino Young Professionals; E.1 Anomaly Detection in Personal Finance Systems; F.1 User Behavioral Profiling in Filipino Personal Finance Contexts

**Contribution to Odin:**

This systematic review provides empirical evidence that friction‑free digital payments and promotional nudges significantly increase impulsive and habitual spending among young adults. It directly justifies Odin’s inclusion of behavioral anomaly detection (to flag unplanned over‑spending) and user profiling (to capture psychological susceptibility to digital cues). The review also highlights the financial literacy gap, reinforcing Odin’s need for intelligent, non‑judgmental budgeting feedback.

**Directly justifies:**

- “E‑wallets reduce the ‘pain of paying,’ leading to more frequent and less deliberate purchases in young users.”
- “Cashback, flash sales, and push notifications act as behavioral triggers that condition automatic spending habits.”
- “Without built‑in budgeting tools, digital payment users often experience post‑spending regret and difficulty saving.”
- “Financial literacy moderates digital spending behavior; its absence makes Gen Z vulnerable to overspending.”

**Limits of relevance:**

- All studies are correlational (no causal inference) and not specific to the Philippines.
- Focuses on general Gen Z, not Filipino young professionals with local cultural obligations (e.g., remittances, family support).
- Does not evaluate any specific anomaly detection or profiling algorithm; only establishes the behavioral phenomenon.

## Limitations

- Sample of 28 studies may miss unpublished negative results (publication bias).
- No longitudinal data — cannot determine if impulsive spending persists or stabilizes over time.
- Only English‑language, peer‑reviewed journals; excludes regional or grey literature.
- Self‑reported spending data in original studies may suffer from recall bias.
