## TL;DR

Indonesian housewives develop sophisticated community-based financial systems (arisan, jimpitan) despite very low formal literacy rates.

## Problem and Motivation

Housewives in lower-income Indonesian communities manage daily finances but have far lower formal literacy (36%) than men (40.6%). Existing research measures literacy deficits, not the adaptive strategies women actually use. This study captures those hidden practices to inform more effective financial inclusion efforts.

## Approach

- Qualitative case study in Salatiga, Central Java.
- 25 housewives selected purposively for diverse education and income levels.
- In-depth interviews (60–90 min each) plus non-participant observation and document analysis.
- Data analyzed using ATLAS.ti with open, axial, and selective coding.

## Findings

1. **5 of 8** housewives keep simple written records; the rest use mental accounting and physical envelopes.
2. **Community systems** (ROSCAs, jimpitan, collective buying) function as informal banks, savings mechanisms, and social insurance.
3. Women diversify income (e.g., selling cakes) to gain financial autonomy without leaving home.
4. Emergency funds are **micro-scale** (Rp50,000–200,000) but universally present.
5. Financial communication with husbands ranges from collaborative to minimal; women use transparency and personal funds to resolve conflicts.

## Definitions

| Term / Acronym | Plain-English Definition |
| -------------- | ------------------------ |
| ROSCA | Rotating Savings and Credit Association (arisan) — members contribute regularly and take turns receiving the lump sum. |
| Jimpitan | Daily collection of a small amount of rice or money for community social funds. |
| Bounded power | Women exercise agency within strict domains while constrained by patriarchal structures. |
| Mental accounting | Categorizing money by purpose even when physically stored together. |
| OJK | Financial Services Authority of Indonesia. |

**Contribution to Odin:**

This paper provides a qualitative case study of financial management in resource-constrained Indonesian households, offering insights into informal community-based mechanisms (rotating savings, collective purchasing, micro-emergency funds) that could inform design of culturally adapted personal finance systems in Southeast Asian contexts, including the Philippines. It challenges the assumption that low formal literacy implies low financial capability, supporting a strengths-based approach to user research.

**Directly justifies:**

**Limits of relevance:**

- Geographic focus on Indonesia (Salatiga), not the Philippines, though cultural and economic parallels exist.
- Demographic is married housewives, not Filipino young professionals.
- Entirely qualitative; no quantitative or algorithmic findings for forecasting, anomaly detection, or classification.

## Limitations

- Small sample (n=25) from a single city limits generalizability.
- Relies on self-reported financial behavior; no objective transaction data.
- Cross-sectional design captures practices at one point in time only.
- No comparison group (e.g., men or formally employed women).

## Remember This

- 🔑 **Community systems (arisan, jimpitan)** serve as informal banks, insurance, and forced savings.
- 💡 **5 of 8** housewives keep written records — but using simple books, not digital tools.
- 📌 Low formal literacy (36% of women) does **not** equal low financial capability.
