| 1 | **Unsupervised Anomaly Detection: Isolation Forest** | Bhavana et al. (2025); Shuryhin & Zinovatna (2024); D’Souza et al. (2026); Begum (2025); Bader & Haraty (2025) | Bhavana: “Anomaly detection accuracy = 95% using Isolation Forest and Autoencoders”; Shuryhin: “Isolation Forest detects financial anomalies via path‑length scoring”; D’Souza: “Isolation Forest outperforms density‑based and boundary‑based detectors”; Begum: “Isolation Forest precision only 0.65 – works as supplement”; Bader: “Anomaly detection achieved precision 90% using Isolation Forest and One‑Class SVM” | Isolation Forest achieves high accuracy (95%) in supervised settings but lower precision (0.65) on heavily imbalanced fraud data. It excels as an unsupervised, scalable method for cold‑start anomaly detection without labeled data. | Isolation Forest is a leading candidate for PFMS anomaly detection due to O(n log n) complexity and lack of labeling requirements. However, performance degrades on extreme class imbalance; best used in hybrid pipelines. | No standardized evaluation across different spending cultures (e.g., Philippines). Few studies report false positive rates for legitimate spending anomalies (vs. fraud). Cold‑start thresholds not systematically tuned. | Odin can use Isolation Forest as the primary unsupervised anomaly detector for user‑entered transactions, combined with user feedback loops to reduce false positives. | 2 | **Deep Learning for Anomaly Detection: Autoencoders & GANs** | Bhavana et al. (2025); Machireddy (2023); Brophy et al. (2023); Gouni (2025); Ahmed et al. (2023) | Bhavana: “Autoencoders for anomaly detection (95% accuracy)”; Machireddy: “Unsupervised autoencoders detect spending anomalies without labeled data”; Brophy: “MAD‑GAN detects anomalies in multivariate time series”; Gouni: “Autoencoders detect anomalies via reconstruction error”; Ahmed: “Generative models detect anomalies without labels” | Autoencoders and GANs (e.g., MAD‑GAN, TimeGAN) effectively detect anomalies in unlabeled time‑series data by learning normal patterns and flagging high reconstruction error or discriminative scores. | Generative unsupervised methods are well‑suited for spending anomaly detection when labeled anomalies are unavailable (cold‑start). TimeGAN preserves temporal dynamics better than static autoencoders. | GAN training is computationally heavy and unstable for mobile deployment. No standard evaluation metric for time‑series GANs. Differential privacy trade‑offs not quantified for personal finance. | Odin can explore lightweight autoencoders for on‑device anomaly detection. GANs may be used server‑side for generating synthetic transaction data to train cold‑start profiles while preserving privacy. |
| 3 | **Sequential & Recurrent Models (LSTM, GRU) for Anomaly Detection** | Polytarchos (2025); Begum (2025); Gouni (2025); Singh (2025); Zlobin & Bazylevych (2025) | Polytarchos: “LSTM achieved 92% accuracy for total funds range”; Begum: “GRU recall 0.89 for sequence‑based fraud detection”; Gouni: “LSTM addresses vanishing gradient for long transaction histories”; Singh: “LSTM 93.6% accuracy for sequential behavior”; Zlobin: “LSTM 93% stock prediction, GRU‑CA reduces anomaly RMSE by 27%” | LSTM and GRU models capture temporal dependencies in transaction sequences, achieving high accuracy for anomaly detection and behavioral profiling (92–93.6%). Hybrid GRU‑CA with attention further reduces error. | Recurrent models outperform static classifiers when spending patterns evolve over time. They require sufficient historical data (not ideal for cold‑start) but adapt well to concept drift. | No evaluation on sparse, manual‑entry transaction data (typical of Odin). Most benchmarks use high‑frequency banking data. Mobile computational cost of LSTM/GRU not reported. | Odin can employ a lightweight GRU or LSTM after a user accumulates ~30 days of transaction history. For real‑time anomaly scoring, a smaller MLP or Isolation Forest may be used initially. |
| 4 | **Real‑Time and Streaming Anomaly Detection** | Polytarchos (2025); Liao (2026); Xiang et al. (2023) | Polytarchos: “Real‑time clustering (BEReTiC) achieved 0.003% false positive rate (lower than deep learning) but lower accuracy (66% vs 92%)”; Liao: “Closed‑loop perception‑decision‑execution for autonomous adaptation”; Xiang: “Parameter updates handle abrupt drift faster than structure updates” | Real‑time unsupervised clustering adapts instantly to new patterns without retraining and produces fewer false positives (0.003%) than deep learning, but classification accuracy is lower (66% vs 92%). Drift detection enables model updates without full retraining. | There is a fundamental accuracy–latency trade‑off: streaming methods are faster and adapt better to concept drift but may underperform batch‑trained deep learning on stable patterns. | No comparison of real‑time anomaly detection on personal spending streams (only credit card fraud). Cold‑start performance of clustering methods is poor. Mobile resource usage not benchmarked. | Odin’s manual input is sparse, making real‑time clustering feasible. A hybrid approach: use streaming clustering for immediate anomaly alerts, then periodically retrain a deeper model for improved accuracy. |
| 5 | **Evaluation Metrics and Performance Benchmarks** | Bhavana et al. (2025); Bader & Haraty (2025); Begum (2025); Polytarchos (2025); Zlobin & Bazylevych (2025); Mienye et al. (2026) | Bhavana: “95% accuracy, 90% precision, 0.93 AUC”; Begum: “F1 0.89 for stacking ensemble, Isolation Forest precision 0.65”; Polytarchos: “0.003% misclassification rate (clustering) vs 0.007% (deep learning)”; Zlobin: “Random forest 99.6% accuracy, 100% precision for fraud”; Mienye: “Temporal leakage common – need out‑of‑time splits” | Commonly reported metrics: accuracy (66–99.6%), precision (65–100%), recall (85–89%), F1 (87.5–99.6%), AUC (0.93), false positive rate (0.003–0.007%). However, calibration (Brier, ECE) and temporal validation are often neglected. | High accuracy alone is misleading for imbalanced anomaly detection. Precision and F1 are more informative. Many studies suffer from temporal leakage (random cross‑validation), overestimating real‑world performance. | No standard benchmark dataset for personal finance anomaly detection across different cultures. Few studies report false positive rates per user or over time, which are critical for user trust. | Odin’s evaluation plan must include temporal validation (rolling origin), calibration metrics, and user‑level false positive tracking. Target: precision >0.85, recall >0.80 on Filipino spending data. |
| 6 | **Cold‑Start and Unlabeled Data Challenges** | Bhavana et al. (2025); Shuryhin & Zinovatna (2024); Machireddy (2023); Harris & Austin (2024); D’Souza et al. (2026) | Bhavana: “No cold‑start evaluation”; Shuryhin: “Isolation Forest unsupervised, applicable to cold‑start”; Machireddy: “Autoencoders without labeled training data”; Harris & Austin: “Unsupervised learning for anomaly detection without labels”; D’Souza: “Isolation Forest for unlabeled personal finance data” | Most anomaly detection methods assume some transaction history. Unsupervised methods (Isolation Forest, autoencoders, clustering) are suited for cold‑start users, but their accuracy with <30 days of sparse manual entries is not empirically validated. | Cold‑start remains a major research gap. While unsupervised algorithms theoretically work with little data, reported benchmarks use large labeled datasets (e.g., European credit card fraud). Real‑world performance on new users is unknown. | No study evaluates anomaly detection on manual‑entry personal finance logs with <30 transactions. The effect of user‑entered category errors on detection accuracy is unmeasured. | Odin must design a cold‑start fallback: rule‑based thresholds for the first 2 weeks, then switch to unsupervised ML. User feedback (marking false positives) will bootstrap a personalized model. |
| 7 | **Concept Drift and Adaptive Anomaly Detection** | Liao (2026); Xiang et al. (2023); Gouni (2025); Kim et al. (2025) | Liao: “Concept drift detection + incremental learning for adaptation”; Xiang: “Parameter updates handle abrupt drift; recurring drift under‑addressed”; Gouni: “Static anomaly detectors increase false positives over time”; Kim: “RevIN normalizes distribution shifts” | User spending behavior drifts due to life events (job change, holidays) and seasonal patterns. Adaptive methods (incremental learning, drift detection, reversible normalization) maintain anomaly detection accuracy over time. | Concept drift is inevitable in personal finance. Active drift detection (ADWIN, CUSUM) combined with parameter‑level updates offers a good trade‑off between stability and plasticity. Recurring drift (e.g., December spending) is often ignored. | No longitudinal study of concept drift in PFMS anomaly detection. Most drift research uses network intrusion or electricity data, not personal spending. Optimal drift detection thresholds for financial data unknown. | Odin must implement concept drift monitoring (e.g., track reconstruction error or classification confidence over time). Use micro‑level drift detection per user to avoid global false alarms. |
| 8 | **Explainable Anomaly Detection (XAI)** | Alammar et al. (2026); Bader & Haraty (2025); Bhavana et al. (2025); D’Souza et al. (2026) | Alammar: “Hybrid SHAP–LIME reduces fairness gap by 40%”; Bader: “Real‑time alerts with user feedback loops reduce false positives”; Bhavana: “SHAP and LIME translate anomaly scores to natural language”; D’Souza: “Explainable AI critical for user trust” | Explainability methods (SHAP, LIME) can justify anomaly scores (e.g., “grocery spending 38% higher due to festival”), improving user trust and financial literacy (78% of participants). Feedback loops further refine detection. | Users need to understand why a transaction is flagged to accept or reject the alert. Natural language explanations derived from feature attribution increase adoption and reduce annoyance from false positives. | XAI for anomaly detection in PFMS is rarely evaluated with real users. Most studies measure algorithmic fidelity, not human trust or behavioral change. Long‑term effect of explanations on financial habits unknown. | Odin must provide user‑friendly explanations for each anomaly alert (e.g., “This dining expense is 2x your 30‑day average”). Use SHAP values from Isolation Forest or LIME for local explanations. |
| 9 | **Privacy and Security in Anomaly Detection** | Brophy et al. (2023); Zlobin & Bazylevych (2025); Shuryhin & Zinovatna (2024); Whitaker (2025); Parameswaran & Saad (2025) | Brophy: “Differentially private GANs resist membership inference”; Zlobin: “Federated learning preserves data privacy”; Shuryhin: “OAuth 2.0, OWASP Top 10 compliance”; Whitaker: “Privacy‑by‑design under GDPR”; Parameswaran & Saad: “Only 11.4% of users confident in data security” | Privacy‑preserving techniques (differential privacy, federated learning, synthetic data generation) can protect user transaction data while still enabling anomaly detection. However, user trust in financial apps is generally low (only 11.4% feel secure). | Anomaly detection must not compromise user privacy. Local processing (on‑device) and federated learning align with regulations (RA 10173). Users are sensitive to data breaches; transparency about data handling is essential. | No benchmark of differentially private anomaly detection on personal spending data. Trade‑off between privacy budget and detection accuracy not quantified for PFMS. Mobile implementation of federated learning not evaluated. | Odin should perform anomaly detection locally on the device whenever possible. For cloud‑based components, use differential privacy or synthetic data. Clearly communicate privacy controls to build trust. |
| 10 | **Limitations and Gaps in Existing Anomaly Detection Systems** | Multiple sources (synthesized) | Bhavana: “No cold‑start evaluation, assumes banking API”; Begum: “Isolation Forest precision 0.65 only”; Polytarchos: “Real‑time clustering accuracy much lower than deep learning”; Brophy: “No standardized evaluation metrics for time‑series GANs”; Mienye: “Temporal leakage common”; D’Souza: “No unified PFMS framework” | Major gaps: (1) Lack of evaluation on cold‑start, manual‑entry data; (2) Overreliance on banking APIs not available in many contexts; (3) Temporal leakage inflates reported performance; (4) No standard benchmarks for personal finance anomaly detection; (5) User trust and explainability rarely validated with human subjects. | Despite high reported accuracy (e.g., 95%+), real‑world deployment of PFMS anomaly detection faces significant challenges: sparse data, concept drift, false positive annoyance, and privacy concerns. No study evaluates anomaly detection for manual‑entry PFMS with low transaction frequency. | Odin’s contribution will be to directly address these gaps: design and evaluate a mobile‑first, manual‑entry anomaly detection system for Filipino young professionals, with transparent cold‑start handling, user feedback, and culturally appropriate thresholds. |
