## FINAL SYNTHESIS (Based on All 35 Sources)

The 35 local studies collectively paint a consistent picture of Filipino financial behavior that directly informs the design of a personal finance management system (PFMS) like Odin.

**1. Robust knowledge‑behavior gap.** Across students, teachers, and professionals, high self‑reported financial literacy does not reliably translate into positive financial actions (saving, budgeting, debt management). Financial literacy explains only 51‑62% of behavior variance at best; present bias often overrides knowledge. **Implication:** Odin must prioritise behavioural interventions (automated alerts, commitment devices, transaction logging) over pure educational content.

**2. Demographics and income structure matter more than literacy.** Monthly expenses, income level, occupation, and marital status consistently predict financial planning and spending – often more strongly than literacy scores. Lower‑income individuals sometimes exhibit better budgeting discipline. **Implication:** Odin’s recommendation algorithms should be driven by income stability and expense load, not assumed knowledge; age, sex, and civil status are essential profiling features.

**3. Culturally specific practices are non‑negotiable.** *Utang na loob* (family support), *paluwagan* (rotating savings), *bayanihan* (community reciprocity), and *petsa de peligro* (pre‑payday scarcity) are embedded, recurring financial behaviours. Family obligations are treated as protected expenses, often prioritised over personal savings. **Implication:** Odin must include culturally grounded expense categories (e.g., “family support”, “paluwagan contribution”) and anomaly detection that distinguishes ritualised spending from overspending.

**4. Income volatility and weak emergency funds are structural vulnerabilities.** Platform workers (8.2% of the workforce, 84.5% as sole job) face significantly lower access to employer benefits; emergency fund practice is consistently low across retirees, teachers, and staff. Even stable government employees report delayed salaries and automatic loan deductions that disrupt cash flow. **Implication:** Dynamic, adaptive budget recommendations are required for variable‑income users; structured nudges for emergency fund building should be a core feature.

**5. Digital tools have dual effects.** Digital wallets and online banking can improve financial discipline (explaining up to 45% of behaviour variance), but social media and BNPL services also drive impulse spending (r=0.612 correlation with poor budgeting). Trust and security perceptions are the strongest correlates of good financial decisions (rs up to 0.823). **Implication:** Odin must balance convenience with spending controls; privacy‑by‑design and transparent security features are critical for engagement.

**6. Research gaps directly limit existing knowledge.** Most studies are cross‑sectional, self‑reported, and focused on students or specific professions – not the 20‑40 young professional demographic. No study evaluates an ML‑based PFMS using objective transaction data. **Implication:** Odin’s development fills a clear empirical gap; the system should be designed to collect longitudinal, transaction‑level data for continuous validation.

In summary, the local literature validates Odin’s core design decisions: behaviour‑first intervention, culturally aware categories, dynamic handling of income volatility, and trust‑centred UX.
