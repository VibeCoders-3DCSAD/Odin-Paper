# Purpose and Description of the Study

## Description of the Study

Filipino young professionals demonstrate a documented gap between financial knowledge and financial action: despite recording the highest financial literacy scores among all age cohorts, young adults aged 18 to 39 are among the least likely to spend within their income (Cacnio & Romarate, 2024), financial literacy seminars produce no measurable change in budgeting behavior — attendees and non-attendees scoring identically at M = 2.69, p = .991 (Am-Una, 2026) — and budgeting itself remains the most cognitively demanding financial task Filipino workers consistently attempt (Am-Una, 2026). Existing personal finance tools fail to close this gap because they are designed for Western income structures and do not account for the culturally specific financial obligations — paluwagan contributions, family remittances, inter-payday liquidity constraints, and government contribution tracking — that materially shape how Filipino young professionals allocate income (Amado, 2026; Cabiles, 2025). In response, this study proposes the development of Odin, a web and mobile personal budget management system for Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila that employs Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks for per-category spending forecasting and the Isolation Forest algorithm for unsupervised behavioral anomaly detection. The core strategy classifies users into one of four Financial Behavioral Profiles — Stable-Obligated, Stable-Flexible, Variable-Obligated, and Variable-Flexible — defined by income stability type and obligation weight, ensuring all system guidance is calibrated to each user's actual financial structure rather than a generic population template. Key system features include an LSTM-driven spending forecast dashboard with profile-average cold-start fallback, Isolation Forest anomaly alerts drawn from each user's personal spending baseline, a rule-based budget recommendation engine operating on a locally validated seven-group expense category structure that explicitly accommodates paluwagan contributions, family support, government obligations (SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG), savings goal tracking with long-term projection, and dual-strategy debt management guidance using the Avalanche and Snowball methods.

## Purpose of the Study

The primary purpose of this study is to design, develop, and evaluate Odin — a web and mobile personal budget management system that applies an LSTM spending forecasting model and an Isolation Forest anomaly detection algorithm to deliver personalized, behavior-aware financial guidance to Filipino young professionals aged 20 to 40 in Metro Manila. This purpose is directly grounded in empirical evidence that financial literacy alone does not significantly affect financial planning behavior among Filipino millennials (Dimaunahan et al., 2025: β = 0.077, p = .153), while monthly expense composition (β = 0.205) and income level (β = 0.180) are the strongest predictors of planning behavior — evidence that effective financial tools must work with a user's actual spending reality rather than assume that knowledge transfer is sufficient. By training the LSTM model on per-user transaction sequences and deploying Isolation Forest as an unsupervised per-user anomaly detector, Odin aims to provide intelligent, data-driven financial guidance that scales progressively — from cold-start profile-average forecasts during onboarding to fully personalized predictions as transaction history accumulates. The desired outcome is a system that demonstrably satisfies ISO/IEC 25010:2023 quality standards, achieves a System Usability Scale score above 80 with Filipino young professional participants in Metro Manila, and meets defined LSTM performance targets (RMSE, MAE, R²) and Isolation Forest performance targets (Precision, Recall, F1-Score) on a synthetic Philippine spending dataset — collectively validating both its technical soundness and its fitness for the target demographic.

## Significance of the Study

This study holds significance at both the practical and academic levels. For the target demographic, Odin addresses a structural gap documented in national-level data: the BSP's 2021 Consumer Finance Survey (Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas, 2021) found that food at home alone accounts for 55.4% of Filipino household expenditure, only 35.3% of Filipino households own a deposit account, and 16.4% carry past-due bills — a consumption and debt landscape that generic, Western-designed budgeting tools are ill-equipped to reflect or guide. Albert et al. (2024) reported that 98% of urban middle-class households — the primary constituent of Odin's target demographic — own mobile phones, establishing the practical viability of mobile delivery, while the pandemic-driven contraction of the Philippine middle class from 43.5% in 2018 to 39.8% in 2021 underscores the urgency of accessible, locally grounded financial management tools for this group. For Filipino workers specifically, Cabiles (2025) documented that debt accumulation ranks as the single most severe financial challenge, with a measurable value-action gap between financial knowledge (mean 3.98) and actual financial behavior (mean 3.03) — a gap that Odin targets directly through automated, data-driven nudges rather than informational content. For the academic field, this study contributes a documented architecture and evaluation of LSTM-based spending forecasting and Isolation Forest-based anomaly detection within a unified personal budget management system grounded in Philippine institutional expenditure data and validated against ISO/IEC 25010:2023 and the System Usability Scale — an approach not yet represented in local financial technology research. The four Financial Behavioral Profiles and the locally validated expense category structure, developed in consultation with a subject-matter expert in business and financial sciences and explicitly incorporating culturally specific Filipino expenditure items, constitute research artifacts that future studies on intelligent personal finance systems for non-Western user contexts can build upon.
