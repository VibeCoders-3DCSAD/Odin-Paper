# Purpose and Description of the Study

## Description of the Study

Filipino young professionals continue to face persistent budgeting difficulties despite wider access to digital financial tools. National evidence shows that only 37% of Filipino adults save regularly, while 41% borrow money for regular spending needs rather than for emergencies (Cacnio & Romarate, 2024). The problem is also visible at the target-user level. The researchers conducted a survey of 45 respondents and found that only 6.7% used a dedicated budgeting app, while 51% identified insufficient income as their main budgeting problem and 27% reported difficulty tracking spending. These conditions suggest that many users do not merely need another expense tracker, but a more intelligent and context-sensitive budgeting system.

In response to these challenges, this research proposes the development of **Odin**, a web and mobile personal budget management system designed for Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila. The system will combine spending forecasting, anomaly detection, financial behavioral profiling, and rule-based budget guidance in a single platform to provide more adaptive financial support. This solution is grounded in evidence that financial planning among Filipino millennials is driven more by monthly expenses, income, occupation, and goal commitment than by financial literacy alone (Dimaunahan et al., 2025).

The core strategy of the study is to align the system’s functions with the actual financial realities of the target users, including variable income, debt obligations, savings goals, and culturally shaped spending responsibilities. Key system features include financial behavioral profile assignment, transaction recording and categorization, LSTM-based spending forecasts, Isolation Forest-based anomaly alerts, savings goal tracking, and debt management support. The design also accounts for culturally significant family-oriented expenses, since Filipino support for parents and relatives often affects real budgeting decisions (Casilan & Baclagan, 2024; Cervantes et al., 2025).

## Purpose of the Study

The primary purpose of this study is to design, develop, and evaluate Odin as a personal budget management system for Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila. Specifically, the study aims to produce a system that can forecast spending patterns, detect unusual transactions, classify users into financial behavioral profiles, and generate practical budgeting guidance. This purpose directly addresses the gap between existing budgeting applications and the lived financial conditions of the intended users.

This purpose is aligned with findings that young Filipinos may possess some financial knowledge yet still struggle to spend within income and sustain long-term financial planning behavior (Cacnio & Romarate, 2024). It is also supported by evidence that financial literacy alone does not significantly predict financial planning among Filipino millennials, while monthly expenses and goal-related factors have stronger influence (Dimaunahan et al., 2025).

By achieving this purpose, the study seeks to provide Filipino young professionals with a functional and usable system that offers more relevant financial support than conventional budgeting tools. The intended outcome is a decision-support application that improves spending awareness, strengthens financial monitoring, and promotes more realistic budgeting behavior based on local financial conditions.

## Significance of the Study

This study is significant because it addresses a real and measurable financial management problem among Filipino young professionals. The researchers' survey found that 60% of respondents described their monthly obligations as moderate or heavy, while 38% identified saving for a major goal and 31% prioritized building an emergency fund. These figures show the need for a budgeting system that can help users manage present obligations while still planning for future goals.

In practical terms, Odin may help users monitor expenses, identify unusual spending behavior, manage debt pressure, and maintain savings discipline through adaptive and profile-aware guidance. The study is also significant for financial technology research because it integrates forecasting, anomaly detection, and behavioral profiling within one localized system architecture, reflecting evidence that budgeting behavior is shaped by spending pressure, online buying tendencies, and demographic differences (Cervantes et al., 2025; Dimaunahan et al., 2025).

At the social and academic levels, the study is significant because it acknowledges that Filipino financial behavior is shaped by family support expectations and relational obligations that are often missing from generic budgeting tools (Casilan & Baclagan, 2024). The study therefore contributes a localized design and evaluation framework for intelligent budget management systems and may serve as a reference for future research on behavioral profiling, predictive budgeting, anomaly detection, and context-sensitive financial applications.
