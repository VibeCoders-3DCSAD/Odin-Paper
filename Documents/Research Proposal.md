| **B. TECHNICAL DESCRIPTION** |
| **Project Context** The advancement of financial technology has fundamentally changed how personal finance is managed. Mobile and web-based applications have become primary tools for budgeting, expense tracking, and financial planning, while machine learning has introduced a new class of capability: systems that move beyond passive transaction recording toward predictive, behavior-aware financial guidance (Talasila, 2024; Bhatele et al., 2023). Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, a class of recurrent neural networks, have demonstrated strong performance in modeling financial time series data, enabling systems to forecast future expenditures based on historical behavior (Mroua & Lamine, 2023; Yang, 2025). Isolation Forest, an unsupervised anomaly detection algorithm, has proven effective at identifying unusual financial activity without requiring labeled training examples (Xu et al., 2023; Xiang et al., 2023; Kumari & Kumar, 2024). Systems that combine machine learning forecasting with automated expense management have demonstrated measurable improvements in users' financial oversight and budgeting adherence (Aishwarya & Hemalatha, 2023; Jadhav & Patil, 2025; de Zarzà et al., 2024). Together, these techniques represent a meaningful shift in what personal finance systems can offer: not merely a ledger of past spending, but an intelligent basis for more informed financial decision-making. Despite this global progress, the application of intelligent personal finance tools to the specific context of the Filipino young professional remains largely unaddressed in both research and practice. Personal financial management literature consistently identifies behavioral, attitudinal, and contextual factors as the primary determinants of financial outcomes, factors shaped by local culture, income structures, and obligation patterns (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2023; Cacnio & Romarate, 2024). Filipino young professionals aged 20 to 40 occupy a financially critical life stage: they are entering or already navigating the workforce, forming households, and beginning to carry significant financial obligations, yet doing so in a context shaped by income diversity, ranging from stable salaried employment to variable freelance or commission-based earnings, and by cultural financial norms distinct from those of Western markets for which most existing tools were designed (Desello & Agner, 2023; Dimaunahan et al., 2025). Digital financial services adoption in the Philippines has accelerated sharply, with the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) reporting that 57.4% of total retail transactions were conducted digitally in 2024, exceeding the government's development plan target. However, structural gaps in financial management behavior persist (Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas, 2024a; Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas, 2023). Studies on Filipino young adults' financial behavior document a persistent gap between financial knowledge and financial action, with spending patterns heavily influenced by social and cultural norms that most existing tools do not account for (Lim et al., 2024; Rodriguez et al., 2024). A systematic review of budgeting applications further confirms that existing tools are predominantly designed for Western income and expense structures, with limited accommodation of the culturally specific financial obligations prevalent in non-Western contexts (Alenazi & Sas, 2024). No existing locally developed system has addressed this demographic through machine learning-driven, behavior-aware financial guidance grounded in their specific income structures, obligation patterns, and cultural financial practices. This study proposes Odin, a personal budget management system for Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila that applies LSTM-based spending forecasting and Isolation Forest-based anomaly detection to provide personalized, adaptive budget guidance. The research problem is not merely the absence of a local budgeting tool, but the absence of a tool that treats users as belonging to distinct financial behavioral profiles, defined by the nature of their income stream and the weight of their financial obligations, and delivers guidance that reflects those differences. A pilot survey conducted among the target demographic (N=45) revealed that only 6.7% of respondents use a dedicated budgeting app, while the majority rely on no formal tool or simple spreadsheets. The most commonly cited challenges were “income is not enough to cover expenses” (51%), “difficult to track spending” (27%), and “lack of spending discipline” (27%). Additionally, 60% of respondents described their monthly obligations as “heavy” or “moderate,” with rent, credit card payments, and support for dependents being the most prevalent fixed expenses. These findings underscore that the target population not only struggles with basic budgeting but also lacks access to intelligent tools tailored to their financial realities. Existing intelligent personal finance systems demonstrate the technical feasibility of ML-driven budgeting and anomaly detection (Aishwarya & Hemalatha, 2023; Jadhav & Patil, 2025; Talasila, 2024), and budget optimization research confirms that personalized, ML-augmented financial planning improves user outcomes (de Zarzà et al., 2024). The BSP has published financial literacy guidance identifying five personal finance competency areas: planning and budgeting, saving, managing debt, investing, and protecting one's finances (Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas, 2024b). However, no locally developed system operationalizes these competency areas as the basis for an intelligent, machine learning-driven budget management application tailored to the Filipino context. The need for such a system is substantiated by national financial data and academic research. The BSP's 2018 Consumer Finance Survey, as analyzed in Cacnio and Romarate (2024), found that only 2% of Filipino adults correctly answered all six financial literacy assessment items, and only 7% had ever attended a financial literacy session. Among the consequences of this gap, 41% of Filipinos borrow money not for emergencies but to meet regular spending needs, a direct indicator of failed baseline budgeting behavior. Only 37% of Filipino adults save regularly, and of those who do, 51% keep their savings at home. Income and expenditure data from the Philippine Statistics Authority confirm that financial allocation patterns among Filipino households reflect this literacy gap, with limited formal savings behavior and high reliance on informal financial mechanisms (Philippine Statistics Authority, 2022; Philippine Statistics Authority, 2024). Studies on Filipino financial behavior document persistent gaps in financial literacy even among university-educated working adults in Metro Manila (Pascual & Santos-Recto, 2024; Eloriaga et al., 2022, as cited in Mireku & Agana, 2023). The pilot survey further corroborated these findings: among respondents, 38% reported “saving for a major goal” as their primary financial objective, while 31% prioritized “building an emergency fund”, goals that require consistent budgeting and disciplined tracking, which the majority of participants currently lack. These figures are consistent with findings from studies on Filipino young professionals specifically, which identify pervasive financial literacy challenges and low levels of financial preparedness among the working population, with young adults aged 18 to 39 being significantly less likely than older cohorts to engage in long-term financial planning behaviors such as maintaining retirement or pension plans (Cacnio & Romarate, 2024). The persistence of poor financial management behavior among Filipino young professionals can be attributed to two reinforcing factors. The first is behavioral and attitudinal: research on Filipino financial behavior documents a widespread tendency to prioritize immediate needs over long-term planning, and a reliance on informal saving methods that hinder consistent budgeting, savings accumulation, and investment behavior (Flores, 2025). Studies confirm that even Filipinos who possess basic financial knowledge frequently fail to translate that knowledge into structured financial behavior, with financial behavior itself serving as the mediating variable between literacy and outcomes (Rodriguez et al., 2024; Lim et al., 2024). The second is structural and cultural: the financial obligations of Filipino young professionals extend beyond the categories captured by most personal finance tools, including statutory government contributions such as SSS and Pag-IBIG, culturally embedded community obligations such as church and *barangay* collections, and informal financial arrangements such as *paluwagan* participation, obligations that are deeply rooted in Filipino social and cultural life and that materially affect how this demographic allocates their income (Flores, 2025). The pilot survey confirmed the prevalence of these culturally specific obligations: nearly one-third of respondents reported having dependents, while credit card payments, personal loans, and government contributions were frequently listed among fixed monthly obligations. These results validate the expense category structure used in Odin, which was developed in consultation with Dr. Pamela A. Go of the College of Business and Financial Sciences, University of Makati, and explicitly includes categories for family support, *paluwagan*, church donations, and *ambag*. Odin addresses this gap directly by combining machine learning-driven forecasting (Mroua & Lamine, 2023; Xu et al., 2023\) with a financial behavioral profiling system and an expense category structure grounded in the actual financial realities of the target demographic, evaluated against ISO/IEC 25010:2023 quality standards (International Organization for Standardization, 2023\) and the System Usability Scale (Bangor et al., 2008). |
| **Objective of the Study General Objective** The general objective of this study is to develop a web and mobile personal budget management system for Filipino young professionals that utilizes a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model for spending forecasting and an Isolation Forest algorithm for anomaly detection. **Specific Objectives** Analyze and document the personal budgeting behavior, income patterns, and financial obligation structures of Filipino young professionals in Metro Manila aged 20 to 40, via a pilot survey, as the basis for defining the system's four financial behavioral profiles: Stable-Obligated, Stable-Flexible, Variable-Obligated, and Variable-Flexible. Explore existing systems or applications of spending forecasting and anomaly detection algorithms that are relevant to the development of a personal budget management system. Analyze, understand, and perform preprocessing activities on the data gathered towards the development of spending forecasting and anomaly detection algorithms.  Train and evaluate the LSTM spending forecasting model and Isolation Forest anomaly detection algorithm using the following performance metrics: Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), measuring the magnitude of LSTM forecasting error relative to actual spending values on a held-out synthetic Philippine spending dataset; Mean Absolute Error (MAE), measuring the average absolute deviation between LSTM-predicted and actual spending values; R² (Coefficient of Determination), measuring the proportion of variance in actual spending values explained by the LSTM model; Precision, measuring the proportion of transactions flagged by Isolation Forest as anomalous that are correctly identified outliers on an outlier-injected synthetic transaction dataset; Recall, measuring the proportion of true anomalous transactions that the Isolation Forest model successfully detected; F1-Score, measuring the harmonic mean of Precision and Recall as the composite anomaly detection performance indicator. Design and develop a web and mobile application with the following key features: Financial behavioral profile assignment during onboarding, classifying users into one of four profiles (Stable-Obligated, Stable-Flexible, Variable-Obligated, Variable-Flexible) based on onboarding information to personalize budget guidance; Manual transaction entry with predefined expense category selection drawn from a locally validated category structure, as well as recurring transaction templates for auto-logging; LSTM-driven spending forecast dashboard, presenting predicted expenditures per category for the upcoming week and month, with cold-start fallback forecasting, displaying a profile-average baseline forecast derived from pre-trained model priors when a user's personal transaction history is insufficient for personalized prediction, with a visible indicator that the forecast is not yet personalized;  Rule-based budget recommendations generated from the LSTM forecast and the user's financial behavioral profile, aligned with BSP personal finance competency guidance, with protected categories that the system will not suggest reducing; Isolation Forest-powered anomaly alerts, notifying users of transactions that deviate significantly from their established spending patterns; Savings goal tracking, allowing users to define a savings target and monitor cumulative progress based on their actual transaction data, with long-term goal projection, providing a deterministic estimate of when a user will reach their savings goal at their current savings rate; Debt management guidance presenting both the Avalanche and Snowball repayment strategies, with the user selecting their preferred approach; Responsive design supporting access on both mobile and web platforms. Test the functional and non-functional requirements of the system, including: Functional requirements validated through defined test cases covering the following system modules: Financial behavioral profile assignment and re-onboarding module Transaction entry, recurring transaction management, and expense category management module LSTM spending forecast dashboard and cold-start fallback module Rule-based budget recommendation engine module Isolation Forest anomaly alert module Savings goal tracking and long-term projection module Debt management guidance module Non-functional requirements covering performance efficiency, including response time for forecast generation under simulated load conditions. Evaluate the system using the ISO 25010 quality standard, focusing on: Functional suitability — the degree to which the system's features meet the stated objectives; Usability — assessed through the System Usability Scale (SUS) administered to Filipino young professional participants in Metro Manila, with a target score above 80, consistent with established SUS benchmarks for information system usability (Suria, 2024; Bangor et al., 2008); Performance efficiency — response time and resource utilization under realistic usage conditions. Deploy the system and conduct a pilot evaluation with Filipino young professional participants in Metro Manila, measuring LSTM forecasting accuracy on real user data and gathering usability and behavioral feedback.  |
| **Scope and Limitations** The primary focus of this research is the utilization of the LSTM model and the Isolation Forest algorithm for the design and development of Odin, a personal budget management system. LSTM serves as the forecasting backbone of the system, trained on transaction data to predict future spending per expense category over weekly and monthly horizons. Isolation Forest operates as the anomaly detection engine, identifying transactions that deviate from a user's established spending baseline in an unsupervised manner. Both techniques are applied within a unified system architecture and evaluated against defined performance targets derived from their respective benchmarking analyses. The system features incorporated in Odin are designed to address the five personal finance competency areas identified by the BSP: planning and budgeting, saving, managing debt, investing, and protecting one's finances. These features include: financial behavioral profile assignment and manual re-onboarding, manual transaction entry with category selection, recurring transaction management, LSTM-driven spending forecast dashboard with cold-start fallback, rule-based budget recommendations, Isolation Forest anomaly alerts, savings goal tracking, long-term goal projection, debt management guidance, and profile override neutral mode. The expense category structure used in Odin has been validated through expert consultation with Dr. Pamela A. Go of the College of Business and Financial Sciences, University of Makati, serving as subject matter expert, and is organized into seven groups reflecting the financial realities of Filipino young professionals: Essential Expenses (food and groceries, utilities, housing, transportation, healthcare and medicine); Government Obligations (SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG contributions, income tax for self-employed users); Debt and Loan Repayments (personal loan, housing loan, car loan, credit card payment, informal debt); Insurance (life insurance and non-life insurance premiums); Family and Community Contributions (family support and remittances, *paluwagan* contributions, church and religious donations, *barangay* and community collections, *ambag*); Discretionary Expenses (dining out and delivery, entertainment and leisure, shopping and personal care, education and self-development, travel and recreation); and Financial Allocation (emergency fund contribution, savings deposit, investment contribution). By default, the Emergency Fund, all Debt and Loan Repayments, Insurance, and the entire Essential Expenses group are treated as protected categories that the system's budget recommendation engine will not suggest reducing.  The geographical scope of this research is Metro Manila, Philippines. Target users are Filipino young professionals between the ages of 20 and 40 residing or working within Metro Manila. Data collection, pilot study participation, and user evaluation activities will be conducted within this geographical boundary. The study encompasses data collected and system development activities conducted during the period from the second semester of academic year 2025–2026 through the end of the second semester of academic year 2026–2027. The study employs the following hardware for the design and development of Odin: Development machines — laptop or desktop computers used by the research team for system development, model training, and documentation; Mobile test devices — Android smartphones used for testing the React Native mobile application under real device conditions. The study employs the following software for the design and development of Odin: React (TypeScript), for the development of the web application frontend; React Native via Expo, for the development of the cross-platform mobile application; Node.js with Express, as the primary backend API framework handling user authentication, business logic, and data storage operations; Python with FastAPI, as a dedicated machine learning service layer exposing the LSTM and Isolation Forest models as internal API endpoints consumed by the Node.js backend; TensorFlow / Keras, for the development and training of the LSTM spending forecasting model; Scikit-learn, for the implementation and training of the Isolation Forest anomaly detection model; PostgreSQL, as the relational database for user and transaction data storage. **Limitations** Despite the study's objectives, there are certain limitations that should be acknowledged: Algorithm Dependency. The forecasting and anomaly detection capabilities of Odin are directly contingent on the performance of the LSTM model and Isolation Forest algorithm. LSTM requires a sufficient volume of historical transaction data before forecasts become reliable; users in the early stages of system use will receive less accurate forecasts until adequate transaction history accumulates. Isolation Forest similarly requires an established transaction baseline per user before anomaly detection becomes effective. Any inherent limitations of these algorithms directly constrain the system's intelligence. Manual Entry Dependency. Odin relies entirely on manual transaction entry by the user. The accuracy of forecasts, anomaly detection, and budget guidance is therefore dependent on the completeness and correctness of user-entered data. The system does not integrate with banks, e-wallets, or any third-party financial data providers. Generalizability. The findings of this study are specific to Filipino young professionals aged 20 to 40 in Metro Manila during the defined temporal scope. The system's financial behavioral profiles, expense categories, and advisory logic may not generalize without modification to other demographics, regions, or financial contexts. Pilot Scope. The pilot evaluation is limited to a small sample of participants in Metro Manila within the thesis timeline. Longitudinal assessment of whether Odin produces measurable improvement in users' actual financial outcomes over time is beyond the scope of this study. Advisory Scope. Odin provides informational budget guidance informed by BSP personal finance competency guidance. It does not constitute licensed financial advice and does not include investment advisory services, retirement planning, or integration with government financial programs beyond tracking user contributions to SSS, Pag-IBIG, and PhilHealth as expense categories. External Factors. The performance and accessibility of Odin are subject to external conditions beyond the control of the research team. These include network connectivity availability on users' devices, compatibility constraints across Android device models and browser environments, and broader economic or policy changes, such as adjustments to government contribution rates for SSS, PhilHealth, or Pag-IBIG, that may affect the accuracy or relevance of the system's expense categories and advisory logic over time. |
