This is **Table 7** of the PSA FIES series, and it completes the picture by showing **where Filipino families spend their money** – the percentage share of total family expenditure on different goods and services. Like Table 6 (income sources), this table provides a detailed breakdown of spending patterns for the Philippines, each region, province, and highly urbanized city (HUC) for 2018, 2021, and 2023p.

### What the Table Shows

For each geographic area, you get:
- **2018, 2021, 2023p** – share (%) of total family expenditure from 19 spending categories.
- **Percentage point changes** (2021 minus 2018) and (2023 minus 2021).

The expenditure categories include:
- **Food and Non‑Alcoholic Beverages** (split into food consumed at home, outside home, and processing services)
- **Alcoholic Beverages and Tobacco**
- **Clothing and Footwear**
- **Housing, Water, Electricity, Gas and Other Fuels** (often the largest non‑food item)
- **Furnishings and Routine Household Maintenance**
- **Health**
- **Transport**
- **Information and Communication**
- **Recreation, Sport and Culture**
- **Education**
- **Hotels (Accommodation Services)**
- **Insurance and Financial Services**
- **Personal Care, Social Protection and Miscellaneous**
- **Durable Furniture and Equipment** (separate from routine furnishings)
- **Special Family Occasion**
- **Other Expenditures**

**Key observations:**

1. **Food share declined post‑pandemic** – From 42.6% in 2021 to 40.9% in 2023p. This is typical as incomes rise (Engel’s law). The drop was driven by a sharp reduction in home food consumption (‑3.2 pp) partly offset by a small rise in eating out (+1.5 pp), but still below 2018 levels.
2. **Housing and utilities peaked in 2021** – Likely due to more people staying home during lockdowns (higher electricity, water, etc.). The share fell back in 2023p but remained above 2018.
3. **Transport rebounded** – From 5.6% in 2021 to 6.8% in 2023p, reflecting mobility recovery.
4. **Education spending surged** – From only 1.5% in 2021 (when schools were closed) to 3.3% in 2023p – a huge increase as face‑to‑face classes resumed.
5. **Insurance and financial services appeared as a new category** in 2021 (likely due to better survey capture or increased uptake), then slightly declined.
6. **Personal care and miscellaneous** – Shrank significantly, possibly due to reduced spending on non‑essentials or reclassification.

### Regional & City Highlights

- **NCR** has a lower food share (37.7% in 2023p) and higher housing/utilities (28.8%) and transport (6.3%) compared to national averages. Eating out (9.9%) is nearly double the national figure (5.6%).
- **BARMM** has the highest food share (57.5% in 2023p) – over half of all spending goes to food – and the lowest housing share (14.6%). This reflects a much lower income level.
- **City of Makati** stands out for high housing share (41.6% in 2023p) – the highest among major cities – and very low food share (25.8%), indicating a wealthy, service‑oriented economy.
- **City of San Juan** saw a dramatic drop in food share (from 39.9% in 2021 to 31.4% in 2023p) and a rise in housing (from 26.4% to 31.0%).
- **Quezon City** eating‑out share jumped from 6.5% in 2021 to 13.5% in 2023p, the largest increase among NCR cities, suggesting a strong recovery in dining and social activities.

### Important Caveats

- **2023 data are preliminary** and may be revised.
- **Region XII and BARMM** figures for 2018/2021 are **not comparable** with 2023 due to the transfer of 63 barangays from North Cotabato to BARMM. Any change for those areas is partly or entirely due to boundary changes.
- The category **"Insurance and Financial Services"** appears only from 2021 onward – it may be a new disaggregation rather than a true zero in 2018.
- Percentages may not add to 100 due to rounding.

### In a Nutshell

This table answers: **What do Filipino families spend their money on?** It shows a clear shift away from food (especially home‑cooked) toward housing, transport, and education as the economy reopened. Regional disparities are stark – from food‑dependent BARMM to housing‑heavy Makati. The data also highlight the pandemic’s impact (surge in home food and utilities in 2021) and the subsequent recovery (rebound in eating out, transport, and education). It’s a vital tool for understanding consumption patterns and how they have evolved over time.
