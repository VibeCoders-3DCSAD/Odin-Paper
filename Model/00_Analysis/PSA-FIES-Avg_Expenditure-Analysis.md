### Overall National Picture – Spending Dipped Then Rose, But Less Than Income

- **2018** average family expenditure: **PHP 238,750**
- **2021** average: **PHP 228,800** – a **-4.2%** drop (pandemic-related)
- **2023p** average: **PHP 258,050** – a **+12.8%** rebound from 2021

Compare this with income: income fell only -2.0% from 2018 to 2021, then jumped +15.0% from 2021 to 2023p.  
So **spending fell more during the pandemic than income did**, and the recovery in spending (while strong) was slightly weaker than the income recovery.

### Spending by Decile – A Very Different Pattern from Income

Looking at the national row under **"All Income Groups"** and the decile columns:

| Decile | 2018 | 2021 | % Change 2018–2021 | 2023p | % Change 2021–2023p |
|--------|------|------|--------------------|-------|---------------------|
| 1st (poorest) | 109.35 | 103.89 | **-5.0%** | 127.86 | **+23.1%** |
| 5th (middle) | 188.32 | 184.15 | -2.2% | 216.19 | +17.4% |
| 10th (richest) | 544.99 | 511.75 | **-6.1%** | 545.66 | **+6.6%** |

**Key observations:**

1. **During the pandemic (2018–2021):**
   - The **richest decile** cut spending the most (-6.1%), likely due to reduced opportunities for luxury spending, travel, dining out, etc.
   - The **poorest decile** also cut spending significantly (-5.0%), which is notable because their income actually increased slightly (+2.15%) during that period. This suggests they may have saved more or received transfers that weren't spent immediately.

2. **During the recovery (2021–2023p):**
   - The **poorest decile** increased spending the most (+23.1%), almost identical to their income growth (+23.5%). This indicates they spent most of their additional income.
   - The **richest decile** increased spending only modestly (+6.6%), far below their income growth (+10.0%). Wealthier families appear to have saved a larger share of their income gains.

### National Average vs. NCR – Spending Patterns Differ

- **NCR** average spending fell sharply from 2018 (PHP 368,680) to 2021 (PHP 321,590), a **-12.8% drop** – much worse than the national average.  
- By 2023p, NCR spending recovered to PHP 385,050 (+19.7% from 2021), surpassing pre-pandemic levels.

Within NCR, some cities saw extreme swings:
  - **City of Makati**: spending dropped -14.0% from 2018 to 2021, then rebounded +22.3% by 2023p, reaching PHP 506,900.
  - **City of San Juan**: spending actually rose slightly from 2018 to 2021 (only -0.73%), then exploded +36.5% to PHP 648,780 in 2023p – one of the highest spending levels in the country.

### Regional Contrasts

- **CAR (Cordillera)**: spending actually **increased** from 2018 to 2021 (+5.5%), unlike most regions. But the 2021–2023p growth was very small (+2.05%), and the richest decile in CAR saw spending **fall -20.9%** in the recovery period – unusual.
- **BARMM**: spending jumped +16.9% from 2018 to 2021, then another +10.2% to 2023p, but note the footnote: 2023 includes 63 barangays from Cotabato, so the increase is partly due to boundary changes, not just real spending growth.
- **Region VIII (Eastern Visayas)**: spending grew steadily across both periods (+5.9% then +5.7%), one of the few regions with positive growth in both intervals.

### Spending vs. Income – A Quick Comparison (National)

| Year | Average Income | Average Expenditure | Difference (Savings) |
|------|---------------|--------------------|----------------------|
| 2018 | 313.45 | 238.75 | 74.70 |
| 2021 | 307.19 | 228.80 | 78.39 |
| 2023p | 353.23 | 258.05 | 95.18 |

- **Savings increased over time**, from about PHP 74,700 per family in 2018 to PHP 95,200 in 2023p.
- The **savings rate** (savings/income) rose from 23.8% in 2018 to 25.5% in 2021, then to **27.0% in 2023p** – Filipinos are saving a larger share of their income post-pandemic.

### Important Caveats (Same as Income Table)

- **2023 data are preliminary** and may be revised.
- **Region XII and BARMM** figures for 2018/2021 are **not comparable** with 2023 due to the transfer of 63 barangays from North Cotabato to BARMM. Any comparison across those years for those two regions is invalid.

### In Summary

This expenditure table reveals that:
- Spending fell more than income during the pandemic, especially for the rich.
- The poor increased spending dramatically during the recovery, almost matching their income gains.
- The rich saved more of their income rebound, leading to a higher national savings rate.
- Geographic disparities are wide – NCR and a few cities (San Juan, Makati, Taguig) saw spending bounce back strongly, while some provinces and CAR saw weaker or negative spending growth among higher deciles.
