### What the Spreadsheet Contains

The table is titled: *"Table 1. Average Annual Family Income, by Per Capita Income Decile Class and by Region, Province and HUCs: 2018, 2021 and 2023p"*

- **Geographic Breakdown:** Data is provided for the **Philippines** as a whole, then by **Region** (e.g., NCR, CAR, Region I), and then by **Province** and **Highly Urbanized Cities (HUCs)** within each region.
- **Per Capita Income Deciles:** The population is divided into ten equal groups (deciles) based on per capita income.
    - **1st Decile:** The poorest 10% of the population.
    - **10th Decile:** The richest 10% of the population.
    - **All Income Groups:** The average for the entire population of that area.
- **Data Points for Each Year:**
    - **2018 & 2021:** Final estimates.
    - **2023p:** Preliminary estimates (the 'p' indicates they may be revised).
- **Calculated Changes:**
    - Percent change from **2018 to 2021**.
    - Percent change from **2021 to 2023p**.
- **Units:** Income figures are in **thousands** of Philippine Pesos. For example, the national average family income for "All Income Groups" in 2023p was PHP 353,230.

### Key National-Level Observations

Looking at the row for **PHILIPPINES**:

1.  **National Average Income Recovered:** Average family income dipped slightly from 2018 (PHP 313,450) to 2021 (PHP 307,190), a -2.0% change. However, it saw a strong recovery by 2023p, reaching PHP 353,230, a +15.0% increase from 2021.
2.  **Pandemic Impact Varied by Income Group:** The 2018-2021 period (covering the height of the COVID-19 pandemic) shows a clear pattern:
    - The **poorest deciles (1st to 5th)** saw their average incomes *increase* slightly (e.g., 1st decile: +2.15%).
    - The **richest deciles (6th to 10th)** saw their average incomes *decrease* (e.g., 10th decile: -6.19%).
    - This suggests that safety nets or other factors may have cushioned lower-income families, while higher-income families (possibly in sectors more affected by lockdowns like business and services) experienced income loss.
3.  **Post-Pandemic Growth Benefited Lower Deciles More:** The recovery period (2021 to 2023p) shows very strong growth for the lower deciles.
    - The **1st decile** saw a massive **+23.5%** increase.
    - The **10th decile** saw a smaller but still significant **+10.0%** increase.
    - This indicates a period of catching up for lower-income families.

### Important Technical Notes from the Spreadsheet

There are two critical footnotes that affect data comparability:

- **For REGION XII (SOCCSKSARGEN):** The 2018 and 2021 estimates **include** 63 barangays from North Cotabato. In 2023, those barangays became part of BARMM. Therefore, the data for Region XII across the three years **are not directly comparable**.
- **For BARMM:** The 2018 and 2021 estimates **exclude** the 63 barangays from North Cotabato, which are included in 2023. Therefore, the data for BARMM across the three years **are also not directly comparable**.

### Regional Examples (NCR vs. CAR)

The spreadsheet allows for easy regional comparisons.

- **National Capital Region (NCR):**
    - Average income (2023p): PHP 513,520
    - Growth (2021-2023p): +22.9%
    - Shows strong recovery and the highest absolute income among regions.
- **Cordillera Administrative Region (CAR):**
    - Average income (2023p): PHP 359,240
    - Growth (2021-2023p): Only +2.5%, much slower than the national average.
    - Interestingly, CAR's 10th decile saw a sharp **-21.9%** decline from 2021 to 2023p, a very different trend from the national picture.

### In a Nutshell

This spreadsheet is a primary source for understanding income inequality, economic recovery patterns, and the impact of major events (like the pandemic) on different segments of the Philippine population, broken down geographically. The inclusion of deciles is the most powerful feature, as it reveals trends that are completely hidden when looking only at the "average."

**Caveat:** Always remember the **2023 data is preliminary** and the boundary changes for **Region XII and BARMM** make comparisons over time for those specific areas invalid.
