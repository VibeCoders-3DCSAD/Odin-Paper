### What Makes This Table Different?

- **Table 1** showed *average* income per family (in thousands).  
- **Table 3** shows *total* income aggregated across all families in an area (in **millions**).  
- This means Table 3 reflects both **income levels** and **population size** – a region with many families can have high total income even if average income is modest.

### Key National‑Level Observations

| Year | Total Income (all groups) | Poorest 10% (1st decile) | Richest 10% (10th decile) |
|------|---------------------------|---------------------------|----------------------------|
| 2018 | 7,757,013 million | 280,591 million | 2,147,951 million |
| 2021 | 8,109,045 million | 306,263 million | 2,149,110 million |
| 2023p | 9,706,613 million | 393,686 million | 2,460,818 million |

- **Total national family income grew by +4.5%** from 2018 to 2021 (pandemic period) – note that average income fell slightly during that time, but total income rose because the number of families increased.
- **From 2021 to 2023p, total income jumped +19.7%** – much faster than the +15.0% growth in average income, again reflecting population growth.
- The **poorest decile** saw its total income surge +28.6% from 2021 to 2023p, faster than any other decile – consistent with the average income data showing strong catch‑up growth for low‑income families.
- The **richest decile** grew more slowly (+14.5%), similar to the pattern in average income.

### Regional Contrasts in Total Income

- **NCR** total income dipped slightly from 2018 to 2021 (-3.9%), then rebounded +27.1% by 2023p. NCR’s total income in 2023p was 1.87 trillion PHP, the largest of any region.
- **CALABARZON** saw total income grow steadily: +2.0% (2018–2021) then +24.6% (2021–2023p), reaching 1.84 trillion PHP in 2023p – almost catching up to NCR.
- **BARMM** total income grew +19.3% from 2018 to 2021 and another +21.4% to 2023p, but note the boundary change footnote (63 barangays added in 2023). The increase is partly due to geographic expansion.
- **Bicol Region (Region V)** total income grew +12.1% then +17.1%, showing robust recovery.

### Interesting City‑Level Examples

- **City of San Juan** (NCR): total income of the richest decile grew from 5.4 billion in 2018 to 6.9 billion in 2023p (+95.7% from 2021!), indicating a massive concentration of high‑income families.
- **Quezon City**: total income of the top decile actually fell between 2018 and 2021 (-6.3%), then recovered only +2.2% by 2023p – very sluggish compared to other NCR cities.
- **City of Makati**: total income of the richest decile grew +77.5% from 2021 to 2023p, reaching 57.7 billion PHP – the highest for any single city decile.

### Relationship Between Tables 1 and 3

You can roughly estimate the number of families in an area by dividing total income (Table 3) by average income (Table 1). For example, nationally in 2023p:
- Total income = 9,706,613 million PHP
- Average income = 353,230 PHP
- Implied number of families ≈ 27.5 million, which is plausible.

This table is useful for understanding **income distribution in aggregate terms** – how many pesos go to each decile group across the country, and how that has changed over time. Policymakers use this to see where the bulk of income gains are occurring (e.g., the bottom decile gained 28.6% in total income from 2021–2023p, a very large real increase).

### Important Caveats (Same as Previous Tables)

- 2023 data are **preliminary**.
- **Region XII and BARMM** comparisons across years are invalid due to the 63‑barangay transfer. Any percent changes for those areas should be ignored unless you adjust for the boundary change.
- Totals may not sum perfectly due to rounding.

### In a Nutshell

This spreadsheet tells you the **total income pie** for the Philippines and each sub‑area, sliced by income decile. It shows that despite the pandemic, total national income grew (because of more families), and the recovery saw the poorest decile’s total income grow the fastest in percentage terms – though the richest decile still holds the largest absolute share. Regional disparities remain stark, with NCR and CALABARZON dominating total income.
